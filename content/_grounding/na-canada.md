# Grounding pack — Canada (North America, NON-EU) C&I power market

Canada is **outside the EU** — none of the EU frame (ETS, CBAM, RED III, Modernisation Fund,
state-aid CEEAG) applies. Figures are **illustrative orders of magnitude in CAD** for teaching;
have learners verify live figures (AESO, IESO, CER, ECCC, CRA) before quoting a client.
Mark live items **"check current"**.

## The one thing to get right: electricity is PROVINCIAL
- **There is no single national power market in Canada.** Under the Constitution, generation,
  transmission within a province, and retail electricity are **provincial jurisdiction**. Each
  province runs its own market and regulator. This is the central teaching point.
- **Federal role is narrow.** The **CER (Canada Energy Regulator)** regulates *interprovincial and
  international* power lines and pipelines, plus designated international electricity. Ottawa also
  sets economy-wide climate rules (carbon pricing, the Clean Electricity Regulations) — but it does
  **not** run a wholesale electricity market.
- **Clean Electricity Regulations (CER regs, SOR/2024-263)** — finalised Dec 2024, in force Jan
  2025; emission limits on fossil generation **bite from 2035**, targeting a net-zero grid. **Alberta
  and Saskatchewan are challenging them on jurisdictional grounds.** (Check current.)

## Province map (the table to teach from)
| Province | Operator | Market type |
|---|---|---|
| **Alberta** | **AESO** (Alberta Electric System Operator) | **Deregulated, energy-only** wholesale market — competitive |
| **Ontario** | **IESO** (Independent Electricity System Operator) | Hybrid: **capacity auction + long-term procurements (RFPs)** |
| **Quebec** | **Hydro-Québec** | Crown utility, **low-cost hydro**, vertically integrated |
| **British Columbia** | **BC Hydro** | Crown utility, **low-cost hydro** |
| **Manitoba** | **Manitoba Hydro** | Crown utility, **low-cost hydro** |

## Alberta — Canada's corporate-PPA hub (real)
- **AESO runs a deregulated, energy-only wholesale market** — generators are paid only for energy
  delivered (no capacity payment), so the pool price is **volatile**. This price signal is exactly
  what lets a **corporate PPA** lock a fixed price against it.
- **~95% of new renewable build in Canada (through ~2025) has been in Alberta** (Rystad). Corporate
  offtakers signing Alberta PPAs include **Amazon, RBC, IKEA, TELUS** and heavy emitters (a PPA also
  helps them manage industrial carbon costs — see TIER below). Amazon's Buffalo Plains (~415 MW
  wind) was among Canada's largest onshore wind farms at announcement. (Check current.)
- **Restructured Energy Market (REM)** — Alberta's market redesign. Ministerial Order **035/2026**
  adopted **10 March 2026**; **phased implementation beginning 2027**. It *enhances* (does not
  abandon) the energy-only design — adding **scarcity pricing, Locational Marginal Pricing (LMP),
  new reserve/reliability products**. Widely flagged as a source of **investment uncertainty**
  during transition. **Check current — this is live.**

## Ontario — procurement-led (real)
- **IESO** runs a **capacity auction** plus **long-term competitive procurements**: **LT1** (the
  largest battery-storage procurement in Canadian history) and the ongoing **LT2 RFP** (energy +
  capacity streams, heavy on **storage/BESS**, 20-year contracts; thousands of MW). Less of a pure
  merchant corporate-PPA market than Alberta — supply is mostly contracted *through the operator*.
  (Check current.)

## Hydro provinces — Quebec / BC / Manitoba (real)
- **Hydro-Québec, BC Hydro, Manitoba Hydro** are **Crown (government-owned) utilities** running on
  abundant, cheap **hydropower**. Regulated retail rates are **already low**, so the merchant
  corporate-PPA opportunity is **smaller** here — the pitch is decarbonised growth/new load, not
  beating a high tariff. Hydro is **dominant nationally** (~60% of Canadian generation).

## Where C&I PPAs actually happen
- **Mostly Alberta**, because it's the only large province with a **deregulated, energy-only**
  market where a corporate buyer can contract directly with a developer against a volatile pool
  price. Ontario adds operator-run procurements; hydro provinces are largely Crown-supplied.

## Carbon — federal benchmark + industrial pricing (GET THIS RIGHT)
- **The consumer federal "fuel charge" was set to $0 effective 1 April 2025** — the retail/consumer
  carbon price is effectively **ended**. This is a common **stale** point; do not say consumers
  still pay it. (Check current.)
- **Industrial carbon pricing REMAINS.** The federal benchmark + the **OBPS (Output-Based Pricing
  System)** still price large-emitter CO₂. Provinces can run their own equivalent — Alberta's is
  **TIER (Technology Innovation and Emissions Reduction)**. A renewable PPA helps an industrial
  buyer cut covered emissions / compliance cost. (Check current — benchmark stringency reviewed.)

## Funding (federal — NOT EU grants)
- **Clean Technology ITC — ~30% refundable** investment tax credit for wind, solar, storage,
  low-carbon heat (qualifying expenditures ~28 Mar 2023 → 31 Dec 2034).
- **Clean Electricity ITC — ~15% refundable** for low-emitting generation, storage and
  inter-provincial transmission; open to taxable corps **and Crown corporations**. Became law
  **26 March 2026** (Bill C-15). A domestic-content requirement is under consultation. (Check current.)
- **Canada Growth Fund (CGF)** — ~$15bn; de-risks projects, notably via **carbon contracts for
  difference** (offtake-style carbon-price certainty).
- **Canada Infrastructure Bank (CIB)** — ~$35bn federal Crown bank; low-cost debt/equity for clean
  power, storage and transmission.

## Resource & numbers (illustrative, CAD)
- **Wind**: strong in **Alberta and the prairies** (Saskatchewan, Manitoba). Solar growing fast in
  **Alberta** (good southern-prairie irradiance). **Hydro dominant nationally.**
- Illustrative C&I figures (teach as examples, real prices in **CAD**, check current): industrial
  all-in tariff and PPA prices vary widely by province; Alberta pool price is volatile, hydro
  provinces are low and stable.

## Quick contrast vs the EU spine
- No ETS / CBAM / RED III / Modernisation Fund. Replace "EU frame → national rule" with
  "**federal climate frame → provincial market**." The recurring lesson: **always ask which
  province** — the answer changes the operator, the market type and whether a PPA even makes sense.
