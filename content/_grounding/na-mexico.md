# Grounding pack — North America: Mexico C&I energy market

Mexico is **not in the EU**. Do **not** apply EU frameworks (RED III, EU ETS, Modernisation
Fund, CEEAG/GBER, CfD auctions) to it — it has its own regulator, market and laws, and it has
just been through a **major 2024–25 constitutional + statutory reform** that is still settling.
All figures are **illustrative orders of magnitude** for teaching; tell learners to verify live
figures (CENACE, CFE/DOF, CNE, SEMARNAT) before quoting them, and treat every reform detail as
**check current** — the secondary regulations and MEM rules were still being updated through 2025–26.

## Institutions (current, post-reform — verify)
- **CNE — Comisión Nacional de Energía.** The energy regulator. Created by the 2025 reform; it
  **absorbed the former CRE (Comisión Reguladora de Energía) and CNH (hydrocarbons)** and is now a
  **decentralised body attached to SENER** (the energy ministry). Most pre-2025 rules still refer to
  "CRE" — in the room, name **CRE's successor, the CNE**. *(Check current.)*
- **SENER — Secretaría de Energía.** Sets energy policy and, e.g., the minimum-demand threshold for
  qualified users. The reform concentrated planning power here and in CFE.
- **CENACE — Centro Nacional de Control de Energía.** The independent **system operator and market
  operator**. Runs the national grid (SEN), administers the wholesale market (MEM) and **sets hourly
  nodal prices (PML)**. Post-reform it must dispatch to guarantee "predominantly state control" while
  still dispatching cheaper energy first. *(Check current.)*
- **CFE — Comisión Federal de Electricidad.** The state utility. The Oct 2024 constitutional reform
  re-classified it from a "productive state enterprise" (*empresa productiva*) back to a **state
  public enterprise (*empresa pública del Estado*)** — a deliberate move away from treating it as a
  market competitor. CFE holds a **monopoly on basic supply** and the dominant role in commercialisation.
- **MEM — Mercado Eléctrico Mayorista.** The wholesale market (energy, capacity, CELs, ancillary
  services), launched 2016 under the old LIE, run by CENACE.
- **DOF — Diario Oficial de la Federación.** Where laws and **CFE regulated tariffs** are published.

| Pillar | Mexico |
|---|---|
| Regulator | **CNE** — Comisión Nacional de Energía (absorbed the former **CRE**) |
| System & market operator | **CENACE** — runs the grid (SEN) + the MEM; sets nodal **PML** |
| Wholesale market | **MEM** — Mercado Eléctrico Mayorista (energy, capacity, CELs, ancillaries) |
| State utility | **CFE** — basic-supply monopoly; now an *empresa pública del Estado* |

## The defining feature — the 2024–25 reform (politically live; check current)
- **Constitutional reform, Oct/Nov 2024.** Amended articles **25, 27 and 28** to re-establish CFE as a
  **state public enterprise** and to entrench state primacy in electricity. Transmission, distribution
  and grid planning/control are **exclusive State functions**.
- **CFE ≥54% generation floor.** The State (via CFE) must supply **at least 54% of the average annual
  energy injected into the grid**; private generation is allowed up to the remaining share.
- **Ley del Sector Eléctrico (LSE / LESE), 18 March 2025 (in force 19 March 2025).** Replaced the 2014
  **LIE (Ley de la Industria Eléctrica)**. Issued alongside the **Ley de la CNE** and other secondary
  laws. The **Reglamento (RLSE)** and updated **MEM Rules** followed in 2025 and were still being
  finalised into 2026. *(Check current.)*
- **What it means for private PPAs:** private generation is **not banned** — it is **subordinated** to
  CFE's 54% and channelled into specific schemes (qualified supply, sale-to-CFE production contracts,
  mixed CFE-majority projects, self-consumption, distributed generation). Permitting paused while the
  new secondary rules took effect. The political tone is state-led, so **regulatory certainty is the
  live risk** in every conversation.

## C&I routes to buy clean power (post-reform)
- **Qualified supply (suministro calificado).** Large users above a SENER-set **minimum-demand
  threshold** register as *usuarios calificados* and buy in the MEM via a qualified supplier — preserved
  under the LSE on broadly similar terms. The main competitive route for a big factory.
- **PPAs.** CFE may sign PPAs with any generator (incl. competitive tenders). Private options include
  **long-term production contracts for exclusive sale to CFE** and **mixed-investment projects** where
  **CFE holds ≥54%**. Corporate PPAs with private generators remain possible but sit under the new frame.
- **Distributed generation.** Plants **under 0.7 MW** (raised from **0.5 MW** under the old regime)
  need **no generation permit** — on-site rooftop solar for a single site is the simplest clean route
  and avoids most market friction.
- **Self-consumption (autoconsumo).** The LSE replaced old "isolated supply"; projects **≥0.7 MW** for
  the owner's own needs. **Legacy *autoabasto* (self-supply) contracts** signed under the LIE are
  grandfathered but have been politically targeted — treat legacy structures as **check current**.
- **CELs — Certificados de Energía Limpia.** Tradable clean-energy certificates (1 CEL ≈ 1 MWh clean).
  Load-serving entities have annual CEL obligations. The reform **expanded eligibility to existing
  renewable plants**, not only new ones — which may soften CEL prices. *(Check current.)*

## Resource & demand
- **Excellent solar/wind, especially the north** (Sonora, Chihuahua, Baja California, Coahuila,
  plus the Tehuantepec isthmus for wind). Specific solar yield roughly **1,800–2,100 kWh/kWp/yr** —
  far above Central Europe's ~950–1,100 and comparable to or above Iberia.
- **Nearshoring** is the demand engine: manufacturers relocating supply chains to Mexico (Bajío,
  Monterrey, the border) are driving industrial power demand and straining the grid — clean, firm,
  competitively-priced supply is a real commercial need.

## Carbon
- **National ETS (Sistema de Comercio de Emisiones, SCEE).** Run by **SEMARNAT**. Pilot 2020–21,
  transitional phase after, with the **operational/compliance phase expected late 2025 / 2026**.
  Covers large industrial + power-sector emitters. **Nascent — check current.**
- **Carbon taxes** also exist: a **federal carbon tax** on fossil fuels plus several **state-level
  carbon taxes**.
- **CBAM exposure.** Mexican exporters of carbon-intensive goods to the **EU** face the EU's Carbon
  Border Adjustment Mechanism from 2026 — so clean power is a competitiveness lever for exporters,
  the same logic as in Europe but driven from the buyer side.

## "Funding" — a different system from the EU (be explicit)
- **No EU-style grants.** There is **no Modernisation Fund, no RRF/PERTE, no CEEAG/GBER** in Mexico.
  Do not transplant that vocabulary.
- **Accelerated tax depreciation.** Mexico's income-tax law (**LISR, Art. 34**) allows a very high /
  **immediate (up to ~100%) deduction** for investment in **machinery and equipment for generating
  power from renewable sources** (and efficient cogeneration), provided the asset runs for a minimum
  period. This is the headline fiscal incentive — a tax-timing benefit, not a cash grant. *(Check current.)*
- **Nearshoring incentives.** Federal decrees (2023–24) and the current **Plan México** offer
  **accelerated depreciation and extra training deductions** for relocating manufacturers in priority
  sectors — adjacent to, not specific to, renewables. *(Check current.)*

## Currency & numbers
- Figures in this market are in **MXN** (and frequently **USD** for large PPAs and project finance),
  **not euros**. The Academy calculator is denominated in €/MWh for cross-market consistency — when
  using it on a Mexican site, treat the €/MWh inputs as a **stand-in for the MXN/USD reality** and
  flag that to the client. CFE regulated tariffs are published monthly via **CFE/DOF**; MEM **PML** is
  published by **CENACE** in MXN/MWh. Everything is **illustrative — check current.**
