How to apply: calls, eligibility, co-financing

Funding is not a faucet you turn on; it is a door that opens for a while and then shuts. To walk through it you have to hit an open call, pass eligibility, fund your share of the cost, and stay inside the state-aid limits. Miss any one and the application fails — so know the rules before you promise a client anything.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain how a call for proposals works, run a project through the core eligibility tests, and tell a client honestly what co-financing and state-aid limits mean for how much grant they can actually receive.

How a call for proposals works

Public money is not always available. It is released in calls for proposals — defined windows during which you can submit an application against a published set of rules. A call has an opening date, a deadline, a budget, and a precise description of what it will and will not fund. Miss the deadline and you wait for the next call, which may be months away or have different terms.

Because each call's exact text governs everything — eligible costs, intensities, scoring — you never rely on memory. The discipline is simple: find the live call, read the call text, build the application to that text. Any budget or deadline I name in training is illustrative; the real ones live in the current call.

Key term · Not-started-works rule

Most grant programmes require that you have not started works on the project before you apply (or before approval). Ordering equipment or breaking ground too early can disqualify an otherwise eligible project — the grant is meant to enable investment that would not otherwise happen, so it cannot reward something already underway. Confirm the exact cut-off in the current call.

Eligibility basics

Eligibility is the set of pass/fail gates that decide whether your application is even considered. They cluster into a few recurring tests:

  • Location — the project must sit in an eligible territory. The Modernisation Fund covers specific member states; some Czech programmes weight or restrict by region.
  • Technology — the call names what it funds (e.g. new solar, storage, efficiency). A technology outside the call's scope is simply ineligible.
  • Applicant type — who you are matters. Some calls are for SMEs, some for large enterprises, some for municipalities. SME (small & medium-sized enterprise) status can change both eligibility and the aid rate.
  • Readiness — many calls expect a defined maturity: permits in hand or in progress, a grid-connection position, a credible budget and timeline.
  • Not-started-works — as above, you generally must apply before commencing works.

Eligibility is binary, and it is checked first. A brilliant project that fails one gate gets no further than a project that fails all five.

Co-financing and state-aid limits

Even when you are eligible, the grant does not pay for everything. Two limits cap it.

First, co-financing: a grant funds only a percentage of the eligible capital cost, and you must bring the rest from equity or debt. The exact share depends on the call. Second, state-aid rules: the EU limits how much public subsidy any project may receive, through frameworks like the GBER (General Block Exemption Regulation) and regional aid maps that set a maximum aid intensity by region and company size. The aim is that a grant only ever closes a viability gap — it is not allowed to make a project "too profitable".

For a salesperson this is a feature, not a flaw: it forces the project to stand on its own economics, with the grant as genuine upside rather than the whole reason to build.

Key term · Aid intensity (GBER / regional aid map)

Aid intensity is the maximum share of eligible cost that public support may cover, set by EU state-aid frameworks — chiefly the GBER and regional aid maps. It varies by region and by company size (SMEs often get a higher ceiling). It is why no grant can fund 100%: the rules cap the help so it only closes a viability gap. Always confirm the applicable intensity for the specific project and call.

An eligibility checklist

Run a prospective project through this before you spend real effort on an application. Every "yes" needs evidence; any "no" or "unsure" is a flag to resolve. The thresholds themselves live in the current call — this is the structure of the questions, not the answers.

The two that catch newcomers most often are not-started-works (acting too early) and state-aid intensity (asking for more grant than the rules allow). Verify every threshold against the current call text.

At EU level

The state-aid ceiling is not a Czech rule — it is EU-wide. The CEEAG (Climate, Energy & Environmental Aid Guidelines, 2022) and the GBER set the aid-intensity limits that every national grant scheme must fit inside. So the same frame governs a grant in Czechia, Germany or Austria: Brussels caps how much subsidy a project may take, and the regional aid map and company size set the exact percentage.

Switzerland sits outside EU state-aid law — its national support (e.g. the PV Einmalvergütung) is set under Swiss rules, not CEEAG/GBER. So when you cross into CH, the eligibility logic still applies but the legal ceiling does not.

Across markets: one EU state-aid frame (CEEAG + GBER) caps grants across all member states; CH is governed by its own law. Confirm the applicable intensity per project and call ("check current").

In the room

How to set honest expectations with a client about what a grant will and won't do:

Sales script

A grant can cover part of the eligible cost — not the whole system — and only while a call is open and the project is eligible. Two things matter most: we apply before we start any works, and we keep the request inside the state-aid limit for your region and company size. Get those right and the grant improves your payback. So before we order anything, let's confirm the live call and lock the eligibility.

Objections you will hear

Can we just order the panels now and apply for the grant later?
Rebuttal

That is the classic trap. Most programmes have a not-started-works rule — start too early and you can disqualify the whole project. The right order is: confirm eligibility and the open call first, get the green light, then commit to works. A few weeks of sequencing protects the grant.

Why can't the grant just cover the whole cost?
Rebuttal

EU state-aid rules cap how much public support a project may receive — the aid intensity, set by the GBER and regional maps. A grant is sized to close a viability gap, not to fund everything. You still bring equity or debt for the rest, and that is by design: it keeps the project genuinely viable on its own.

You told me a figure last month — is that still what we'll get?
Rebuttal

I would not bank on a remembered figure. Call budgets, deadlines and eligible costs change between calls, so the only number I trust is the one in the live call text at the moment we apply. Let's pull the current call and base the application on that, not on last month's terms.

Knowledge check
What is the "not-started-works" rule?

Grants enable investment that would not otherwise happen, so they cannot reward works already underway. Confirm the cut-off in the call.

What does "aid intensity" under GBER / regional aid maps control?

Aid intensity caps subsidy so a grant only closes a viability gap — never funds 100%.

A client asks you to guarantee the grant amount from last quarter's call. Best response?

Always work from the current call — windows, budgets and eligibility move, so old figures can mislead.
