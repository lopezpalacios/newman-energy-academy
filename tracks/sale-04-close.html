Closing & term-sheet basics

A "yes" in the room is not a deal — it is permission to start one. Closing means turning that yes into a signed term sheet with six numbers on it, then knowing precisely what happens next. This is where the sale becomes a project.

Learning objective

By the end you can ask for the close confidently, walk a prospect through a basic PPA / EaaS term sheet — price, term, volume, indexation, exclusivity, conditions precedent — and tell them exactly what happens between "yes" and switch-on.

How to actually close

Closing intimidates newcomers because they imagine a hard sell. In C&I energy it is the opposite: a calm, assumptive move to the next concrete commitment. You've shown the saving, handled the objections — now you ask for the decision and name the next step in the same breath.

The cleanest close is the summary-and-step: restate the value in one sentence, then propose signing a term sheet. "So we're agreed this saves you roughly a fifth on power with no capex — shall we put the headline terms on one page so legal can start?" If they hesitate, find the one remaining doubt with a direct question ("What's the one thing still making you pause?") and answer only that. Don't re-pitch the whole deal; close the gap.

What you're closing to is not the 80-page contract. It is the term sheet — a short, mostly non-binding summary of the key commercial terms that lets both sides agree the shape of the deal before lawyers spend money on the full PPA.

Key term · Term sheet

A one-to-two-page summary of a deal's key commercial terms — price, term, volume, indexation, exclusivity, conditions precedent — agreed before the full contract is drafted. Mostly non-binding, except usually the exclusivity and confidentiality clauses. It exists to confirm both sides want the same deal before anyone pays for legal drafting. Signing one is the real "close".

Reading the term sheet

Six terms carry almost all the meaning. A businessperson can hold the whole deal in their head with these. Learn what each one is and what's at stake in it, so you can explain any line a prospect points at.

Price is what they pay per MWh — the saving versus their tariff. Term is how long, typically 10–15 years. Volume is how much energy, and crucially how the obligation is shaped: do they take all you generate, or up to a cap? Indexation is how the price moves over time — flat, or rising with inflation (CPI). Exclusivity protects your development spend during the run-up. And conditions precedent are the boxes that must be ticked before the deal goes live.

The six terms — a basic PPA / EaaS term sheet

An illustrative on-site rooftop deal. Real numbers; the structure is what to memorise.

Basic term sheet — 2 MWp rooftop PPA, Czechia (illustrative)
TermExampleWhat it controls
Price€78 / MWhBelow their ~€140 grid tariff — the saving. The headline number.
Term15 yearsLong enough to fund the build; matches asset life.
Volume~2,100 MWh/yr"As-generated", self-consumed on site; surplus exported or curtailed.
IndexationCPI, capped 3%Price drifts up with inflation but never above the cap — predictability for both sides.
Exclusivity90 daysCustomer won't shop the roof to rivals while you survey & finance.
Conditions precedentSee belowSurvey, grid, board sign-off — all must clear before go-live.
Indicative saving≈ €130k/yr(€140 − €78) × 2,100 MWh — the reason they signed.

Figures are illustrative for teaching, not an offer. Note that price and indexation together define the real saving over 15 years — a low headline price with aggressive indexation can be worse than a slightly higher flat one.

Conditions precedent — the deal's safety catches

Conditions precedent (often "CPs") are the things that must be true before the contract becomes binding and the build begins. They protect both sides: the customer isn't locked in if the project can't actually be delivered, and you're not building on a roof that fails survey. Typical CPs on a rooftop PPA:

  • Structural survey passed — the roof carries the load with margin.
  • Grid connection / metering confirmed — the distribution operator permits the connection.
  • Permitting in hand — any building or planning consent secured.
  • Customer board / credit approval — the offtaker's internal sign-off and credit check clear.
  • Financing confirmed — the developer's funding for the asset is committed.

Walk a nervous prospect through the CPs deliberately: they are the proof that nobody is trapped in a deal that can't be built. Each unticked box is an exit, not a risk.

What happens after the yes

The prospect signs the term sheet — now narrate the path so the momentum doesn't stall. A clean handoff sequence: (1) countersign and start the exclusivity clock; (2) book the structural and electrical site survey; (3) submit the grid connection enquiry; (4) the customer runs internal board/credit approval in parallel; (5) lawyers draft the full PPA from the agreed term sheet; (6) CPs clear, contract signs, build is scheduled. Give them names and dates for the first two steps before you leave the room — a yes with no calendar entry cools fast.

In the room

The summary-and-step close, then the immediate next action — said in one continuous move:

Sales script

So we're agreed: around a hundred and thirty thousand a year saved, no capex, and a fifteen-year price that sits below your grid tariff and then stops moving. The next step isn't the big contract — it's a one-page term sheet with those six numbers on it, enough to start the lawyers and lock in your survey slot. I'll have it with you tomorrow morning; sign it this week and I'll book the structural survey for the same week. Shall we do that?

Objections you will hear

I'm not signing anything until I've seen the full contract.
Rebuttal

And you won't have to — the term sheet isn't the contract. It's a one-page summary of the six commercial terms, mostly non-binding, that just confirms we both want the same deal before anyone pays lawyers to draft eighty pages. The only parts that bind are confidentiality and a short exclusivity window. It works in your favour: it pins your price and your saving down in writing before the legal clock — and the legal bill — ever starts.

Why do you need exclusivity? I like to keep my options open.
Rebuttal

Fair — and it's a narrow ask. We're about to spend real money surveying your roof and lining up financing on the strength of this deal, before you've paid a cent. Ninety days of exclusivity simply means no rival is quoting the same roof while we carry that cost — it's time-boxed, it covers this site only, and the moment the window closes you're free again. It's what lets us put our money in first, before you've risked any of yours.

What if something in the conditions precedent doesn't clear?
Rebuttal

Then the deal simply doesn't go live, and nobody is trapped — that's exactly what those conditions are for. If the roof fails its survey, the grid won't permit the connection, or your board declines, the term sheet falls away with no penalty to you. They're not hurdles I'm hiding; they're the safety catches that guarantee you only ever sign a deal that can actually be built and delivered.
