Learning objective

By the end you can explain the development premium — value created by taking risk through the gates — and lay out a simple cost-buildup versus ready-to-build value comparison that shows where the margin comes from.

The development premium

Across this track the same idea has returned again and again: a project's value climbs as its risk falls. The development premium is the name for the reward that climb produces — the difference between the money spent getting a project to a milestone and the price the market will pay for it at that milestone.

It is not a trick or a markup. It is payment for risk transfer. The developer absorbs the chance that the land falls through, the grid says no, the permit is refused or the offtake collapses. A buyer who steps in at ready-to-build pays a premium precisely so they don't have to carry those risks. The premium is the market price of the certainty you created.

This premium is realised at one of two exits: selling the project ready-to-build (RTB) before construction, or holding through to the commercial operation date (COD) and selling the operating asset. Either way, the value above cost is the developer's margin.

Key term · Development premium

The value created by moving a project from idea toward an operating asset — the gap between the cost of clearing the development gates and the price a buyer will pay for the de-risked result. It is compensation for absorbing land, grid, permitting, offtake and funding risk, not a simple cost-plus margin.

Cost vs value — two different numbers

The single most important habit in this business is to keep cost and value in separate columns in your head. They are not the same thing and they do not move together.

Development cost is what you actually spend to clear the gates — option fees on the land, grid-study and connection charges, planning and EIA fees, legal and engineering work, and the team's time. For a mid-size project this is modest: a small fraction of the eventual build cost.

Ready-to-build value is what a buyer will pay for the fully-consented project, with every gate cleared. It reflects the future profitability of the finished plant, discounted for the risk that remains — which, at RTB, is small. Because the risk is small, the value is high.

The premium lives in the gap. You spend a little to remove a lot of risk; the market rewards the risk removed, not the euros spent. A developer who confuses the two will either undersell their work or overspend chasing it.

Now the value side. A common rule of thumb prices a ready-to-build solar project at roughly €60–100 per kWp of capacity. For 5 MWp that is ≈ €300,000–500,000 of premium over cost — meaning an RTB sale around €650,000–850,000 against the ~€350,000 spent. The developer roughly doubles their money, and never poured a single foundation.

The whole margin is the gap between the two columns. It exists because the buyer pays for the risk you removed, not the cash you spent. Hold the project further — through construction to COD — and a second premium stacks on top, which is what the exit track quantifies.

Setting up the exit

There are, then, two premiums a developer can capture, and you should be able to name both:

  • The development premium — earned by clearing the gates to ready-to-build. Captured by selling RTB, or rolled forward if you build.
  • The exit / operating premium — earned by carrying the project through construction to COD and selling the operating asset to a fund at a lower required yield. This is yield compression, and it is the entire subject of the next track.

A developer chooses how far up this ladder to climb based on their appetite for risk and capital. Sell at RTB for a fast, smaller, lower-risk gain; hold to COD for a larger one that ties up money and adds construction risk. In the room

How you explain to an investor why development — not generation — is where you make money:

Sales script

People assume we make our money selling electricity. We don't — that's a thin trickle spread over decades. We make it by taking a project from a blank field to fully permitted and contracted. That costs us maybe three-fifty thousand in studies, fees and legal work, and the finished, de-risked project is worth roughly double that to a buyer who'd rather not carry the risks we just retired. We're not paid for the paperwork — we're paid for the certainty we manufacture. And if we hold it through construction, a second premium stacks on top when a fund buys the operating asset.

Objections you will hear

Doubling your money on paperwork — isn't that just markup?
Rebuttal

It's payment for risk transfer, not markup. For two years we carried the chance that the grid, the permit or the offtake fell through — any one of which could take the project to zero. The buyer pays a premium precisely so they never have to shoulder those risks themselves. They're not buying paperwork; they're buying certainty, and certainty has a market price.

If the premium is so good, why ever sell at ready-to-build instead of holding?
Rebuttal

Because selling at RTB recycles our capital fast into the next three projects, with no construction risk carried. Holding to operation earns the second premium but ties up money and adds risk. It's a deliberate portfolio choice — we sell some early to fund the pipeline and hold others for the bigger gain.

What if ready-to-build prices fall and the premium disappears?
Rebuttal

Then the cost of clearing the gates falls with them, and we develop fewer, better-sited projects. The premium tracks how scarce de-risked projects are — and with grid capacity tight and renewable demand mandated by EU policy, well-connected, fully-permitted projects stay genuinely scarce. We underwrite every project on a deliberately conservative exit, so a softer market is downside we've already priced in.

Knowledge check
What is the development premium fundamentally a payment for?

It's the market price of certainty. The buyer pays for the risk removed, not the cash spent — that's why it isn't cost-plus.

In the worked example, why is the ready-to-build value so much higher than the development cost?

Cost and value are different numbers. You spend a little to remove a lot of risk; value reflects the risk removed.

Which two premiums can a developer capture?

Sell at RTB for the development premium; hold to COD and sell to a fund for the second, yield-compression premium — the subject of the next track.
