Selling a C&I deal in Austria

Austria is a small, hydro-rich market that trades in Germany's shadow — but it has its own regulator, its own grid, its own renewables law, and a quietly booming energy-community movement. Here is everything you need to walk into an Austrian factory and pitch a PPA.

Learning objective

By the end you can walk an Austrian C&I prospect through a renewable PPA — naming the right regulator (E-Control), grid operator (APG) and support scheme (EAG), and deciding when an energy community is a better fit than a corporate PPA.

Austria at a glance

Austria's power system is built on water. Hydropower — much of it run-of-river on the Danube and Alpine storage — supplies the lion's share of generation, which makes the country one of Europe's greenest grids by default. That shapes every conversation you will have here.

The market is also small and tightly coupled to Germany. Until 2018 Germany and Austria formed a single joint bidding zone — one price for both. That split in October 2018, so Austria now prices separately, but its dynamics still track the German market closely.

Two venues matter for trading: EXAA (the Vienna-based Energy Exchange Austria) and EPEX SPOT, the pan-European day-ahead/intraday exchange Austria couples into.

The EAG — Austria's renewables law

The support scheme you will quote is the EAG (Erneuerbaren-Ausbau-Gesetz, the "Renewables Expansion Act", 2021). It is Austria's equivalent of Germany's EEG, and it works two ways:

  • Market premium (Marktprämie) — a top-up paid on top of the wholesale price the generator earns selling into the market, awarded through competitive tenders. It closes the gap between the market price and what a project needs to be viable.
  • Investment grants (Investitionszuschüsse) — one-off capital subsidies for building the plant, used especially for smaller PV, wind and storage.
Key term · Market premium

A sliding or fixed top-up added to the wholesale price a renewable generator already earns. Unlike an old-style feed-in tariff (which replaces the market price), a premium keeps the asset selling into the market and only fills the shortfall — so the project stays exposed to, and rewarded by, real price signals.

The EAG carries a national headline target: 100% renewable electricity on a national-balance basis by 2030 — meaning Austria aims to generate, across the year, as much renewable power as it consumes. Hitting it needs roughly +27 TWh of new generation (PV- and wind-led), on the way to climate neutrality by 2040. Those targets are the demand driver behind every project you will pitch. Energy communities — Austria's quiet boom

Austria has run faster than almost anyone on energy communities (Erneuerbare-Energie-Gemeinschaften, EEGs) — local groups that generate, share and consume renewable power among their members, instead of every participant buying separately from the grid. They were enabled by the EU's RED II/III renewables directives, which member states had to transpose into national law.

The uptake is striking: 4,000+ energy communities were in operation or formation by mid-2025. For a small country, that is a movement, and it changes how some C&I sites think about sourcing power — they may want to share generation locally, not just sign a PPA.

Across the region

A new electricity-market law, the ElWG (Elektrizitätswirtschaftsgesetz), is advancing through the Austrian parliament in 2025–26. It is designed to cut costs, add flexibility and expand energy sharing well beyond today's rules. Catch: it needs a 2/3 parliamentary majority, so its timing and final shape are genuinely uncertain — check current before promising a client what the law will allow.

The PPA & C&I landscape

Austria's corporate-PPA market is smaller and younger than Germany's, but the logic is the same — and because the two markets are so coupled, German price dynamics largely set the backdrop for Austrian deals.

The local twist is the hydro-heavy supply base. A factory owner here will often say "our grid is already mostly clean" — and they are not wrong on a national-average basis. The pitch therefore shifts from "go green" to price certainty and additionality: a PPA locks a fixed €/MWh for 5–15 years against a volatile market, and a new-build solar or wind asset adds new renewable capacity the country still needs to hit its +27 TWh gap.

So the Austrian deal sits on three legs: a corporate PPA for the price hedge and Guarantees of Origin, EAG support to make the underlying asset bankable, and — for the right site — an energy community as an alternative or complement for locally shared power.

The funding stack

Layer the money sources from national to EU level — and remember the ceiling that caps them all:

  • EAG grants & premium — the national instrument: investment subsidies and the competitive market premium described above.
  • EU pots — the Innovation Fund (large-scale clean-tech, financed by EU ETS auction revenue) and InvestEU (an EU budget guarantee that crowds in private finance) sit above the national schemes.
  • State-aid ceiling — every grant must fit inside the EU's CEEAG (Climate, Energy & Environmental Aid Guidelines, 2022) and GBER (General Block Exemption Regulation) caps. These limit how much subsidy any one project may take — so you cannot simply stack every pot to 100%.
At EU level

Because Austria is in the EU, the full EU frame applies: RED III's binding renewables target, EU ETS carbon pricing, and the 2024 Electricity Market Design reform that actively promotes PPAs and two-way CfDs. Lead with that shared frame, then localise to EAG + E-Control. (Programme details and call windows — check current.)

In the room

How you open with an Austrian prospect who is proud their grid is already clean:

Sales script

Austria's grid is already mostly hydro — so for you this isn't really about going green, it's about locking a price. Right now you're exposed to a wholesale market that tracks Germany and can swing hard. A fifteen-year PPA fixes most of your electricity cost in euros per megawatt-hour, comes with Guarantees of Origin for your reporting, and the new asset behind it qualifies for EAG support, which keeps the price competitive. And if local sharing fits your site, an energy community can sit alongside it. Same product the German plants are buying — built on Austrian rails.

Objections you will hear

Our grid is already mostly hydro — why do we need a PPA?
Rebuttal

Clean on average, yes — but you still pay a volatile, German-linked wholesale price on top of network charges. A PPA isn't about colour, it's about certainty: a fixed €/MWh for a decade-plus, Guarantees of Origin for your CSRD reporting, and additionality — your contract funds new capacity Austria needs to close its +27 TWh gap to 2030.

Shouldn't we just join an energy community instead?
Rebuttal

For some sites, absolutely — and 4,000+ communities show it works. But community sharing suits local, smaller, co-located generation; it rarely covers an industrial load on its own. The two aren't rivals: a PPA hedges the bulk of your consumption at scale, a community can layer local shared power on top. We'll size whichever combination fits your meter.

Isn't the new ElWG law about to change all of this?
Rebuttal

The ElWG is moving through parliament and will likely expand energy sharing — but it needs a two-thirds majority, so the timing and final text aren't settled. We don't build your deal on a law that hasn't passed. The PPA stands on today's rules under the EAG; if ElWG opens new options, that's upside we'll add, not a dependency. Knowledge check
Who is Austria's electricity transmission system operator (TSO)?

APG runs the wires; E-Control regulates; EXAA is a trading exchange. Don't mix the three in front of a client.

What does the EAG (Erneuerbaren-Ausbau-Gesetz) primarily provide?

EAG = market premium (top-up on the wholesale price) + investment grants, aiming at 100% renewable electricity by 2030.

A prospect says "our grid is already hydro." What is the strongest PPA angle?

In a hydro-rich market the value is the price hedge and additionality, not the green colour. Lead with certainty.
