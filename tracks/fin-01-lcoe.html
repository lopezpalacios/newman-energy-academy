Learning objective

By the end you can build LCOE on a napkin from four inputs, state the formula, and explain to a CFO why moving the discount rate by a few points changes the answer more than haggling over panel prices ever will.

What LCOE actually is

Every generation project has lumpy money going out and a long, thin stream of electricity coming back. You cannot compare a €600 panel to a €120 power bill directly — one is a one-off cost, the other is a yearly flow. LCOE (Levelised Cost of Energy) is the device that makes them comparable.

The definition in plain English: LCOE is the constant price per unit of energy that, charged over the asset's whole life, exactly pays back every euro you spent — both the upfront build and the running costs — once all of it is brought to today's money. Quoted in €/MWh, it is the project's true break-even price. If your LCOE is below the customer's grid tariff, there is a deal. If it is above, there is no business no matter how green the story.

Think of it as the cost floor under your PPA price. You sell power above LCOE; the gap is margin. The grid sells power above its own (much higher) all-in cost; that gap is your opening.

Key term · LCOE

Levelised Cost of Energy — total lifetime cost (capex + discounted opex) divided by total lifetime generation (discounted), expressed in €/MWh. The lowest price at which the project breaks even. For C&I solar in Czechia it lands around €50–70/MWh, comfortably under an industrial grid tariff of €100–180/MWh.

The formula — four inputs, one ratio

LCOE is a ratio. The top is everything you spend, the bottom is everything you generate — and crucially, both are discounted to present value so a euro of cost or a kilowatt-hour of output in year 20 counts for less than one today.

LCOE = ( Capex + Σ Opexₜ / (1+r)ᵗ ) ÷ ( Σ Energyₜ / (1+r)ᵗ )

Read it left to right. The numerator is the upfront capex plus the present value of every future year's opex (operations & maintenance, land lease, insurance). The denominator is the present value of every future year's energy — the kWh the plant actually produces. Divide and you get a cost per unit.

  • Capex — the build cost, paid once at the start (no discounting needed). C&I solar ≈ €500–750/kWp.
  • Opex — yearly running cost, ≈ €10–15/kWp/yr. Small, but it compounds over 25 years.
  • Energy — the specific yield, the kWh each kWp produces per year. In Czechia ≈ 950–1,100 kWh/kWp/yr (Iberia gets 1,500+; sun is geography).
  • r — the discount rate, your cost of capital. The quiet input that does the most damage.

Why the discount rate matters most

The discount rate is the return your money could earn elsewhere — your weighted cost of debt and equity, often called the WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital). It appears in both the cost and the energy side of the ratio, so its effect is not obvious until you run it.

Here is the intuition: a higher discount rate shrinks the value of future kWh faster than it shrinks the small future opex. The denominator collapses while the big capex sits there undiscounted. So a higher discount rate pushes LCOE up — sometimes brutally. Same panels, same sun, same site; just a more demanding investor, and suddenly the project is no longer below the grid.

Key term · Discount rate (WACC)

The annual return an investor requires, used to convert future euros to today's value. Built from the cost of debt and the cost of equity, weighted by how much of each funds the project. A developer might use 7–11%; a de-risked operating asset is valued at 6–8%. Lower rate → lower LCOE → more projects clear.

Worked example — LCOE of 1 kWp of Czech solar

We build LCOE per kWp so the number scales to any plant size. Realistic, conservative inputs:

The build (per kWp, 25-year life, 7% discount rate)
Capex (build cost)€600
Opex per year (O&M, lease, insurance)€12
Specific yield (annual generation)1,050 kWh
Discounted opex over 25 yrs @ 7% (× 11.65 annuity factor)€140
Discounted energy over 25 yrs @ 7% (× 11.65 annuity factor)12,236 kWh
LCOE = (€600 + €140) ÷ 12,236 kWh≈ €60.5 / MWh

€60.5/MWh against a Czech industrial tariff of, say, €120/MWh leaves roughly €60/MWh of headroom — room for a PPA price the customer loves and a margin you can sell. This is a real business.

Same plant, three discount rates — watch LCOE move
Discount rateLCOE
3% (cheap institutional debt)≈ €44 / MWh
7% (developer base case)≈ €60 / MWh
12% (expensive equity / risky market)≈ €84 / MWh

Nothing physical changed — same panels, same Czech sun, same opex. The cost of power swung ~90% on financing alone. That is why getting the asset cheap money (and de-risking it) is worth more than any procurement saving.

In the room

How you frame LCOE to a factory owner who only cares about the bill:

Sales script

The grid hands you a different number every year and you sign it, because you have no choice. The solar on your roof produces power at roughly sixty euros a megawatt-hour against the hundred and twenty you pay the grid — and that sixty is fixed for twenty-five years. You stop renting a price you can't control and start owning one. The only question left is how cheaply we finance it, because that sets the sixty — and unlike the grid, that's a number you get to watch us prove.

Objections you will hear

€60/MWh sounds too good — solar can't be that cheap.
Rebuttal

It is a build cost, not a retail price — and you can check every line of it. There is no fuel, no network charge and no supplier margin stacked on top; the sun is free. At €600/kWp over a 25-year life, the maths lands at €50–70 every time. The grid's €120 carries the fuel, network and margin you simply stop paying behind the meter.

Czech sun is weak — won't that wreck the number?
Rebuttal

It softens the number; it does not break it. Czechia yields about 1,050 kWh/kWp against Spain's 1,500, so our LCOE sits higher than Iberia's — and still comfortably under your €120 grid price. Drag the yield down to 950 in the tool yourself; you are still beating the grid by a wide margin. Weaker sun, a smaller saving — but one that's still locked and still yours.

You're hiding behind a discount rate I can't check.
Rebuttal

Fair — so let's put it on the screen and you check it. I'll show you LCOE at 3, 7 and 12 percent live; the rate is just our cost of money, and the cheaper we fund it, the lower your price. That's why we chase low-cost debt and de-risk the asset — it flows straight through to your tariff, in front of you, not behind a curtain.

Knowledge check
What does LCOE represent?

LCOE is the cost floor: total discounted cost ÷ total discounted generation. Sell above it for margin.

Raise the discount rate from 7% to 12% on the same plant. LCOE…

A higher rate shrinks the discounted-energy denominator faster than the cost, so LCOE climbs. Financing is the biggest lever.

Which input does NOT enter the LCOE numerator (the cost side)?

Numerator = capex + discounted opex. Denominator = discounted lifetime energy. Yield sits on the bottom.
