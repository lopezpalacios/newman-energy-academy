Learning objective

By the end you can walk a Czech C&I prospect from their bill to a signed PPA — and name the bodies, the support scheme and the funding involved: who regulates the market, where power is priced, why a Czech solar PPA beats the grid tariff, and how the Modernisation Fund pays part of the build.

Czechia at a glance

Start with the plumbing. Three bodies decide how power moves and what it costs in Czechia: the regulator, the transmission system operator (TSO), and the market operator that runs the spot market and the green-certificate registry.

The regulator is the ERÚ — the Energetický regulační úřad (Energy Regulatory Office). It sets the regulated network tariffs and oversees the rules of the market. The TSO is ČEPS, a.s., which runs the high-voltage transmission grid and balances supply and demand nationally. The market operator is OTE, a.s., which runs the Czech day-ahead (spot) market and keeps the Guarantees of Origin (GO) registry.

Crucially, OTE's day-ahead market is coupled into the EU single day-ahead market — so the Czech hourly price is set in the same merit-order auction that clears across most of the continent. Plants are stacked cheapest-first and the price is set by the last (most expensive) unit needed, usually a gas plant. So gas sets the Czech power price even when most of the electricity flowing is cheap nuclear, coal or solar.

What a factory actually pays is not just that commodity price. The Czech industrial all-in tariff is order-of-magnitude €100–180/MWh (check current) depending on voltage level — and a big slice of that is the ERÚ-regulated network charges and levies sitting on top of the energy commodity. That regulated stack is exactly what a behind-the-meter PPA lets a customer avoid.

Support schemes — how Czechia pays renewables

Czechia does not run a big feed-in or auction scheme like Germany's EEG. Instead, the support is capital grants drawn largely from EU-financed pots. Three names matter, and you must be able to define each in the room.

Key term · Modernisation Fund (RES+)

An EU fund financed by the sale of ETS carbon allowances, reserved for 13 lower-income member states including Czechia. In CZ it is operated by SFŽP ČR (the State Environmental Fund) through national programmes — most relevantly RES+, which grants capex for new solar and other renewables, plus storage and efficiency. It is the single most relevant pot for Czech solar and BESS.

Key term · OP TAK

Operační program Technologie a aplikace pro konkurenceschopnost (Operational Programme Technology & Applications for Competitiveness, 2021–27, ERDF-funded). Run by the Ministry of Industry & Trade (MPO) and administered by the agency API (Agentura pro podnikání a inovace), it runs energy-saving and renewables-for-business calls — the channel a company applies through.

Nová zelená úsporám (New Green Savings) is the third: an SFŽP programme aimed mainly at buildings — rooftop PV and storage rather than utility-scale plants. Together these three cover most of the Czech support map: Modernisation Fund/RES+ for new generation, OP TAK for business energy projects, and Nová zelená úsporám for the building envelope.

The Czech PPA & C&I landscape

A PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) is a long-term contract — typically 5–15 years — that fixes the price of power between a generator and a buyer. The Czech case for one is unusually clean, because the economics already favour it.

Czech solar runs at a specific yield of roughly 950–1,100 kWh/kWp/yr (check current) — lower than Iberia, but enough that the levelised cost of solar power (LCOE) lands around €50–70/MWh. Set that against an industrial all-in grid tariff of roughly €100–180/MWh and you have grid parity and then some: the solar electron is materially cheaper than the grid electron the factory buys today.

Key term · Behind-the-meter advantage

When panels sit on the client's own roof or land and the client buys the kWh directly, the power never touches the public grid — so it avoids the ERÚ-regulated network charges and levies that make up a big slice of the tariff. That is the single biggest source of saving, and it is structural, not a subsidy.

The result: growing corporate-PPA interest from Czech industrials looking to lock a price below the grid, clean up their Scope 2 carbon reporting, and hedge the volatility that the 2022 gas shock burned into every CFO's memory. The market is younger and thinner than Germany's, but the underlying spread — cheap solar versus an expensive regulated tariff — is wider.

Grid connection & permitting

A Czech project lives or dies on two gates: grid connection and permitting. Treat the interconnection as the critical path — it is usually the longest-lead, highest-risk item in the whole programme.

Connection runs through ČEPS for transmission-level projects, or the relevant regional distribution operator for smaller ones. You apply for a connection study and a reservation of capacity; congested substations can delay or constrain a project regardless of how good the site is.

Permitting centres on the building permit — the stavební povolení — issued by the local building authority, plus an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) where the size or location triggers it. EU RED III is pushing member states toward faster permitting and "renewables-acceleration areas", so the direction of travel is shorter timelines (check current) — but in practice the realistic gate today is still the connection queue, not the planning office.

Funding the deal

A Czech renewable project usually stacks several support and finance sources rather than relying on one:

  • Modernisation Fund / RES+, applied for via SFŽP ČR (sfzp.cz) — the primary capex grant for new solar and storage.
  • OP TAK, administered by API (agentura-api.org) — energy calls for business projects.
  • Národní plán obnovy — the Czech National Recovery Plan under the EU's Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF), with green-energy components.
  • NRB (Národní rozvojová banka — the National Development Bank) — co-finance and guarantees that sit alongside the grant.

Two guardrails to name in the room. First, grants are co-financing: a programme typically funds only part of eligible capex (often ~30–65%) and you bring the rest. Second, public support is capped by the EU's CEEAG (Climate, Energy & Environmental Aid Guidelines) and the GBER (General Block Exemption Regulation) — they decide how much subsidy a single project may legally absorb, so you cannot stack every pot to 100%. Always read the live call text before quoting amounts (check current).

In the room

How you open with a Czech industrial prospect — the plant manager or CFO of a manufacturer outside Brno or Plzeň:

Sales script

Right now the grid charges you somewhere around a hundred and twenty euros a megawatt-hour — and most of that isn't even power, it's ČEPS network charges and ERÚ levies stacked on top, a number you don't control. Czech solar lands nearer fifty to seventy, so we put panels behind your meter, fix that price under a PPA, and have the Modernisation Fund through SFŽP carry part of the build cost — EU money de-risking your capex. You keep buying the rest from the grid exactly as you do now. Same factory, an energy line your CFO can finally draw as a straight line, with Brussels helping pay for it.

Objections you will hear

The grant process is slow and uncertain — I can't build a plan on "maybe".
Rebuttal

Fair — so we don't lean on it. The savings case stands alone: behind-the-meter solar at fifty-to-seventy beats your grid tariff with or without a grant, so the Modernisation Fund through SFŽP is upside on payback, never the foundation. We run the RES+ or OP TAK application ourselves and sequence the works to the call window — because grants can't legally start before you apply, the support is never put at risk by accident.

Czech winters are dark — solar won't produce when I need it.
Rebuttal

You're right that winter output dips — which is exactly why we size the PPA to a share of your load, sixty percent in the example, and keep the grid as backup for the rest. At ~950–1,100 kWh per kWp a year the annual yield is still very real, and storage can shift the summer midday surplus. You're hedging your biggest, most volatile cost — not betting the factory on sunshine.

A 10–15 year PPA locks me in — what if prices fall or my needs change?
Rebuttal

The lock-in is the protection, not the trap — it's what shields you from the next 2022, when wholesale power hit three to four hundred euros. We fix the price below today's grid tariff, so you're ahead from day one, and shape the volume to your real demand with flexibility built in. The certainty is the product: your CFO finally gets a straight line on the energy budget.

Knowledge check
In Czechia, who regulates the market and who runs the day-ahead spot market?

ERÚ is the Czech energy regulator; OTE is the market operator and GO registry, coupled into the EU day-ahead market. ČEPS is the TSO (it doesn't regulate), and SFŽP operates the Modernisation Fund. (BNetzA/EPEX is Germany.)

What is the Modernisation Fund, and who operates it in Czechia?

The Modernisation Fund is ETS-financed and reserved for 13 lower-income member states including Czechia; SFŽP ČR operates it nationally, with RES+ the key capex grant for new renewables and storage.

Why does a behind-the-meter solar PPA save a Czech factory more than buying from the grid?

The saving is structural: cheaper generation plus avoided regulated network charges and levies, because the power never touches the public grid. Czech yield is actually lower than Iberia's — the spread, not the sun, makes the case. (Figures: check current.)
