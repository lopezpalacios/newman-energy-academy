Objection handling that lands

An objection is not a wall — it is the prospect telling you exactly what they need to believe before they can say yes. Handle the five that come up every time, calmly and with specifics, and the objection becomes the moment you win the deal.

Learning objective

By the end you can answer the five recurring C&I energy objections — too good to be true, what if you go bankrupt, lock-in, technology risk, roof risk — with a calm, specific reply that converts doubt into trust rather than into a debate.

The method: acknowledge, reframe, prove

Every good objection answer has the same three beats. First acknowledge — the concern is legitimate, and saying so disarms it. Then reframe — show why the thing they fear is actually handled by how the deal is built. Then prove — a number, a clause, a precedent. Never argue; agree, redirect, evidence.

An objection is a buying signal: a prospect who is leaving doesn't bother to object. Treat each one as a request for the specific reassurance they need, and answer that request precisely. The fastest way to lose is to get defensive or to bury them in jargon. The fastest way to win is to have already thought about it more carefully than they have.

Key term · Step-in rights

A contract clause that lets the customer's power keep flowing even if the developer fails — typically by letting the financier or a replacement operator "step in" and take over the asset and the O&M contract. It is the single most powerful answer to "what if you go bankrupt", because it proves the customer's supply does not depend on the seller surviving.

The five objections you will hear every time

Across hundreds of C&I meetings, the doubts cluster into five. Know them cold:

  • Too good to be true — "Cheaper power and no upfront cost? Where's the catch?"
  • What if you go bankrupt — "I'm tying my factory's power to a company that might not exist in ten years."
  • Lock-in / long contract — "Fifteen years is a long time to be tied to one supplier."
  • Technology risk — "What if the panels or batteries underperform, or are obsolete in five years?"
  • Roof / structural risk — "What happens to my roof — leaks, weight, repairs under the panels?"

The three strongest go in your active playbook below. The other two — lock-in and technology risk — are handled in prose after it. Notice that none of these is really about price; they are all about risk. Your job is to show the risk is already engineered out.

Across the region

A DACH or EU buyer adds a sixth objection: "Is Switzerland even in the EU power market?" Answer it straight — not today. The Bilaterals III electricity agreement was signed 2 March 2026 but is not in force (a Swiss referendum is likely; realistically ~2030 if ratified), so Switzerland still trades bilaterally under ElCom and Swissgrid, outside the EU's coupled market. Crucially, the project economics — solar below the local tariff, a fixed PPA price — don't depend on it either way. Check current.

Across markets: the risk-transfer answers travel unchanged; only the market-integration footnote differs — and Switzerland's pending EU electricity deal changes nothing about the saving on offer today.

Lock-in and technology risk, in prose

Lock-in. The fear is being trapped at an above-market price. Reframe it: the contract is a hedge, deliberately set below their current grid tariff and shielded from the gas-driven volatility that has whipsawed wholesale prices since 2022. Then prove the exit exists — most C&I contracts carry an assignment clause (the deal moves with the building if they sell) and a buy-out schedule (a pre-agreed price to end early). They're not locked in; they're locked to a price below the one they'd otherwise pay, with a door if they need it.

Technology risk. The fear is paying for kit that underperforms or dates. The reframe is ownership: under a PPA or EaaS the developer owns and operates the asset and is paid only for energy actually delivered, so underperformance is the developer's loss, not the customer's. Module output is warranted (panels routinely carry 25-year performance warranties degrading to ~80–85% of day-one output), and because you're selling kWh, not hardware, "obsolescence" is your problem to manage — they just keep buying cheap power.

In the room

The universal opener that buys you the floor on any objection — acknowledge before you answer:

Sales script

That's exactly the right question, and if you weren't asking it I'd be worried. Here's the thing — we've built the deal so that the risk you're describing isn't actually yours to carry. The panels sit on your roof, the contract has a step-in clause, and if we ever disappeared, your power keeps flowing while a replacement operator takes over. You're not betting on us surviving. You're buying cheaper power with the risk parked somewhere else.

Objections you will hear

Cheaper power and no upfront cost — what's the catch?
Rebuttal

There's no catch, there's a trade. Solar costs about fifty to seventy euros a megawatt-hour to generate — well below the hundred-plus you pay the grid — so the saving is real physics, not a gimmick. What we ask in return is a long contract, because that's what lets us fund the build with no money from you. The "catch" is simply that you commit to buying your own cheap power for a decade. I'd rather you knew that on slide one than find it in the small print.

What happens to my factory's power if your company goes bankrupt?
Rebuttal

Your power doesn't depend on us surviving — and that's deliberate. The asset is physically on your roof, and the contract carries step-in rights: if we ever failed, our financier or a replacement operator takes over the asset and the maintenance contract, and your kWh keep flowing under the same terms. In the very worst case you simply revert to the grid, exactly where you are today. There's no version of this where you're left in the dark.

Who's liable if the panels damage my roof — leaks, weight, repairs?
Rebuttal

We are, in writing. Before a single panel goes up we run a structural survey, and we only proceed if your roof carries the load with margin. Through the whole contract the developer insures and indemnifies the roof — if a penetration leaks, we fix it. At end of term we remove the system and leave the roof as we found it. You're getting a free structural inspection and a roof someone else is now responsible for keeping watertight.

Is Switzerland even part of the EU electricity market?
Rebuttal

Not today — and it doesn't change your saving. Switzerland trades bilaterally, outside the EU's coupled market, under ElCom and Swissgrid. The Bilaterals III electricity agreement was signed in March 2026, but it isn't in force; with a referendum likely it's realistically around 2030 before anything shifts, if at all. None of that touches the deal in front of you: your solar still generates below your regulated tariff, and your PPA price is fixed regardless of how the EU integration plays out. We're selling you cheaper power on your own roof, not a bet on Brussels and Bern.

Knowledge check
What is the three-beat structure of a strong objection answer?

Agree, redirect, evidence. Defensiveness loses; calm specifics win.

Which clause is the strongest answer to "what if you go bankrupt"?

Step-in rights prove the customer's supply doesn't depend on the developer surviving.

Under a PPA/EaaS, who carries the risk that the panels underperform?

Selling kWh, not hardware, parks performance and obsolescence risk with the developer.
