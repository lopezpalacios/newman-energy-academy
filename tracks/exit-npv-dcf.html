NPV, DCF & the exit valuation

You do not get rich generating electricity. You get rich selling the machine that generates it — to a fund that accepts a lower return than you did. This lesson shows exactly how that valuation works, and why the gap is the whole business.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain, on a whiteboard, why a renewable asset is worth more to a fund than it cost you to build — and put a number on that premium using discounted cash flow.

Where the money actually is

A newcomer assumes the profit in a solar or battery project is the spread between what it costs to generate power and what the customer pays. That spread is real, but it is thin and it dribbles in over decades.

The large, fast profit is the development premium: you take on the messy, risky work of finding a site, signing a customer, getting a grid connection and building the plant. Once it is switched on and selling power under a signed contract, almost all of that risk is gone. The asset becomes a bond-like stream of contracted cash — exactly what a pension fund or infrastructure fund wants to buy.

And here is the trick: they will pay more for that safety than you required to take the risk. You needed, say, an 11% return to justify building. They are happy with 7% because the risk is gone. A lower required return on the same cash flows means a higher price. That re-pricing is called yield compression, and it is the engine of the whole industry.

Key term · Yield compression

When a buyer values an asset at a lower discount rate (required yield) than the developer did, the same future cash flows translate into a higher present value. Build at a high yield, sell at a low one, pocket the difference.

Discounted cash flow, in one idea

A euro next year is worth less than a euro today, because today's euro can be invested. Discounting just reverses that: it asks "how much is a future euro worth right now?" The answer depends on the discount rate — the return the buyer demands.

PV = CF₁ / (1 + r)¹  +  CF₂ / (1 + r)²  +  …  +  CFₙ / (1 + r)ⁿ

Add up the present value of every year's cash flow and you get the Net Present Value (NPV) — the price a buyer should rationally pay. The lower the discount rate r, the bigger every term, the higher the price. That single lever — r — is where yield compression lives.

  • CF — the net cash the asset throws off each year (revenue minus operating cost).
  • r — the discount rate / required yield. Yours when you build; the fund's when they buy.
  • n — the asset's contracted (and useful) life in years.

Worked example — a 5 MW Czech solar plant

Take a ground-mounted solar plant outside Brno. Round, realistic numbers:

The build
Capacity5 MWp
Build cost (capex, ≈ €600 / kWp)€3,000,000
Annual generation (≈ 1,050 kWh / kWp)5,250 MWh

At a build cost of €3.0M for €350k/yr over a 25-year life, you are earning roughly an 11% return for taking the development risk. Good — but now watch what happens at sale.

The sale — present value of the same cash flows at a 7% fund yield
InputValue
Net cash flow per year€350,000
Life remaining25 years
Your build hurdle rate11%
Fund's required yield7%
Sale price = NPV of cash flows @ 7%≈ €4,080,000

You spent €3.0M. A fund pays ≈ €4.08M for the identical asset — a ~€1.08M gain, a 1.36× money multiple — purely because they discount the same cash at 7% instead of 11%. That is the exit.

In the room

How you say this to a non-technical investor or business owner who is deciding whether to back the project:

Sales script

We build the plant for three million and it earns about three-fifty a year on a signed, fifteen-year contract — that is roughly an eleven percent return just for holding it. But once it is operating and de-risked, the pension funds buying these assets are happy with seven. Same cash, lower required return, higher price. That re-rating is worth about a million euros, and it is why we build to sell, not to hold.

Objections you will hear

If it's such a good asset, why are you selling it?
Rebuttal

Because our edge is development, not holding. Funds have a lower cost of capital than we do — they can pay more for the safe cash flow than it is worth to us. Selling recycles our capital into the next three projects. Everyone wins at their own cost of money.

What if interest rates rise and the fund's yield goes up?
Rebuttal

Then the compression premium shrinks — drag the fund-yield slider up and you will see it. That is exactly why we lock the offtake contract and sell into a competitive process. We also model the exit at a conservative yield; anything better is upside, not the base case.

Isn't the premium just financial engineering?
Rebuttal

No — it is paid for real risk transfer. We absorbed permitting, construction and offtake risk; the buyer pays to step in after all of it is resolved. The premium is the market price of the certainty we created. That is a service, not a trick.

Knowledge check
A buyer values your asset at a lower discount rate than you used. What happens to the price?

Lower required yield → higher NPV on identical cash flows. That gap is the exit premium.

In the worked example, where does the ~€1.08M gain come from?

Same cash, lower discount rate, higher price. The four-point yield gap is the whole gain.

Why is the asset worth more after it is built and contracted than during development?

De-risking is what compresses the yield. The buyer pays for certainty you created.
