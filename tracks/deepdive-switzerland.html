Selling renewables in Switzerland

Switzerland is in DACH but not in the EU — and today not in the EU internal electricity market. Same product, different rails: a hydro-heavy grid, a regulator called ElCom, a brand-new energy law, and a one-time PV grant instead of an EU feed-in scheme. This is how the Swiss pitch differs.

Learning objective

By the end you can describe how renewable power is regulated, supported and contracted in Switzerland — naming ElCom, Swissgrid, the Stromgesetz and the Einmalvergütung — and explain why Switzerland's non-EU status changes the picture versus Germany, Austria or Czechia.

Switzerland at a glance

Switzerland is a member of the DACH region (Germany, Austria, Switzerland) by language and economy — but it is not an EU member state. That single fact reshapes everything below.

The energy regulator is ElCom (Eidgenössische Elektrizitätskommission), the independent electricity-market watchdog. The transmission system operator (TSO) — the company that runs the high-voltage grid — is Swissgrid. The renewable-certificate registry is run by Pronovo.

Because Switzerland sits outside the EU internal electricity market, it trades electricity bilaterally with neighbours rather than through EU day-ahead market coupling. It is not part of the EU single day-ahead coupling today. The grid is heavily hydro-dominated, which is cheap and clean in summer but leaves the country reliant on winter imports — so reserve / backup power and winter supply security are the dominant policy themes you will hear from any Swiss counterparty.

The EU relationship — signed, not in force

This is the most misunderstood point about Switzerland, and the one a sharp prospect will test you on. Be precise.

Key term · Switzerland and the EU

Carbon is already connected: the EU ETS and the Swiss ETS have been linked since 2020, so an emissions allowance flows between the two systems.

But the electricity market is not yet integrated. As part of the Bilaterals III package, the EU and Switzerland signed a broad agreement — including an electricity agreement — on 2 March 2026. It would integrate Switzerland into the EU internal electricity market, but it is not yet in force: Swiss parliamentary debate runs 2026–27, a referendum is likely, and (with European Parliament approval also still pending) entry into force is realistically ~2030 at the earliest. Signed ≠ done.

So today Switzerland still trades bilaterally, and EU directives like RED III and EU state-aid rules do not apply directly. (Check current — this is the fastest-moving fact on the page.)

Practical takeaway for the room: do not lead a Swiss pitch with EU mandates. The EU tailwinds (RED III, the 2024 market-design reform pushing PPAs and two-way CfDs) are real for your German and Austrian deals — but in Switzerland they are, at best, a future "if ratified" story.

The Stromgesetz / "Mantelerlass"

Switzerland's headline energy reform is the Stromgesetz — the "Mantelerlass" (umbrella act), a single law that amended several energy statutes at once. It was approved by referendum on 9 June 2024 with about 68.7% in favour — a strong popular mandate, which matters in a country where voters can overturn legislation.

Its purpose is to accelerate renewables build-out and shore up winter supply security — directly answering the hydro-import problem above. It is being phased in: the Energy Act parts took effect from 1 January 2025, and the Electricity Supply Act parts from 1 January 2026.

When a Swiss prospect asks "is the country actually serious about this?", the answer is a two-thirds referendum win — not a Brussels directive. That framing lands far better than citing EU law that does not apply.

Support & Guarantees of Origin

Switzerland does not run a German-style ongoing feed-in tariff for new PV. Instead, solar is supported mainly through a one-time investment grant — the Einmalvergütung: a single upfront contribution toward the cost of the system, rather than a per-kWh payment over many years.

For the salesperson, that changes the economics conversation: the subsidy lowers the capex the project carries, instead of topping up the revenue each year. It improves the build-side return and the payback, not the ongoing tariff.

Guarantees of Origin — the certificate proving 1 MWh came from a renewable source — are called Herkunftsnachweis in Switzerland, and the registry that issues and tracks them is run by Pronovo.

The Swiss C&I / PPA landscape

The product is the same one you sell everywhere — a PPA (Power Purchase Agreement), a contract that fixes the price of power for years instead of riding the volatile spot market. What is different in Switzerland is the rails it runs on:

  • Outside the EU market: pricing references a bilaterally-traded, not EU-coupled, market, so spot benchmarks and cross-border flows behave differently from a German or Czech deal.
  • Hydro-heavy: abundant cheap hydro in summer and the winter-import squeeze make supply security — not just price — a genuine selling point for a fixed long-term contract.
  • Grant, not feed-in: a corporate PPA stacks naturally on top of the Einmalvergütung — the grant cuts the build cost, the PPA contracts the offtake.
  • GO trading & cross-border recognition: Switzerland participates in the AIB (Association of Issuing Bodies) hub through Pronovo, so Swiss Herkunftsnachweis can be recognised across borders — but treat the exact cross-border recognition rules as a live nuance and check current, since they are tied to the unresolved EU-CH relationship.

Funding: national support only

Here is the sharpest contrast with the rest of your patch. In Germany, Austria and Czechia, projects can tap the EU funding stack — the Modernisation Fund, Innovation Fund, InvestEU, EIB — all sitting inside the EU state-aid envelope (CEEAG / GBER) that caps how much subsidy a project may take.

None of that applies in Switzerland. EU funds and EU state-aid rules do not reach a Swiss project. Support is national only — principally the Einmalvergütung PV grant. So when you build a Swiss case, do not assemble an EU-grant story; build it on the Swiss grant plus the PPA economics, full stop.

Remember this across markets

Same pitch logic everywhere — fixed-price clean power that beats the grid tariff. Only the support scheme and regulator change: EEG/BNetzA in Germany, EAG/E-Control in Austria, Modernisation Fund/ERÚ in Czechia, and Einmalvergütung/ElCom in Switzerland — outside the EU market and its funding.

In the room

How you say this to a Swiss factory owner or CFO — leaning on supply security and the grant, not EU rules:

Sales script

You already know the worry — Switzerland leans on imports through the winter, and ElCom keeps flagging supply security. A long-term solar PPA fixes a chunk of your power price for years, so you sit out that winter scramble instead of paying for it. The one-time federal grant — the Einmalvergütung — knocks the build cost down, which is what lets the contracted price beat your grid tariff. And it all stands on its own under Swiss law: your price is settled in Bern, not waiting on anything in Brussels.

Objections you will hear

We're not even in the EU electricity market — does any of this work here?
Rebuttal

It works precisely because it doesn't depend on the EU market. A PPA is a private contract between you and the generator under Swiss law, regulated by ElCom and settled through Swissgrid. Sitting outside EU coupling is exactly why a fixed long-term price is worth so much — it shelters you from a market that trades bilaterally and tightens every winter.

Isn't the EU deal coming anyway — shouldn't we just wait for it?
Rebuttal

The Bilaterals III electricity agreement was signed in March 2026, but it isn't in force — parliament debates it through 2026–27, a referendum is likely, and realistic entry is around 2030 if it's ratified at all. The grant and the PPA are on the table today, under current Swiss law — solid, signable, here. Waiting means deferring real savings for a maybe at the end of the decade.

Prices are in francs and the grant rules keep moving — how solid are these numbers?
Rebuttal

Fair — so we build your case on your actual CHF bill, never a euro model, and we size the Einmalvergütung against the current published rate before we commit a single number. The figures on screen are illustrative; the proposal you sign is in francs, at the grant level in force on the day. We re-check both before we ever quote.

Knowledge check
What is Switzerland's status in the EU internal electricity market today?

Signed ≠ in force. Entry is realistically ~2030 if ratified; today CH still trades bilaterally and EU directives like RED III do not apply directly. Only the ETS link (2020) connects carbon.

How is new PV mainly supported in Switzerland?

Switzerland favours an upfront capex grant over an ongoing revenue top-up. EU funds do not apply to a non-EU country.

Which institution runs Switzerland's high-voltage grid, and which issues its Guarantees of Origin?

Swissgrid operates the grid and ElCom regulates it; Pronovo runs the GO registry. Switzerland participates in the AIB hub for cross-border recognition, but does not issue through it.
