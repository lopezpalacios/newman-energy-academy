Volatility & gas-set marginal pricing

Here is the fact that unlocks the whole sales case: even when most of Europe's power is cheap renewables, the price is set by the last, most expensive plant switched on — usually a gas plant. Understand that one rule and you understand why your customer is in pain.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain why gas sets the power price even when renewables dominate the grid, and show — with numbers — how a fixed PPA price turns a wildly volatile cost into a flat line the customer can plan around.

Merit-order: the last plant sets the price

Every hour, the market needs to meet demand at the lowest possible cost. So it stacks generators from cheapest to most expensive and switches them on in that order. This stack is the merit order.

Cheapest first: renewables (sun and wind cost almost nothing to run once built) and nuclear. Then, as demand rises, more expensive plants join — coal, then gas. The market keeps switching plants on until supply finally meets demand. The price everyone gets paid is the running cost of that last unit dispatched — the marginal plant. This is marginal pricing: one clearing price for the whole hour, set by the most expensive plant needed.

Because demand in most hours is high enough to need gas, gas usually is the marginal plant. So the price of gas sets the price of electricity — even if 70% of the power in that hour came from wind and solar that cost nothing. The cheap renewables get paid the expensive gas price too; that is just how a single clearing price works.

Key term · Marginal (merit-order) pricing

All generators in an hour are paid the running cost of the last (most expensive) plant needed to meet demand. Since that plant is usually gas, gas sets the power price — which is why a gas-price spike becomes an electricity-price spike across the whole market.

The 2022 shock — and why prices stayed structurally higher

When European gas supply was disrupted in 2022, the marginal-pricing rule did exactly what it is built to do: it transmitted the gas spike straight into power. Wholesale prices that had sat around €40–50/MWh before 2021 spiked to €300–400+/MWh at the worst peaks.

Prices have since reverted to roughly a €60–110/MWh range — but notice that range sits structurally above the old €40–50 norm, and it moves around far more than it used to. The point is not the spike itself; the spike has faded. The point is that the customer now lives with a cost that is both higher and far less predictable than it was a few years ago.

Key term · Structural volatility

Wholesale power no longer sits at a stable low number. Post-2022 it swings within a wider, higher band (illustratively €60–110/MWh, with spikes possible) because gas — itself volatile — keeps setting the marginal price. Verify live figures on OTE/ENTSO-E before quoting any specific number.

Volatility is the customer's pain — and the PPA is the cure

A factory owner does not actually fear a high price as much as they fear a price they cannot plan. A CFO can price products around €120/MWh if it stays at €120. What destroys them is signing a customer order at one energy cost and then watching the cost double before they deliver.

That uncertainty is the pain. And it is exactly what a PPA — Power Purchase Agreement removes: it fixes the energy price for 5–15 years. The customer trades the chance of a lucky cheap year for the certainty of a known number every year. For a business that lives or dies on margins, certainty is usually worth more than the gamble.

Across the region

Market coupling means the gas-set volatility you just saw is shared across the connected EU bidding zones. Czechia, Germany and Austria all settle their day-ahead auctions inside SDAC — Single Day-Ahead Coupling (run via ENTSO-E), so one gas event lands on a Brno, a Munich and a Vienna bill in the same hour. Germany trades on EPEX SPOT, Austria on EXAA/EPEX, Czechia on OTE — but the cleared prices move together.

Switzerland is the exception: it is not in the EU internal electricity market today and sits outside single day-ahead coupling, trading bilaterally — so Swiss prices can diverge from the coupled core. (An EU–CH electricity agreement was signed 2 March 2026 but is not yet in force, realistically ~2030 if ratified — check current.) The EU's Electricity Market Design reform (in force 16 July 2024) was written partly to blunt exactly these spikes and push long-term contracting like PPAs.

Across markets: the merit-order rule is identical everywhere — gas sets the marginal price in every coupled zone, and a fixed PPA is the same cure whether the spot prints on OTE, EPEX or EXAA.

In the room

How to make marginal pricing land with a CFO in one breath:

Sales script

Here's the thing nobody tells you: even when most of the grid is cheap solar and wind, you pay the price of the last gas plant that had to switch on. So your electricity bill is really a gas bill in disguise — and gas is the most volatile commodity there is. In 2022 that doubled or tripled overnight. We can't make gas behave. What we can do is take your energy cost off the gas rollercoaster entirely and put it on a flat line for the next ten years.

Objections you will hear

If renewables are so cheap, why hasn't my bill gone down?
Rebuttal

Because of how the market prices power. Cheap renewables don't set the price — the last, most expensive plant does, and that's usually gas. So until you have your own fixed contract, you keep paying the gas price no matter how much cheap solar is on the grid. A PPA is how you finally capture the cheap-renewable cost directly.

The 2022 spike is over. Why would I lock in now?
Rebuttal

The spike faded, but prices settled higher and bouncier than before — and the next gas event is a question of when, not if. You don't buy insurance after the fire. Locking a fixed price now isn't betting prices rise; it's removing the bet entirely so you can plan your margins with confidence.

What if spot prices fall below my PPA price? I'll have overpaid.
Rebuttal

In some years you might pay slightly more — our example showed exactly that. But you're not buying the lowest possible price; you're buying certainty and protection from the disaster years. Companies don't fail because energy was 5% over spot one year. They fail because it tripled in the year they'd already fixed their sales prices.

Knowledge check
Under merit-order pricing, who sets the electricity price in a given hour?

All generators are paid the running cost of the marginal (last) plant. Since that's usually gas, gas sets the power price.

What is the real pain a fixed PPA solves for a factory CFO?

A PPA sells certainty, not the lowest price. In some years spot may be cheaper — but the customer can finally plan.

In the worked example, why did the PPA still win over four years despite costing more in Years 1 and 4?

The PPA cost a little more in calm years but removed the disaster year — net €700k saved plus full predictability.
