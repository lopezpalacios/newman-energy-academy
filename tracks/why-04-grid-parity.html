Learning objective

By the end you can state the full macro case in two minutes: solar now undercuts the grid on price (grid parity), and it simultaneously fixes the three pains — price volatility, carbon cost, and energy security — that every C&I customer is living with.

Grid parity — the moment the maths flipped

Grid parity is the point at which generating your own renewable power costs the same or less than buying it from the grid. For years solar needed subsidies to compete. Today, in much of Europe — Czechia included — well-sited solar produces power for less than the industrial tariff, with no subsidy needed.

This is the hinge of the whole business. Before parity, you were selling a more expensive green product and asking the customer to pay up for their conscience. After parity, you are selling a cheaper product that also happens to be clean. The pitch stops being "pay more to do good" and becomes "pay less and do good" — a fundamentally easier sale.

Key term · Grid parity

When the cost of generating renewable power (its LCOE) is at or below the price of buying power from the grid (the industrial tariff). Once crossed, renewables compete on cost alone — no subsidy required.

LCOE — the unit cost that makes parity measurable

To compare "build your own" against "buy from the grid" you need one fair number for each. For generation that number is the LCOE — Levelised Cost of Energy: the total lifetime cost of a plant (build, finance, operate) divided by all the energy it produces over its life, expressed in €/MWh. It is the true all-in cost of a megawatt-hour from that asset.

For Czech solar, LCOE lands around €50–70/MWh — driven by capex near €500–750/kWp and a specific yield of roughly 950–1,100 kWh/kWp/year (lower than sunny Iberia's 1,500+, which is why CZ numbers sit a little higher). Set that €50–70 beside a Czech industrial all-in tariff of €100–180/MWh and parity isn't close — solar wins outright. You will build LCOE cell by cell in Track 03; here, just hold the headline.

Key term · LCOE (Levelised Cost of Energy)

Lifetime cost of a generating asset ÷ lifetime energy produced, in €/MWh. The single number that lets you compare a solar plant against a grid tariff on equal footing. Czech solar ≈ €50–70/MWh.

The three customer pains — the whole macro case

Everything in this track collapses into three pains every C&I customer feels at once. Name them in order and you have your entire opening argument:

  • 1 · Price volatility — the energy cost rides the gas-set spot market and can double overnight (Lessons 1–2). A fixed PPA flattens it.
  • 2 · Carbon cost — ETS allowances, CBAM and CSRD reporting turn emissions into a rising, board-level cost (Lesson 3). Renewable power + GOs removes it.
  • 3 · Energy security — dependence on imported gas and a single grid supply is a strategic risk. On-site and contracted renewables give the business control over its own supply.

The reason this is a business — and not charity — is that grid parity means you can solve all three pains while lowering the customer's cost. Cheaper, more predictable, cleaner, more secure. That is the macro case, and every product in this academy is one way of delivering it.

Key term · The three pains

Price volatility, carbon cost, energy security. The three problems every commercial & industrial energy buyer carries. A well-structured renewable PPA addresses all three at once — and, past grid parity, does so at a lower price than the status quo.

In the room

The whole-track close, delivered to a business owner in one move:

Sales script

Let me make this simple. You have three problems with energy: the price won't sit still, the carbon is becoming a real cost, and you don't control your own supply. Five years ago, fixing those meant paying a premium. Today it doesn't — building solar costs about sixty euros a megawatt-hour and you're paying the grid around a hundred and forty. So we can make your energy cheaper, flatter, cleaner and more secure, all at once. The only question left is which structure fits your roof, your balance sheet and your appetite — and that's what the rest of this is about.

Objections you will hear

If solar is genuinely cheaper than the grid, why isn't everyone already doing it?
Rebuttal

Because the cheaper number is the lifetime cost, and most businesses think in this year's cash, not a fifteen-year average — plus building it takes capital, permitting and roof space they don't want to manage. That gap between "obviously cheaper" and "actually done" is exactly the friction we remove. The economics flipped faster than habits did.

Czech sunshine is weak. Does solar even make sense here?
Rebuttal

Czechia yields around 1,000 kilowatt-hours per kilowatt a year — lower than Spain, true. But the LCOE still lands near sixty euros against a grid tariff well over a hundred. You don't need Iberian sun to beat a Central European industrial tariff; you just need the maths to clear, and here it clears comfortably.

Which of the three pains should I actually care about most?
Rebuttal

Whichever one keeps you up at night — and the point is you don't have to choose. The same renewable PPA addresses all three: it flattens the price, cuts the reportable carbon, and hands you control over your own supply. That's why this is a structural shift, not a single-issue product. We tailor which pain leads the pitch to your situation.

Knowledge check
What does "grid parity" mean?

Grid parity = renewable LCOE at or below the grid tariff. Past it, the pitch flips from "pay more to do good" to "pay less and do good."

What does LCOE measure?

LCOE is the all-in lifetime €/MWh cost of a plant — the fair number to set against a grid tariff. Czech solar ≈ €50–70/MWh.

Price volatility, carbon cost, energy security — the three pains. Past grid parity, a PPA solves all three while lowering cost.
