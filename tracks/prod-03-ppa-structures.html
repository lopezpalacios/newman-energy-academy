PPA structures: physical, sleeved, virtual

A PPA is the contract that turns a power plant into a business. But "PPA" hides three very different deals — and pitching the wrong one wastes a meeting. This lesson gives you all three, the trade-offs, and a rule for choosing.

Learning objective

By the end you can define the three PPA structures, explain the trade-off each makes between saving, scale and simplicity, and pick the right one to pitch based on the customer's roof, load and motive.

What a PPA is

A PPA — Power Purchase Agreement — is a long-term contract (typically 5–15 years) in which a buyer agrees to buy electricity at an agreed price. It is the instrument that replaces the volatile spot market — the day-ahead price set each day on OTE, the Czech market operator — with a fixed, predictable number.

When the buyer is a company taking that power for its own operations, it is a corporate offtake — and "offtaker" is just the name for whoever contracts to take the output. The fixed price is what makes the project bankable: a lender will finance a plant with a signed PPA because the future revenue is contracted, not hoped for.

The three structures differ in one thing: how the electrons get from the plant to the buyer — physically, through the grid, or not at all.

On-site / behind-the-meter physical PPA

The plant sits on the customer's own roof or land and is wired behind the meter — inside their connection, so the power is consumed before it ever reaches the public grid. The customer buys those kWh directly from the developer at the PPA price.

Because the energy never touches the grid, it avoids network charges and regulated levies — the biggest single saving available. The catch is scale: you are limited by how much roof or land the site has and how much the site can actually consume on the spot.

Key term · On-site physical PPA

Generation built on the offtaker's premises and connected behind their meter. They buy the physical kWh directly, offsetting grid power at the full retail tariff including network charges. Biggest saving per unit, smallest possible volume — capped by the site's own roof, land and demand.

Sleeved PPA

What if the customer wants renewable power but has no suitable roof, or wants more than their site can hold? A sleeved PPA solves it: the plant is built off-site, and a utility "sleeves" the generation through the public grid to the customer's meter — handling the imbalances, the metering and the supply licence in between, for a fee.

The customer still takes physical renewable power, just delivered over the grid rather than over a private wire. The trade-off: because it travels on the public network, it does pay network charges, so the saving per unit is smaller than on-site — but you can contract far larger volumes.

Key term · Sleeved PPA

An off-site plant whose output is delivered to the offtaker through the grid, with a utility (the "sleeve") managing balancing, metering and supply for a fee. Physical renewable delivery without needing the customer's own roof — larger scale, but network charges still apply.

Virtual / financial PPA — a CfD

The third structure moves no electrons at all. A virtual PPA (also called a financial PPA) is a pure financial hedge built as a CfD — Contract for Difference. The customer keeps buying its physical power from the grid as before. Separately, it and the developer settle the difference between the market price and an agreed strike price.

If the market price is below the strike, the offtaker tops the developer up; if it is above, the developer pays the offtaker back. The net effect: the customer has locked in a fixed effective price without changing a single physical connection. It also receives the Guarantees of Origin (GOs) — the tradable certificates that prove the power was renewable — so it can claim clean energy and cut its Scope 2 emissions.

Key term · Virtual PPA / CfD (Contract for Difference)

A purely financial contract: no physical power changes hands. The two parties settle the gap between the live market price and a fixed strike price, so the offtaker locks in a price and collects the Guarantees of Origin. Works anywhere, at any scale, with no grid or wiring changes — but delivers no network-charge saving, because the customer still buys physical power from the grid.

Trade-offs at a glance

Same goal — fixed-price renewable power — three structures, three trade-offs. The choice falls out of what the customer has and what they want.

Choosing the structure
StructureSavingScaleComplexity
On-site physical (BTM)HighestSmallestLow
SleevedMediumLargeMedium
Virtual / financial (CfD)Price hedge onlyAnyHigher (financial)

Read it as a decision tree. Has a big roof and wants the deepest saving? On-site physical. Wants real green power but no roof, at volume? Sleeved. A multi-site corporate that mainly wants a fixed price and a clean-energy claim? Virtual CfD.

None of these is "best" in the abstract. The right structure is the one that matches the asset the customer brings to the table.

At EU level

The three structures are the same everywhere — but each rides its national rails, all under one EU frame. The Electricity Market Design reform (in force 16 Jul 2024) actively promotes PPAs and two-way CfDs; two-way CfDs become mandatory for new direct price-support schemes concluded on or after 17 Jul 2027 — a structural tailwind for the PPA business.

In the room

How you steer a customer to the right structure without overwhelming them:

Sales script

There are really only three ways to do this, and they all land in the same place: a fixed price you can sign and forget. Got the roof? We put panels on it and you buy the power directly — the deepest saving, because it skips the grid charges. No roof but you want real green power? We build off-site and sleeve it to you through the grid. And a multi-site group that just wants a locked price and a clean-energy claim can have it as a pure financial hedge — no panels, no wiring, just the contract. You don't pick the structure; your situation does.

Objections you will hear

A virtual PPA sounds like a paper trick — am I actually getting green power?
Rebuttal

You get two things that are very real: a locked-in price, and the Guarantees of Origin that legally back your renewable claim and cut your Scope 2 reporting. Yes, the electrons in your wires don't change — but your contract is what gets a new renewable plant built, and you hold the certificates that prove it. That's not a workaround; it's exactly how most large corporates buy clean power today.

Why would I sleeve through a utility and pay their fee — isn't on-site cheaper?
Rebuttal

On-site is cheaper per unit — when you actually have the roof and the demand to soak it up. When you don't, sleeving is what lets you contract a full-scale off-site plant and still take physical renewable power, even ten times more than your site could ever hold. The sleeve fee is a rounding error next to the volume it unlocks.

Fifteen years is a long time to commit to one price.
Rebuttal

It is — and that length is precisely the value. You're trading a grid bill that's re-priced every single day on the spot market for one fixed number you can drop into a budget and forget for a decade. That same term is what makes the plant financeable, which is the only reason we can offer you a price below the grid at all — and if you want room to manoeuvre, we size the contract to cover only part of your load.

Knowledge check
Which structure delivers the biggest saving per unit, and why?

On-site behind-the-meter generation never touches the grid, so it dodges network charges — the deepest saving, but capped by the site's roof and demand.

In a virtual PPA / CfD, what actually changes hands?

A virtual PPA is a pure financial hedge: settle the gap to the strike price, hand over the GOs, leave the physical supply untouched.

A multi-site retailer wants a fixed power price and a renewable claim, but has no usable roofs. Which do you pitch?

No roof, multi-site, mainly wants price certainty and a clean claim — that is the textbook case for a virtual CfD.
