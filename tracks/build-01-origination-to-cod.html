Learning objective

By the end you can narrate, stage by stage, how a signed offtake contract becomes a generating asset — naming the six phases from origination to the commercial operation date (COD) and the key milestone that ends each one.

A contract is not a plant

When you close a deal in Track 04, you have a signed offtake contract — a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) in which a customer promises to buy power for, say, fifteen years. That document is enormously valuable, but it generates nothing. Nobody has dug a hole, bought a panel, or connected a wire.

Everything between that signature and the first kilowatt-hour sold is called project development and construction. It is where the asset is actually created — and, as the next lessons show, where most of the margin is made. A salesperson who can describe this journey credibly turns "trust me" into "here is exactly what happens next."

Think of the whole thing as a relay race with six legs. Each leg removes a specific risk, and the project only has value because someone is willing to run all six.

The six stages, plainly

The lifecycle from idea to operating asset moves through six phases. The names vary by firm; the logic does not.

1 · Origination. Finding the opportunity — a factory with a big roof, a landowner near a substation, a corporate that wants clean power. It ends when both sides sign a deal: a Letter of Intent, a heads-of-terms, or the PPA itself.

2 · Development. The unglamorous heart of the business. You secure site control (a lease or option on the land), apply for a grid connection, obtain permits, and finalise the design. The output is a project that is "ready to build" (RTB) — every approval in hand, nothing left to stop a shovel.

3 · Financing. With permits and offtake locked, the project is bankable. You raise the money — typically a mix of equity (the developer's cash) and debt (a bank loan secured against the contracted cash flows). It ends at financial close: the moment funds are committed and can be drawn.

4 · Construction (EPC). A contractor engineers, procures and builds the plant under an EPC contract (Engineering, Procurement & Construction). For a mid-size solar farm this is months, not years.

5 · Commissioning. The plant is finished but not yet trusted. Engineers test every string, inverter and protection relay, energise the connection, and prove it performs to spec. This ends at grid energisation and successful testing.

6 · Commercial operation (COD). The commercial operation date is the formal day the asset starts selling power under the PPA and earning revenue. From COD the project is an operating asset — bond-like, contracted, and saleable to a fund.

Key term · COD (commercial operation date)

The contractual milestone on which a plant is declared fully operational and begins delivering power and invoicing under its PPA. It is the finish line of development and the starting line of the asset's earning life — and the reference date for warranties, performance guarantees and most exit valuations.

Key term · Ready-to-build (RTB)

A project that has cleared development — site secured, grid connection offer accepted, permits granted, design finalised — so that construction can begin without further approvals. RTB is the single biggest value step before COD, because nearly all the "will this ever happen?" risk has been removed.

What "EPC" actually means

You will hear EPC constantly, so own it. Engineering, Procurement & Construction is a single wrapped contract under which one contractor takes responsibility for designing the plant, buying all the equipment, and building it — usually for a fixed price and a fixed date.

The appeal is risk transfer. A turnkey EPC means the developer hands over a scope and gets back a working plant; cost overruns and delays are the contractor's problem, backed by liquidated damages (pre-agreed penalties for being late or under-performing). That single contract is what makes the project financeable — a bank wants one accountable party, not twenty subcontractors.

Note the shape: development and financing eat most of the calendar, yet they involve almost no spending on steel. The slow, cheap, risky part is paperwork; the fast, expensive, low-risk part is building. That asymmetry is the whole reason the development premium exists.

The milestones that matter

Three dates do all the heavy lifting in conversations with customers, lenders and buyers:

  • Ready-to-build (RTB) — the project is fully consented. Risk drops sharply; this is where a developer can first sell the project on at a profit.
  • Financial close (FC) — the money is committed. After FC, construction can actually start.
  • Commercial operation date (COD) — revenue begins and the asset becomes a sellable, bond-like cash stream. Everything in Track 06 (the exit) is dated from here.

If you remember only one sentence: origination signs the promise, development de-risks it, construction builds it, and COD turns it into money.

In the room

How you reassure a factory owner who has just signed and is wondering what they actually committed to:

Sales script

Signing today doesn't put panels on your roof tomorrow — and that's normal. We now run a two-to-three-year process: we lock the land and the grid connection, get the permits, raise the finance, then a single contractor designs, supplies and builds the plant for a fixed price and date. The day it's tested and switched on is the commercial operation date — that's when you start paying for clean power and not a day before. You carry none of the construction risk; we do.

Objections you will hear

Two to three years? That's forever — why so slow?
Rebuttal

Building the plant is fast — under a year. The time goes into the grid connection and permits, which are out of anyone's hands to rush. We start those on day one precisely so the slow clock runs in the background while you keep operating exactly as you do now. And you pay nothing until the power flows.

What if the project never gets built?
Rebuttal

Then you've lost nothing — the PPA only charges you for delivered power, so no plant means no bill. We carry the development cost and risk. The contract is structured so that the burden of getting to commercial operation sits entirely with us, not you.

Who's actually building this — you?
Rebuttal

A specialist EPC contractor builds it under a fixed-price, fixed-date turnkey contract with penalties for delay or under-performance. We manage them; the bank funding the plant insists on exactly that structure, which is your assurance the build is professionally backed, not improvised.
