Developer economics: IRR, DSCR, debt

The customer cares about their bill. The investor cares about four letters: IRR, NPV, DSCR and the gearing. This lesson takes the same 5 MW plant and looks at it from the money side — how it gets financed, what the lender demands, and why borrowing makes the equity work harder.

Learning objective

By the end you can walk an investment committee through a project's financing: define IRR, NPV, DSCR and gearing, size the debt a lender will lend, and explain why cheap debt lifts the equity IRR well above the unlevered project return.

Start with capex and the cash flow

Same plant as the savings lesson, now seen by the investor. Capex — capital expenditure, the all-in build cost — is the money out the door on day one. Against it sits the project's annual net cash flow: revenue from selling the power, minus operating costs. Everything in this lesson is built on those two figures.

The 5 MW plant — capex and steady-state cash flow
Capacity5 MWp
Capex (≈ €600 / kWp)€3,000,000
Annual generation (≈ 1,050 kWh/kWp)5,250 MWh
Revenue @ €75 / MWh PPA€393,750
Operating cost (O&M, lease, insurance)−€43,750
Net cash flow per year (CFADS)€350,000

This €350k of yearly cash is the raw material every financing metric is cut from. In project finance it is called CFADS — Cash Flow Available for Debt Service.

Project IRR and NPV

Two ways to judge whether €3.0M for €350k/yr over 25 years is a good deal:

NPV (Net Present Value) discounts every future cash flow back to today at a chosen discount rate and subtracts the capex. Positive NPV means the project clears that required return; negative means it does not. (We built the discounting machinery in the LCOE lesson — same engine.)

IRR (Internal Rate of Return) flips the question around. Instead of picking a rate, it solves for the rate at which NPV equals exactly zero — the project's own annualised return. Here, €3.0M in and €350k/yr out for 25 years solves to an IRR of roughly 11%. That is the unlevered project IRR: the return on the whole project assuming you paid all cash, no borrowing.

Key term · IRR & NPV

NPV = sum of discounted cash flows minus capex; positive = beats your hurdle. IRR = the discount rate that makes NPV zero, i.e. the project's intrinsic annual return. Compare IRR to your hurdle rate (the minimum you'll accept); a 11% IRR against a 10% hurdle is a go.

Debt, DSCR and gearing

Nobody funds these with all equity — that wastes the cheapest money available. You borrow most of the capex as project-finance debt secured on the asset's cash flows, and put up equity for the rest. The split has a name: gearing (or leverage) — the percentage of capex funded by debt. Solar typically gears 70–80%.

The lender's whole world is one ratio: the DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) — the project's annual cash (CFADS) divided by its annual debt service (interest + principal repayment). It measures how comfortably the cash covers the loan. Lenders to contracted renewables want a DSCR of about 1.2–1.4×: at 1.3×, every €1.00 of debt payment is backed by €1.30 of cash, a 30% safety buffer before a missed payment.

DSCR = CFADS ÷ Annual debt service  ·  lenders want ≈ 1.2–1.4×

The DSCR also sizes the debt. The lender takes your CFADS, divides by their required DSCR to get the maximum debt service they'll allow, then works back — given the interest rate and loan tenor — to the largest loan that service can support. Cash flow sets the loan; the loan sets the equity.

Key term · DSCR & gearing

Gearing = debt ÷ total capex (how much is borrowed). DSCR = cash available ÷ debt service (how safely the loan is covered). Higher gearing lifts equity returns but lowers DSCR — push gearing up until DSCR hits the lender's floor of ~1.2–1.4×, and no further.

Equity returns and why leverage helps

The equity IRR is the return to your money — the slice left after the bank is paid. Here is the key insight: if the project earns 11% and the debt costs only ~5%, every euro you borrow earns 11% but only costs 5%. That 6-point spread accrues to equity. This is positive leverage: cheap debt below the project return drags the equity IRR up, typically into the mid-to-high teens on a 70%-geared solar asset.

The trade-off: leverage cuts both ways. More debt = higher equity returns and higher risk if cash flows disappoint, because debt service is fixed and must be paid first. The DSCR floor is the lender's guardrail against you gearing into fragility.

Worked example — financing the 5 MW plant

Take the €350k CFADS and gear it 70% with cheap, long debt:

Debt sizing, DSCR and the equity cheque
Capex€3,000,000
Gearing (debt share of capex)70%
Debt drawn (70% × €3.0M)€2,100,000
Equity (the remaining 30%)€900,000
Debt service @ 5%, 15-yr amortisation≈ €202,000 / yr
CFADS€350,000 / yr
DSCR (€350k ÷ €202k)≈ 1.73×
Cash to equity after debt service≈ €148,000 / yr

DSCR of 1.73× sits comfortably above the ~1.35× a lender requires — there is headroom to gear higher if you want more leverage. Equity puts in €900k and collects ~€148k/yr for 15 years, then the full €350k once the loan is repaid. That lifts the equity IRR into the high teens, well above the 11% unlevered project IRR. That gap is the work the debt is doing.

In the room

How you brief an investor weighing whether to back the equity:

Sales script

The project earns about eleven percent all-cash — solid for contracted solar. But we don't fund it all-cash. We borrow seventy percent at five against a fifteen-year offtake, and the bank is comfortable because the cash covers their payment one-point-seven times over. That cheap debt does the heavy lifting: your nine hundred thousand of equity targets the high teens, not eleven. Same plant, better return, because the leverage is positive.

Objections you will hear

An 11% project IRR isn't exciting — funds want more.
Rebuttal

Eleven is the unlevered number — the project on all cash. Nobody holds it that way. Gear it 70% with 5% debt and the equity IRR moves into the high teens, because every borrowed euro earns 11 and costs 5. The 11% is the foundation; the leverage is the building.

A 1.7× DSCR means you've under-borrowed — that's lazy.
Rebuttal

It's a deliberate margin of safety, and yes, there's room to gear higher toward the 1.35× lenders accept — which we can flex if equity wants more leverage. We start conservative so a bad solar year never threatens debt service. You can dial gearing up; you can't dial a covenant breach back down.

If rates rise, your cheap debt disappears and the model breaks.
Rebuttal

Which is why we fix the rate and the tenor at financial close, sized against a contracted PPA — the debt service is locked for 15 years regardless of what the central bank does next. Rising rates hit the next project's pricing, not this one's covenants. The DSCR we underwrite to is the rate we've already secured.

Knowledge check
What does the IRR of a project represent?

IRR solves for the rate that zeroes NPV. Compare it to your hurdle rate to decide go / no-go.

A lender requires DSCR ≥ 1.3×. What does that mean?

DSCR = CFADS ÷ debt service. 1.3× means €1.30 of cash backs every €1.00 of payment. It also caps how much can be borrowed.

Why does adding 70% debt at 5% raise the equity IRR above the 11% project IRR?

When debt costs less than the project return, leverage lifts equity returns — at the price of higher risk if cash flows fall.
