BESS revenue stacking

A battery that earns from only one market is a battery that doesn't pay for itself. The trick is stacking — letting the same megawatts collect from three pockets at once. This lesson builds the stack, tests how fragile it is, and lands the payback.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain revenue stacking — how a battery earns arbitrage, capacity and ancillary income simultaneously — show which pocket dominates, and pressure-test the payback against the two inputs that move it most: spread and cycles.

Why a battery has to stack

A solar plant has one job: generate and sell. A BESS (Battery Energy Storage System) has no fuel and no output of its own — it only moves energy through time. On any single revenue stream the economics are thin. The business case only closes when the same asset earns from several markets that don't conflict. That is revenue stacking.

Two numbers define the battery before any revenue: its power (MW — how fast it can charge or discharge) and its duration (hours at full power). A "5 MW / 2h" battery holds 10 MWh of usable energy. Power wins capacity and ancillary income; energy (power × duration) drives arbitrage. And nothing is free: Li-ion round-trip efficiency is ~86%, so 100 MWh in returns ~86 MWh out — the 14% loss is baked into the arbitrage maths.

Key term · Revenue stacking

Earning from multiple, non-conflicting markets with one asset: energy arbitrage + capacity payments + ancillary services. No single stream justifies the capex; the stack does. The art is dispatching so the streams complement rather than cannibalise each other.

The three pockets

Each layer of the stack pays for a different service the battery provides to the grid:

  • Energy arbitrage — buy cheap, sell dear. Charge when power is cheap (midday solar glut), discharge into the expensive evening peak. The earnings scale with the spread (€/MWh price gap) and how many times a year you do it (cycles), net of the 86% efficiency.
  • Capacity payments — a fixed €/MW/yr for simply being available to the grid at peak, whether or not you're called. Paid on power, not energy. Order of magnitude €30,000/MW/yr in many EU markets — predictable, contracted, the floor of the stack.
  • Ancillary services — fast grid-balancing, chiefly FCR (Frequency Containment Reserve): the battery injects or absorbs power in milliseconds to hold grid frequency at 50 Hz. Paid on power availability, ≈ €15,000/MW/yr. High-value, but the market is shallow and prices fall as more batteries enter.
Key term · Arbitrage spread & FCR

Spread = the €/MWh gap between the cheap charging price and the rich discharging price — the lifeblood of arbitrage, and the most volatile input. FCR = Frequency Containment Reserve, the fastest ancillary product; the battery's sub-second response is worth a premium, but the pot is small and crowds quickly.

Sensitivity — spread and cycles rule the arbitrage

Capacity and ancillary income are fixed €/MW figures: steady but capped. The variable, and usually the swing factor, is arbitrage, which is a straight product:

Arbitrage = Energy (MWh) × Cycles × Spread (€/MWh) × Round-trip efficiency (0.86)

Because it is multiplicative, arbitrage scales linearly with both spread and cycles — double either and you double that pocket. On our 5 MW / 2h battery, dropping the spread from €60 to €40/MWh cuts arbitrage by a third and stretches payback by well over a year; pushing cycles from 365 to 250 does similar damage. This is why a battery business case lives or dies on a volatile market: arbitrage is the upside and the fragility. Capacity and ancillary are the ballast that keep the stack standing when spreads compress.

Worked example — a 5 MW / 2h Czech battery

10 MWh usable, cycling daily, with mid-range market values:

Usable energy (5 MW × 2 h)10 MWh
Arbitrage (10 MWh × 365 cycles × €60 × 0.86)€188,340
Capacity (5 MW × €30,000/MW)€150,000
Ancillary / FCR (5 MW × €15,000/MW)€75,000

Capex ≈ €3,500,000 (10 MWh × 1,000 × €350/kWh). Simple payback = €3.5M ÷ €413k ≈ 8.5 years. Notice the shape of the stack: arbitrage is the biggest single slice but under half the total — the fixed capacity and ancillary layers (€225k combined) are what make the case bankable when spreads are weak.

In the room

How you frame the battery to an investor who only knows solar:

Sales script

A battery doesn't make power — it makes money from three places at once. It buys cheap midday solar and sells it into the evening peak, it gets paid just to be available, and it earns a premium for steadying the grid in milliseconds. Stack those and a five-megawatt unit clears north of four hundred thousand a year. The arbitrage moves with the market; the capacity and grid-services payments are the contracted floor underneath it.

Objections you will hear

Arbitrage spreads are volatile — your revenue is a guess.
Rebuttal

The arbitrage layer is volatile, yes — that's why we don't lean the whole case on it. Capacity and ancillary are contracted €/MW payments, about €225k a year here, independent of spread. They're the floor that keeps the asset bankable; arbitrage is upside on top. Drag the spread to €40 in the tool — the battery still earns through it.

An 8.5-year payback is too long for a battery that degrades.
Rebuttal

That's simple payback at today's mid-range spread — deliberately conservative. Push the spread up, add cycles, or factor the falling capex (storage is €250–450/kWh and dropping) and it shortens fast. And degradation is managed by cycle limits the revenue model already respects. It's a 15-year-plus asset paying back in eight.

If everyone builds batteries, the ancillary prices collapse.
Rebuttal

True for FCR — it's a shallow pot that saturates, which is exactly why we treat it as the smallest, most cautious layer, not the thesis. As ancillary compresses, the bigger arbitrage opportunity grows, because more renewables means more volatility to trade. The stack rotates; the asset stays useful. We model FCR decaying, not holding.

Knowledge check
What does "revenue stacking" mean for a battery?

One asset, multiple complementary revenue streams. The stack — not any single pocket — is what makes a BESS pay.

Which revenue layer is most sensitive to market conditions?

Arbitrage = energy × cycles × spread × efficiency. Spread is the most volatile input; capacity and ancillary are fixed €/MW floors.

On the 5 MW / 2h example (€3.5M capex), why is simple payback ≈ 8.5 years?

Payback = capex ÷ total annual revenue. The whole stack (€413k), not any one layer, drives it — and a richer spread shortens it.
