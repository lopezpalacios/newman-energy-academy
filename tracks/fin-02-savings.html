Customer savings: tariff vs PPA

The whole pitch reduces to one subtraction the customer can do in their head: what they pay the grid, minus what they'd pay you, on the slice of load you can cover. Get this number right and defensible, and the rest of the sale is logistics.

Learning objective

By the end you can size a customer's saving live — covered load × (tariff − PPA) — and convert it into the three numbers a CFO actually asks for: the percentage off, the payback on any capital, and the ten-year value.

The anatomy of a saving

A customer's saving is not magic and it is not a discount you negotiate. It is arithmetic on three known numbers: how much power they use, the price the grid charges, and the price you can offer under a PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) — a contract that fixes a €/MWh price for, typically, 5–15 years.

The core saving is simply the price gap times the energy it applies to:

Annual saving = Covered load (MWh) × ( Grid tariff − PPA price ) (€/MWh)

An industrial customer in Czechia might pay an all-in grid tariff around €120/MWh. A behind-the-meter solar PPA might be priced at €80/MWh — above your ~€60 LCOE (that gap is your margin), still €40 under the grid. That €40/MWh gap, multiplied by real volume, is the entire value proposition.

Key term · PPA price vs tariff gap

The customer's saving per unit is tariff − PPA. The grid tariff is what they pay today (energy + network + levies + supplier margin); the PPA price is your contracted offer. The gap, not the absolute PPA price, is what you sell. A €40/MWh gap on heavy load is a six-figure annual cheque.

Covered load — the number people get wrong

You almost never cover 100% of a customer's consumption. Rooftop solar only generates when the sun shines, and only up to the roof's size. The slice you can actually supply is the covered load — also called the coverage or solar fraction.

If a factory uses 5,000 MWh/yr and your system can realistically serve 60% of it, your covered load is 3,000 MWh. The other 40% still comes from the grid at the old price — and that is fine. The saving only ever applies to the covered slice. Quoting savings on total load is the fastest way to lose credibility with a CFO who can divide.

Coverage rises with battery storage (shift solar into the evening) and falls for night-shift or 24/7 loads. Size it honestly; it is the multiplier on everything.

Three numbers a CFO asks for

The raw euro saving is the start. Turn it into the metrics that get a deal signed:

  • Percentage saved — saving ÷ the covered bill. With a €120 tariff and €80 PPA, that is 40/120 = 33% off the covered portion. CFOs remember a percentage; it survives the meeting.
  • Payback — only relevant if the customer puts up capital. Under an EaaS / no-capex PPA there is no customer outlay, so payback is immediate (day one, they pay less). If they self-fund the solar, payback = their capex ÷ annual saving — typically 5–8 years on C&I rooftop.
  • Ten-year value — annual saving × 10, a conservative floor that ignores tariff inflation. If the grid price rises (and gas-set markets are volatile), the real number is larger — but quote the floor and let the upside be a pleasant surprise.
Key term · Payback period

The time for cumulative savings to repay the capital the customer invested: customer capex ÷ annual saving. Under a no-capex PPA the customer invests nothing, so they are cash-positive from month one — there is no payback to wait for, which is the heart of the EaaS pitch.

Worked example — a 5,000 MWh/yr Czech factory

A mid-size manufacturer on the grid. We cover 60% of its load with an €80/MWh PPA:

Sizing the saving
Annual consumption5,000 MWh
Share covered by PPA60%
Covered load (5,000 × 60%)3,000 MWh
Grid tariff€120 / MWh
PPA price€80 / MWh
Old cost on covered load (3,000 × €120)€360,000
New PPA cost on covered load (3,000 × €80)€240,000
Annual saving (3,000 × €40)€120,000

That is 33% off the covered bill and, at a flat €120k/yr, a €1.2M ten-year saving on a no-capex contract — before any grid-price inflation. The customer spends nothing up front and is cash-positive from the first invoice.

In the room

How you land the saving with a plant manager who is watching the clock:

Sales script

You use five thousand megawatt-hours a year. We can cover about sixty percent of that from your own roof at eighty euros instead of the hundred and twenty you pay the grid. That is forty euros off three thousand megawatt-hours — a hundred and twenty thousand a year, every year, with nothing to pay up front. Over ten years that's one-point-two million, and that assumes the grid price never rises. It will.

Objections you will hear

You can't cover all my load, so the saving is overstated.
Rebuttal

Correct — and we never claim it. The €120k is on the 60% we actually supply; the rest stays on the grid at today's price, unchanged. We size coverage from your real load profile, and we can lift it with storage. The saving is on the covered slice only — that honesty is exactly why the number holds up.

What if the grid price falls below your PPA?
Rebuttal

In a gas-set market that has run €60–110 since 2022 and structurally above the old €40–50, that's an unlikely floor — but it's your call on the gap. We can set the PPA with a modest discount-to-grid collar so you always pay under the grid. You're buying price certainty; we just make sure certainty is also cheaper.

Ten-year value assumes prices stay flat — that's a guess.
Rebuttal

It's the opposite of a guess — it's the conservative floor. We deliberately hold the grid tariff flat so the €1.2M needs no optimism to be true. Every euro the grid rises is upside we didn't promise. Your PPA price, meanwhile, is fixed and known. We sell the floor and let the market hand you the rest.

Knowledge check
A factory uses 5,000 MWh/yr, you cover 60% at €80 vs a €120 grid tariff. The annual saving is…

Saving = covered load (3,000 MWh) × (tariff − PPA) = 3,000 × €40 = €120,000. Always on the covered slice.

Under a no-capex PPA, what is the customer's payback period?

Payback = customer capex ÷ saving. With zero customer capex there is nothing to pay back — the core of the EaaS pitch.

Why quote the ten-year saving with the grid tariff held flat?

Hold the tariff flat and the saving needs no optimism. In a volatile gas-set market, inflation is upside you didn't have to promise.
