Learning objective

By the end you can explain why two rational parties value the identical asset differently, list the four things that move a buyer's required yield, and stress-test the compression premium instead of quoting it as a certainty.

Two parties, two costs of capital

The premium is not a trick and it is not luck. It comes from a simple fact: a developer and an infrastructure fund do not have the same cost of money.

The cost of capital is the blended return a business must earn to satisfy everyone who funded it — its lenders (who want interest) and its equity backers (who want a return for the risk they carry). A developer's backers are taking early-stage, binary risk: a grid connection that might not come, a permit that might be refused, a contractor that might overrun. They price that risk high — they want 10–13% or they put their money elsewhere.

A pension or infrastructure fund is the opposite kind of money. It manages decades-long liabilities and wants long, boring, inflation-linked cash. An operating solar plant with a signed 15-year offtake is almost exactly that. The risky part — building it — is already done. So the fund is content with 6–8%, and it can pay a price that delivers only that return.

Discount the same cash flows at 7% instead of 11% and the present value jumps. The four-point gap between the two costs of capital, applied to a long cash-flow stream, is the entire exit premium.

Key term · Cost of capital

The minimum blended return an investor requires to put money into an asset, given its risk. Higher perceived risk → higher required return → lower price they will pay. Yield compression is just the buyer's cost of capital being lower than yours.

What actually sets the buyer's required yield

Funds do not pull 7% out of the air. The required yield is built up from a base rate plus a stack of risk premia. Four levers move it most, and you should know each because every one is a negotiation point in a sale.

  • The risk-free rate. Government bond yields are the floor. When central-bank rates rose in 2022–23, the 10-year benchmark moved several points, and required yields on every infrastructure asset moved up with it. Rates are the tide that lifts or sinks all valuations.
  • Contract quality & counterparty credit. A 15-year PPA with an investment-grade industrial offtaker is worth far more than three years left on a deal with an unrated SME. The longer and more creditworthy the contracted cash, the lower the yield a buyer accepts.
  • Merchant (price) exposure. Cash that depends on the volatile OTE spot market — not a fixed PPA — is risky, so buyers discount it harder. The share of revenue that is contracted versus merchant is one of the biggest single drivers of the yield.
  • Technology, scale & track record. Proven solar at scale, with a clean operating history and a credible O&M provider, prices tighter than a first-of-its-kind battery or a sub-scale one-off.
Key term · Required yield

The return a buyer demands on an operating asset = risk-free rate + premia for contract length, counterparty credit, merchant exposure and technology risk. Lower it by one point on a 25-year asset and the price moves materially — which is why de-risking before you sell pays for itself.

Worked example — what one point of yield is worth

Back to the 5 MW Brno plant: €350,000 of net cash a year, 25 years of life remaining. Watch how sensitive the sale price is to the buyer's required yield — this is the single most important table in the whole track.

Read the last row carefully: priced at your own 11% hurdle, the asset is worth roughly what it cost — there is no premium without a lower-cost buyer. The whole exit lives in the gap between rows. Moving from 8% to 6% — two points — is worth about €0.7M on a single 5 MW plant.

These are illustrative orders of magnitude, but the lesson is exact: you do not just hand a fund a plant. You hand it a de-risked, well-contracted plant, because every point of yield you compress is six figures of price.

In the room

How you frame compression to an investor who worries the premium is fragile:

Sales script

The price isn't a hope — it's mechanical, and I can show you the maths. A fund buys our cash flow at its own cost of capital, six to eight percent, because the building risk is already behind it; ours was eleven. Every point we shave off the buyer's yield is worth roughly three-fifty thousand on a plant this size — so we don't just build it, we sign a long, investment-grade offtake and run it clean for a year first. That's what turns an eleven-percent project into a seven-percent asset a fund is glad to own.

Objections you will hear

Rates are higher now — hasn't compression disappeared?
Rebuttal

It narrowed, it didn't vanish — and we underwrite to a conservative exit yield precisely so a higher-rate world is already in our base case. Higher rates lifted our own build hurdle too, so the gap between developer and fund cost of capital is still there; funds remain structurally cheaper money for de-risked assets. Any tightening from here is upside we haven't promised.

Why would a fund pay you a premium instead of building its own?
Rebuttal

Because development risk is not what they're paid to take — their investors want steady operating yield, not permitting lottery tickets. Buying a finished, contracted asset is cheaper and faster for them than standing up a development team and carrying the risk for years. We're the specialist supplier; the premium is simply our margin for absorbing the risk they don't want.

How do I know the yield you're assuming is realistic?
Rebuttal

We benchmark it against recent operating-asset transactions in the same market and technology, then deliberately model the exit one to two points wide of the best comparable. Push the yield yourself in the calculator — at any reasonable number the premium stays positive, because our build hurdle is genuinely higher than a pension fund's. We'd rather show you the conservative case than sell you the optimistic one.

Knowledge check
Fundamentally, why can a fund accept 7% where you needed 11% on the same asset?

Same cash, two different costs of capital. The fund's lower required return is what lets it pay more.

Which change would most reduce the buyer's required yield (and so raise the price)?

Longer, higher-credit contracted cash is exactly what compresses yield. Merchant exposure does the opposite.

In the worked example, what happens to the premium if the asset is priced at your own 11% hurdle?

No yield gap, no premium. The whole exit lives in the spread between your hurdle and the buyer's.
