Selling a C&I deal in Canada

There is no "Canadian power market." Electricity is a provincial matter, so before you pitch anything you must ask one question: which province? Alberta runs a deregulated, energy-only market and is where almost every corporate renewable PPA in the country gets signed. Ontario procures supply through a public operator. Quebec, BC and Manitoba already sell cheap government hydro. Same product, ten different rulebooks — here is how to read them.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain why Canada has no single national power market — that electricity is provincial, with only a narrow federal layer (the CER) on top — and you can identify the right operator and market type for a given province, explain why corporate PPAs cluster in Alberta, and name the federal carbon and ITC levers that frame a Canadian renewable deal.

Why there is no single national market

This is the one idea that separates a Canadian deal from a European one. In the EU you start from a shared frame and localise down to the member state. In Canada you do the opposite: electricity is a provincial responsibility under the Constitution. Generation, the grid inside a province, and retail supply are all set province by province. There is no Canadian equivalent of a single wholesale market — each province has its own operator, its own regulator and its own market design.

The federal layer is real but narrow. The CER (Canada Energy Regulator) oversees only interprovincial and international power lines and pipelines — not the markets inside a province. Ottawa also sets economy-wide climate rules: the Clean Electricity Regulations (finalised December 2024, in force January 2025) aim to cap fossil-generation emissions toward a net-zero grid, with the binding limits only biting from 2035. Alberta and Saskatchewan are challenging them on jurisdictional grounds — which tells you exactly how guarded the provinces are about their turf. (Check current.)

So the salesperson's reflex is a question, not a pitch: which province? The answer changes the operator, the market type, and whether a corporate PPA even makes commercial sense.

Read this top to bottom before any Canadian meeting. The EU reflex "directive sets the frame, member state implements" becomes "federal climate frame, provincial market." Always ask which province first.

Alberta — Canada's corporate-PPA hub

Alberta is the outlier, and it is where your deals will be. It is the only large province with a fully deregulated, energy-only wholesale market, run by the AESO (Alberta Electric System Operator). "Energy-only" means generators are paid only for the energy they actually deliver into the pool — there is no separate capacity payment for merely being available. With nothing smoothing it, the pool price is volatile: high when supply is tight, low when it is loose.

Key term · Energy-only market

A wholesale market where generators earn revenue solely from the price of energy dispatched — no capacity payment for standing by. The upside is a clean, competitive price signal that rewards being available when the grid is short. The downside is price volatility: revenue swings with the pool. That volatility is precisely why a corporate PPA is attractive — it lets a buyer (and a developer) lock a fixed price and step out of the swing. Contrast Ontario, which layers a capacity payment on top to pay for availability.

Because a buyer can contract directly with a developer against that volatile pool, Alberta has become the natural home of the corporate PPA. Through roughly 2025, around 95% of all new renewable build in Canada has been in Alberta (Rystad). The offtakers are blue-chip: Amazon, RBC, IKEA, TELUS and heavy industrial emitters have all signed Alberta wind and solar PPAs — Amazon's ~415 MW Buffalo Plains wind farm was among the largest onshore in Canada at announcement. (Check current.)

One live wrinkle to flag honestly: Alberta is mid-redesign. The Restructured Energy Market (REM) — adopted by Ministerial Order in March 2026, with phased rollout beginning 2027 — keeps the energy-only foundation but adds scarcity pricing, locational marginal pricing (LMP) and new reserve products. It is not a switch to a capacity market, but the transition has created genuine investment uncertainty, and a sophisticated counterparty will know that. (Check current — this is live.)

Ontario's procurements & the hydro provinces

Ontario works differently. Its operator, the IESO (Independent Electricity System Operator), runs a capacity auction plus periodic long-term competitive procurements (RFPs). The recent LT1 round was the largest battery-storage procurement in Canadian history, and the ongoing LT2 RFP has procured further thousands of megawatts of energy and capacity — heavily weighted to storage (BESS) — on 20-year contracts. The practical consequence for a salesperson: in Ontario new supply is mostly contracted through the operator, not via a free-running merchant corporate PPA, so you compete into RFPs rather than negotiate bilaterally. (Check current.)

Across the provinces

Quebec (Hydro-Québec), British Columbia (BC Hydro) and Manitoba (Manitoba Hydro) are a different world again: Crown (government-owned) utilities sitting on abundant, cheap hydropower. Regulated retail rates are already low, so there is far less room for a merchant PPA to beat the tariff. Hydro is dominant nationally — roughly 60% of Canadian generation. In these provinces the conversation is about new clean load and growth, not undercutting an expensive grid. (Check current.)

Where C&I PPAs actually happen

Put the three province types together and the map of opportunity is clear: corporate C&I PPAs in Canada happen mostly in Alberta. It is the one large market where a deregulated, energy-only pool lets a corporate buyer sign a fixed-price contract directly with a developer to hedge that volatility — exactly the structure your European playbook already teaches.

Ontario adds a second, narrower channel: you win supply by competing into IESO procurements rather than negotiating a bilateral PPA. And in the hydro provinces the merchant PPA is largely beside the point — the Crown utility is already the low-cost supplier. So when a prospect says "we want a Canadian renewable PPA," your first job is to locate them on this map. If they are an Alberta load, you have a textbook deal. If they are in Quebec, you reframe to growth and decarbonisation, not savings.

Carbon: industrial pricing stays, the consumer charge ended

Canada has no EU ETS and no CBAM — its carbon system is its own, and it changed materially in 2025, so get this right. The federal approach is a benchmark that provinces must meet, with a federal backstop where they do not.

The point people get wrong: the consumer "fuel charge" — the retail carbon price on petrol, gas and home heating — was set to $0 effective 1 April 2025. For practical purposes the consumer carbon price has ended. (Check current.)

But industrial carbon pricing remains fully in force. Large emitters still pay through the federal OBPS (Output-Based Pricing System) or an equivalent provincial scheme — in Alberta that is TIER (Technology Innovation and Emissions Reduction). That is the lever that matters for a C&I pitch: a renewable PPA helps an industrial buyer cut the covered emissions, and the compliance cost, that industrial carbon pricing still imposes. (Check current — benchmark stringency is periodically reviewed.)

The funding stack — federal, not EU

Forget everything about Modernisation Funds and CEEAG state-aid caps — none of it applies here. Canada funds clean power through federal investment tax credits and two federal financing institutions:

  • Clean Technology ITC — a refundable ~30% tax credit for investments in wind, solar, stationary storage and low-carbon heat (qualifying expenditures roughly March 2023 → December 2034). This is the workhorse credit for a typical C&I generation or storage project.
  • Clean Electricity ITC — a refundable ~15% credit for low-emitting generation, storage and inter-provincial transmission, open to taxable corporations and Crown corporations. It became law in March 2026 (Bill C-15); a domestic-content requirement is under consultation. (Check current.)
  • Canada Growth Fund (CGF) — a ~$15bn federal fund that de-risks projects, notably through carbon contracts for difference that give a project price certainty on its carbon revenue.
  • Canada Infrastructure Bank (CIB) — a ~$35bn federal Crown bank providing low-cost debt and equity into clean power, storage and transmission.
Not the EU

Because Canada is outside the EU, there is no ETS, no CBAM, no RED III target and no Modernisation Fund — and no state-aid ceiling capping how pots stack. The frame is federal climate policy + federal ITCs, deployed into a provincial market. Lead with "which province," then layer the Clean Technology / Clean Electricity ITCs, CGF and CIB on top. (Programme details and credit rates — check current.)

In the room

How you open with an Alberta corporate or industrial prospect — leading with the one thing that makes Alberta different, then the carbon hook:

Sales script

The first thing to know about Canada is there's no national power market — electricity is provincial, and you're in the one province built for this. Alberta's market is deregulated and energy-only, so the pool price swings hard; a ten-to-fifteen-year PPA lets you step out of that swing and lock a fixed price per megawatt-hour, well below what you'd average paying the pool — the same contract Amazon, RBC and IKEA already signed here. The carbon piece still bites industrially too: the consumer fuel charge ended in 2025, but large-emitter pricing under OBPS and Alberta's TIER is very much alive, so clean supply cuts your compliance cost, and the federal Clean Technology tax credit covers about thirty percent of the build. Volatile market, fixed price, carbon hedge, federal credit — that's why the deals happen here.

Objections you will hear

We're a Canadian company — and Canada already has cheap hydro. Why would we sign a PPA?
Rebuttal

It depends entirely on which province you're in — and that's the whole point. In Quebec, BC or Manitoba you're right: those Crown utilities sell low-cost hydro and a merchant PPA rarely beats it, so there the conversation is about adding new clean capacity for growth. But your Alberta load doesn't run on hydro — it runs on a deregulated, energy-only pool where the price swings, so here a PPA isn't competing with cheap hydro, it's fixing a volatile market price. Tell me the province, and I'll tell you straight whether a PPA makes sense.

Alberta's pool price is wild and the market's being rebuilt. Isn't that too risky?
Rebuttal

The volatility is exactly why a PPA exists — a fixed-price contract is the cure for a swinging pool, not a victim of it; you lock your price per megawatt-hour for years and stop caring what the pool does day to day. On the redesign, the Restructured Energy Market keeps the energy-only foundation and adds scarcity and locational pricing, phasing in from 2027 — an enhancement, not a teardown. And the transition itself has created some investment uncertainty, which is one more reason to lock a contracted price now rather than ride the merchant pool through the change. (Check current — REM is live.)

Carbon pricing got cancelled and the tax credits keep changing — can I count on any of it?
Rebuttal

First, the economics of your PPA stand on the contracted price alone — the carbon savings and the credits are upside, never the foundation. And the headline misleads: what ended in April 2025 was only the consumer fuel charge, the price at the pump and on home heating, while industrial carbon pricing — the part that touches a facility like yours — is still fully in force through OBPS and Alberta's TIER. On the credits, the Clean Technology ITC at around thirty percent has been law since 2023 and runs to 2034, and the Clean Electricity ITC is now enacted too. (Check current.)

Knowledge check
Why is there no single national electricity market in Canada?

Generation, the in-province grid and retail supply are provincial. Ottawa's CER regulates only interprovincial/international transmission, and federal climate rules (the Clean Electricity Regulations) frame — but don't run — the provincial markets. Always ask which province first.

Why do most Canadian corporate renewable PPAs get signed in Alberta?

Alberta's AESO market is energy-only and competitive, so the pool price swings — and a bilateral PPA fixes that price. Ontario contracts supply through IESO procurements; the hydro provinces are Crown-supplied. That's why ~95% of new Canadian renewable build through 2025 was in Alberta.

What happened to Canadian carbon pricing in 2025?

The retail/consumer fuel charge ended in spring 2025 — a common stale point to get right. Large-emitter industrial pricing through the OBPS and provincial equivalents like Alberta's TIER continues, and it's the lever that gives a C&I PPA a carbon-compliance benefit. (Check current.)
