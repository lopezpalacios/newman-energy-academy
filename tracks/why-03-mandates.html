RED III, CBAM & mandatory demand

Volatility makes clean power attractive. Regulation makes it compulsory. This lesson shows how a stack of EU rules quietly converts "going green" from a marketing choice into a line item the board cannot avoid — which is the demand engine under everything you sell.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain how four pieces of EU regulation — RED III, CBAM, the EU ETS, and CSRD — turn clean power from a preference into a requirement, and use a carbon number to show a client that buying green is now cheaper than not.

RED III — the target that forces supply

RED III is the third Renewable Energy Directive (agreed 2023). It raises the EU's binding 2030 renewables target to roughly 42.5% of final energy consumption, with an aspiration of 45%. It also speeds up permitting and declares renewable projects to be of "overriding public interest" — legal language that pushes them past local objections faster.

Why a businessperson cares: RED III is a demand signal with the force of law. It commits every member state — including Czechia — to pull a defined volume of renewables onto the grid this decade. That mandate is what makes building solar and storage a market with a guaranteed buyer behind it, not a niche.

Key term · RED III

The EU Renewable Energy Directive (2023 revision). Binding 2030 renewables target ~42.5% (aim 45%), faster permitting, and "overriding public interest" status for renewables. The top-level rule that turns clean-energy growth into national obligations.

CBAM & the EU ETS — putting a price on carbon

The second force is a price on pollution. Under the EU ETS — Emissions Trading System, heavy industry must buy an allowance for each tonne of CO₂ it emits. That carbon price has often sat in the €60–90 per tonne range. So emitting is no longer free — it is a direct, growing cost line.

The newer companion rule is CBAM — the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism. CBAM puts a carbon cost on certain imports (steel, aluminium, cement, fertiliser and more) so that goods made in high-carbon countries can no longer undercut cleaner EU producers. Its definitive regime is phasing in from 2026. The flip side matters for your client: an EU exporter that cleans up its own power is better positioned as carbon accounting tightens, while dirtier rivals face the charge.

Key term · EU ETS & CBAM

EU ETS: a market where emitters buy allowances per tonne of CO₂ (often €60–90/t). CBAM: a carbon charge on high-carbon imports (definitive regime phasing in 2026+) so EU industry isn't undercut. Together they make carbon a real, rising cost — and clean power a hedge against it.

Scope 2, CSRD & Guarantees of Origin

The third force is disclosure. Scope 2 emissions are the emissions from the electricity a company purchases — for most manufacturers, a big share of their footprint. Under the CSRD — Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, large EU companies must now report those emissions to the board and the market. Once a number is reported, it becomes a target, and targets need solutions.

The cleanest solution is a renewable PPA bundled with Guarantees of Origin (GOs) — tradable certificates, each proving that 1 MWh was generated from renewables. Buy renewable power plus its GOs and you can legitimately cut your reported Scope 2 emissions toward zero. That is why corporate sustainability teams, not just procurement, are now in the room when a PPA is signed.

Key term · Scope 2 & Guarantees of Origin

Scope 2: emissions from purchased electricity — a board-level reporting liability under CSRD. Guarantees of Origin (GOs): certificates proving 1 MWh came from renewables. A PPA + GOs is how a company cuts and proves a lower Scope 2 number.

Worked example — the carbon cost of doing nothing

Take a Czech industrial site whose purchased grid power carries roughly 0.35 tonnes of CO₂ per MWh, consuming the same 5,000 MWh/year. Put a €75/tonne ETS-style carbon value on those emissions to see the exposure a clean PPA removes:

As ETS prices climb and CBAM bites, that avoided cost only grows. Figures illustrative — verify the client's actual grid factor and current ETS price.

At EU level

The demand you just costed is set in Brussels and enforced nationally. A Directive fixes the target; each member state transposes it into its own law. Two targets now bind: RED III (≥42.5% renewables by 2030) and — newly law — the 2040 target of a 90% net GHG cut vs 1990, adopted into the European Climate Law (in force ~April 2026). Carbon pricing tightens alongside: the EU ETS, CBAM (definitive regime from 2026) and the new EU ETS2 for buildings, transport and small industry (now launching 2028).

That EU frame becomes a concrete national mandate — same driver, different statute and number:

In the room

How to turn regulation from a customer's headache into your opening:

Sales script

Three years ago, going green was a marketing decision for you. It isn't anymore. Brussels has put a price on your carbon through the ETS, a border tariff on dirty imports through CBAM, and a legal duty to report your electricity emissions to your own board through CSRD — three separate costs that only point one way. So the question on your desk has flipped: it's no longer "should we go renewable?" but "how much is it costing us not to?" A PPA with guarantees of origin answers all three at once — and lowers your energy bill while it does. Doing nothing is now the expensive option.

Objections you will hear

This is all EU politics — it'll get watered down before it touches me.
Rebuttal

The direction has only ever tightened: ETS allowances get scarcer each year by design, CBAM's definitive regime is phasing in from 2026, and CSRD reporting is already law for large companies — none of it speculative. Betting the rules reverse is a bet against twenty years of trend. Act now and you fix the price of your hedge on your terms; wait, and you buy the same protection in a panic, at whatever it costs then.

We're not a big emitter. Why does Scope 2 matter to us?
Rebuttal

It's less about your own smokestacks and more about keeping your customers. The large companies you supply must report their Scope 3 — which includes the emissions embedded in what they buy from you. Clean power is increasingly what keeps you on their approved-vendor list, not a nice-to-have. The real risk here isn't a fine; it's quietly losing the contract to a supplier who already cleaned up.

Can't I just buy cheap certificates and call myself green?
Rebuttal

You can buy unbundled Guarantees of Origin, but scrutiny on thin claims is rising and auditors increasingly want additionality — proof you actually brought new clean power online. A real PPA gives you a Scope 2 reduction that survives an audit and a fixed energy price. Cheap certificates give you neither the price protection nor a story that holds up — and a claim that collapses under audit costs far more than it saved.

Knowledge check
What does RED III primarily do?

RED III is the Renewable Energy Directive — the binding 2030 target. CBAM is the import tariff; CSRD is the reporting duty.

What are Scope 2 emissions?

Scope 2 = purchased electricity emissions. A PPA + Guarantees of Origin is the standard way to cut and prove a lower number.

In the worked example, what creates the ~€131k/year carbon exposure a renewable PPA removes?

5,000 MWh × 0.35 t/MWh × €75/t = €131,250. Renewable power + GOs takes reported Scope 2 toward zero.
