Selling a C&I deal in Poland

Poland is the big one in Central & Eastern Europe: a large industrial economy still running on coal, with the EU's highest carbon intensity and some of its most expensive, carbon-exposed power. That is precisely why the clean-PPA case is so strong here — and why Brussels is pouring more money into Poland than into any other member state. Here is how to walk into a Polish factory and pitch it.

Learning objective

By the end you can walk a Polish C&I prospect through a renewable PPA — naming the right regulator (URE), grid operator (PSE) and trading venue (TGE), and explaining why a coal-heavy, carbon-exposed grid makes the price case unusually strong, then pointing to the EU funds that de-risk the build.

Poland at a glance

Poland is the largest economy in Central & Eastern Europe, and its power system was built on coal. As recently as 2024, coal still supplied around 57% of generation. The transition is now moving fast — coal fell to roughly 52% in 2025, and for one month (June 2025) renewables actually beat coal for the first time. But the country still has the highest carbon intensity of any EU grid, and that single fact shapes every deal you will do here. (Live figures — check current.)

The institutions are easy to keep straight. The regulator is URE (Urząd Regulacji Energetyki, the Energy Regulatory Office). The transmission system operator — the company that owns and runs the high-voltage grid — is PSE (Polskie Sieci Elektroenergetyczne). Wholesale power is traded on the TGE (Towarowa Giełda Energii, the Polish Power Exchange), which has been coupled into the EU single day-ahead market since 2022, so Polish prices move with the wider European market.

OZE auctions & the net-billing switch

Poland's headline renewables-support scheme is the OZE auction system (aukcje OZE; OZE = odnawialne źródła energii, "renewable energy sources"). Under the 2015 RES Act, URE runs competitive auctions in which projects bid a price; winners receive a 15-year contract-for-difference (CfD) — a top-up that pays the gap between their bid and the market reference price (and claws back the excess when the market runs above it).

Key term · Contract for Difference (CfD)

A two-way price guarantee. The generator sells into the market as normal; the CfD then settles the difference against a fixed strike price. If the market is below strike, the scheme tops the generator up; if above, the generator pays the surplus back. It fixes revenue without a fixed feed-in tariff — and it is the exact model the EU's 2024 market-design reform now pushes across the bloc.

The 2025 round (held in early July) offered on the order of 75.9 TWh of contracts, with solar again outbidding wind on volume. (Live figures — check current.)

For small rooftop owners there is a separate, important change. On 1 April 2022 Poland replaced net-metering with net-billing. Net-metering let a prosumer bank exported kilowatt-hours and draw them back later, almost like a battery in the meter. Net-billing instead values exports in money at the day-ahead market price, and offsets that value against the cost of imported power. It is less generous when wholesale prices are low, which sharpens the case for self-consumption and on-site storage. Prosumers who installed before April 2022 stay on the old net-metering rules until April 2027. (Check current — there is live political pressure to soften the regime.)

The PPA & C&I landscape

This is where Poland is genuinely exciting. It is the CEE leader on corporate PPAs (cPPAs) and one of Europe's faster-growing PPA markets. While overall European PPA contracting cooled through 2025, Poland actually hit record PPA volumes in the fourth quarter of 2025 — large corporates locking long-term clean supply (for example the Energa–EDP deal, ~600 GWh/yr from 2026).

The reason is straightforward, and it is the opposite of the Austrian story. Poland's grid is coal-heavy, so wholesale power is both expensive and heavily exposed to the EU carbon price (more on that next). A Polish factory owner is not telling you "our grid is already clean" — they are watching a volatile, carbon-loaded bill and worrying about where it goes next. A corporate PPA fixes a large slice of that cost in €/MWh for 5–15 years, delivers Guarantees of Origin to cut reported Scope 2 emissions, and insulates them from the rising carbon component baked into coal-set prices.

So the Polish deal sits on three legs: a corporate PPA for the price hedge and the green attributes, an OZE auction CfD (or merchant tail) to make the underlying asset bankable, and the EU funding stack below to de-risk the build itself.

The grid bottleneck & the 10H rule

The biggest practical constraint in Poland is not demand — it is the grid. Connection requests run far ahead of available capacity: PSE has warned that the reported potential of RES projects is roughly four times what the system can currently absorb, and connection refusals are a normal part of the landscape. A deal here lives or dies on whether the site can actually get connected.

Across the region

The Grid Act (the 2026 amendment package, known as UC84) overhauls connection rules — making connection conditions valid for about a year, curbing speculative "zombie" applications that hoard capacity without building, and freeing up real headroom. It is designed to attack exactly the bottleneck above. Check current before promising a client a connection timeline.

On the wind side, Poland spent years under the notorious "10H" rule — onshore turbines had to sit at least ten times their tip height from the nearest home, which froze most of the country to new wind. That was liberalised in 2023 (the amendment came into force in April 2023), cutting the minimum to roughly 700 m, and a further loosening toward ~500 m has been advancing through 2025–26. This is steadily reopening Poland's strong onshore-wind resource. (Check current.)

Carbon & the price story — the opportunity

Here is the mechanic that makes Poland the strongest PPA case in the region. EU wholesale power is set by the last (most expensive) plant dispatched — the merit order. On a coal-heavy grid like Poland's, that marginal plant is very often a coal unit, and every coal unit must buy EU ETS allowances for the CO₂ it emits.

So the carbon price passes straight through into the power price. When ETS allowances are dear, Poland's electricity is dear — and because Poland burns more coal per kilowatt-hour than anyone else in the EU, that pass-through bites harder here than almost anywhere. The result: among the most expensive and carbon-intensive power in the EU.

Key term · ETS pass-through

Coal plants buy EU ETS carbon allowances to run. Because a coal plant is frequently the price-setting unit in Poland, the cost of those allowances is embedded in the wholesale price every hour coal sets the price — even the clean generators get paid that higher number. Rising carbon therefore means rising power bills for industry.

For a salesperson this is gold: the customer's pain (high, carbon-loaded, volatile bills) is the product's value. A renewable PPA strips the carbon component out of the customer's covered load and fixes the price — and unlike a coal-set grid bill, it does not get worse as carbon rises. The dirtier and more exposed the grid, the better the PPA looks.

The funding stack

Poland is the single largest destination for EU climate money, which makes the de-risking story exceptionally strong. Layer the sources from EU pot to national programme — and remember the ceiling that caps them all:

  • Modernisation Fund — Poland is its largest beneficiary (around 43% of the whole pool, potentially worth over €11bn). Financed by EU ETS auction revenue, it is disbursed in Poland through NFOŚiGW.
  • KPO (Krajowy Plan Odbudowy) — Poland's national recovery plan under the EU Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF), ~€54.7bn in grants and loans, a large share of it energy and grid.
  • FEnIKS — European Funds for Infrastructure, Climate & Environment 2021–27, ~€24bn — the largest cohesion programme in the entire EU.
  • NFOŚiGW programmes — the National Fund for Environmental Protection & Water Management runs the retail-facing schemes, notably Mój Prąd ("My Electricity") for prosumer PV + storage (the 6.0 round was ~PLN 1.85bn, now requiring storage), plus large grant pots for utility-scale batteries.
  • State-aid ceiling — because Poland is in the EU, every grant must fit inside the CEEAG (Climate, Energy & Environmental Aid Guidelines, 2022) and GBER (General Block Exemption Regulation) caps. You cannot stack every pot to 100% — the rules limit total aid intensity per project.
At EU level

Because Poland is in the EU, the full EU frame applies: RED III's binding renewables target, EU ETS carbon pricing (the engine of the price story above), and the 2024 Electricity Market Design reform that actively promotes PPAs and two-way CfDs. Lead with that shared frame, then localise to OZE auctions + URE + the funds. (Programme details and call windows — check current.)

In the room

How you open with a Polish industrial prospect watching a rising, carbon-loaded bill:

Sales script

Your power price is high for a specific reason: most hours it's a coal plant setting the price, and that coal plant has to buy carbon allowances — so the EU carbon price is baked straight into your bill, and it only goes up from here. A ten-or-fifteen-year renewable PPA takes that carbon-loaded slice out of two-thirds of your load and fixes it in euros per megawatt-hour, with Guarantees of Origin for your reporting. And you're in the best-funded country in Europe for this — Poland gets the biggest share of the Modernisation Fund, plus the recovery plan and FEnIKS — so the asset behind your contract is heavily de-risked. Same product the German plants buy, on Polish rails, with a stronger price case than almost anywhere.

Objections you will hear

Coal is cheap and it's what we know — why change?
Rebuttal

Coal fuel may be familiar, but coal power isn't cheap for you — it's the most expensive in the EU, because every coal megawatt-hour carries an EU carbon cost that passes straight into your price. That cost rises over time by design. A PPA fixes your covered load in €/MWh and removes that carbon exposure. You're not betting on green ideology; you're hedging a cost that only goes one way.

Grid connections take forever here — how do I know this gets built?
Rebuttal

You're right that connection is the constraint in Poland — PSE's queue is roughly four times system capacity. That's exactly why we qualify the connection point first and only build on secured capacity, and why the 2026 Grid Act is clearing out speculative "zombie" applications to free real headroom. A virtual/financial PPA can also hedge your price without waiting on a physical connection at all. We size the structure to what the grid can actually deliver.

The złoty moves and the rules keep changing — net-billing, auctions, the lot.
Rebuttal

Fair — so we don't build your deal on a moving rule. The PPA can be struck in your reporting currency to manage złoty exposure, and it stands on its own commercial terms regardless of what happens to net-billing or the next OZE round — those schemes support the asset, they're not the basis of your hedge. If a scheme improves, that's upside; if it changes, your fixed PPA price is unaffected. (Check current scheme status before quoting specifics.)

Knowledge check
Who is Poland's electricity transmission system operator (TSO)?

PSE runs the wires; URE regulates; TGE is the power exchange. Don't mix the three in front of a client.

Why is Polish wholesale power among the most expensive and carbon-exposed in the EU?

Coal sets the price on a carbon-intensive grid, so the ETS cost is embedded in the bill. That pain is exactly what a fixed-price PPA removes.

What did Poland switch to on 1 April 2022 for new prosumers?

Net-billing values exports at the market price rather than banking kWh, which sharpens the case for self-consumption and storage. Pre-2022 prosumers keep net-metering until April 2027.
