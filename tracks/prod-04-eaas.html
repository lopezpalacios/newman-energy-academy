Energy-as-a-Service & the no-capex pitch

The single biggest reason a good solar or battery project doesn't get built is the cheque. EaaS removes the cheque entirely: the developer owns the asset, the customer just pays for the energy. This lesson shows you the model, the pitch, and who it's for.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain the EaaS model in one sentence, deliver the no-capex pitch, name the customers it fits best, and use the savings calculator to show a factory its bill falling from day one.

The Energy-as-a-Service model

EaaS — Energy-as-a-Service — flips who owns the hardware. Instead of the customer buying panels or a battery, the developer owns the asset, builds it, operates it and maintains it. The customer signs up to buy the energy or the service it produces — paying either €/kWh for the power, or a fixed monthly service fee.

It is the same idea as leasing a company car instead of buying a fleet, or paying for software by subscription instead of owning the servers. You consume the outcome — clean, cheaper power — and someone else carries the asset on their balance sheet. Mechanically, the energy is usually delivered under an on-site or sleeved PPA; EaaS is the ownership and packaging wrapped around it.

Key term · Energy-as-a-Service (EaaS)

A model where the developer owns, funds, builds and operates the energy asset, and the customer pays only for what it produces — per kWh or a flat service fee — with zero upfront capital. The customer gets cheaper energy and outsourced operations; the developer keeps the asset and its long-term cash flow.

The no-capex angle

This is the sales hook, so make it sharp. Capex (capital expenditure) is the upfront cost of building the plant. For most businesses that cheque is the dealbreaker: the money is wanted for the core business — new machines, hiring, inventory — not for an energy project, however good its returns.

EaaS makes the project no-capex: the customer puts in nothing. There is no large outflow, no new debt on their balance sheet, no capital-approval battle with the board. The energy bill simply gets smaller from the first month. You have turned a capital-investment decision — slow, contested, easily deferred — into a procurement decision: are you happy to pay less for the same power? That is a far easier "yes".

Key term · No-capex (off-balance-sheet)

Because the customer never owns the asset, the cost never appears as capex on their books and usually stays off their balance sheet. They convert a big one-off capital decision into an ongoing operating expense (opex) — easier to approve, and it preserves their cash for the core business.

Who EaaS suits

EaaS is not for everyone, and knowing the fit saves you wasted meetings.

It fits customers who want the saving but can't or won't spend the capital: businesses guarding cash for growth, tenants who don't own their building outright, organisations whose internal hurdle rate for capital projects is brutally high, and anyone for whom a board fight over capex would kill the deal.

It fits less well where the customer has cheap capital and wants to own the upside — a cash-rich firm that would rather buy the asset, take the full saving and book the gain itself. For them, an outright purchase beats EaaS, because in EaaS the developer keeps a slice of the value in exchange for carrying the asset. Your job is to read which one is across the table.

Covering 60% of load (3,000 MWh) at €80 instead of €120 saves €40/MWh on that slice — about €120,000 a year, roughly a 33% cut on the covered bill, for no money down. Over ten years that is more than €1.2M the customer keeps, having invested nothing.

Across the region

The no-capex pitch widens wherever rules reward managed flexibility and shared generation — both enabled by the EU's RED II/III. Because EaaS lets the developer own and operate the asset, it is the natural home for the flexibility a customer would never run themselves.

In the room

How you open with a cash-conscious owner who assumes "going solar" means a big cheque:

Sales script

You don't buy anything, and you don't spend a koruna. We own the system, we build it, we run it, we carry every risk in it — and you simply buy the power it makes, cheaper than the grid sells it to you. No capital request to take to your board, nothing new on your balance sheet, no cheque to defend. Your only decision is whether you'd like a smaller energy bill starting next quarter. Keep your cash for the business; let us carry the asset.

Objections you will hear

If it's such a good investment, shouldn't I just buy it and keep all the saving?
Rebuttal

If you've got cheap capital sitting idle and want to own the asset outright, buying can genuinely be the better deal — and we'll put that quote in front of you too. EaaS is for when your capital earns more inside your business than tied up in a roof: you trade a slice of the saving for zero outlay, zero risk and zero operations to run. For most growing firms, that trade pays for itself.

What happens at the end of the contract — do I own the system or not?
Rebuttal

It's all set out before you sign, so there's no ambiguity to fear: at the end of the term you choose — extend the service, buy the asset at a fair residual value, or have us take it away. You're never trapped. And across the whole contract you've paid less than the grid would have charged, with us on the hook for every repair and every panel-clean along the way.

I'm only a tenant — I can't put a system on a building I don't own.
Rebuttal

That's one of the cases EaaS is built for, not blocked by. Because the developer owns and operates the asset, we structure it around your lease term and the landlord's consent — often with a roof agreement that outlasts your tenancy. You never take a fixed asset onto a building you don't own; you just buy cheaper power for as long as you're there.

Knowledge check
In an EaaS arrangement, who owns the energy asset?

EaaS keeps the asset on the developer's books. The customer consumes the outcome — cheaper power — with no ownership and no capex.

Why is the "no-capex" framing such a powerful sales angle?

No upfront cheque, nothing on the balance sheet, no board fight — just a smaller bill. The decision shifts from capital approval to simple procurement.

Which customer is the weakest fit for EaaS?

When capital is cheap and the customer wants the whole upside, outright purchase beats EaaS — in EaaS the developer keeps a slice for carrying the asset.
