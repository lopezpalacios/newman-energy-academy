Battery storage (BESS)

Solar makes cheap power when the sun shines. A battery lets you move that power through time — and sell the same hardware into three or four markets at once. This lesson explains what a battery actually earns, the two numbers that define one, and how revenue gets stacked.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain the four jobs a BESS does, tell the difference between its power and its energy, and show why "revenue stacking" is what makes a battery pay — using the live calculator to add the streams up.

What a battery actually does

A BESS — Battery Energy Storage System — is a box of lithium-ion cells with an inverter, that charges when power is cheap or plentiful and discharges when it is expensive or scarce. It earns money in four distinct ways, and a good project does several at once.

Arbitrage is the simplest: buy energy cheap (midday, when solar floods the market) and sell it dear (the evening peak). The profit per unit is the spread between the two prices.

Peak shaving is the customer-side version: a factory discharges its battery during its own highest-demand half-hours to cut the capacity charge — the part of an industrial bill set by your single biggest spike, not your total usage.

Ancillary services are jobs the battery does for the grid operator to keep the system stable — most importantly FCR (Frequency Containment Reserve), where the battery automatically injects or absorbs power in seconds to hold the grid at 50 Hz. Batteries are paid handsomely for this because they respond faster than any power plant.

Capacity is a standing payment — €/MW/yr — for simply being available to deliver power when the system needs it, whether or not it is ever called.

Key term · FCR (Frequency Containment Reserve)

A fast-acting ancillary service: the battery senses the grid frequency drifting from 50 Hz and corrects it within seconds by charging or discharging. Because batteries react far faster than gas or coal plants, grid operators pay a premium — making FCR one of the richest revenue lines in a battery's stack.

Power vs energy: MW and MWh

This is the one distinction newcomers get wrong, so nail it. A battery has two ratings, and they are not interchangeable.

Power is measured in MW (megawatts) — how fast the battery can push energy in or out. It is the size of the tap.

Energy is measured in MWh (megawatt-hours) — how much it can store in total. It is the size of the tank.

Divide energy by power and you get duration — how many hours the battery can run flat-out before it is empty. A 5 MW / 10 MWh system is a "2-hour battery": it can deliver 5 MW for 2 hours. A "1-hour" battery (5 MW / 5 MWh) is cheaper but exhausts faster; a "4-hour" battery stores more but costs more to build.

Key term · Duration

Duration = energy (MWh) ÷ power (MW). It tells you how long the battery can sustain full output. Short-duration (1–2 h) batteries suit fast services like FCR; longer-duration (2–4 h) batteries suit arbitrage, where you want to shift a lot of energy across the day. The duration you choose decides which revenue streams you can chase.

Round-trip efficiency

No battery returns every unit it stores — some is lost as heat in charging, holding and discharging. Round-trip efficiency is the share that survives the journey. For modern lithium-ion BESS it is about 86%: put 100 units in, get roughly 86 back out.

This matters for arbitrage. If you buy at €40/MWh and sell at €100/MWh, the headline spread is €60 — but you only resell 86% of what you bought, so the effective spread is smaller. Always quote spreads after efficiency, or a fund analyst will catch you.

Revenue stacking — the whole point

A single revenue stream rarely justifies a battery. The economics work because the same hardware earns from several at once. Revenue stacking is the practice of layering arbitrage + capacity + ancillary income on one asset, sequenced so they don't clash.

You might commit the battery to FCR overnight, do arbitrage around the evening peak, and collect a capacity payment year-round just for being available. Add the streams up and a project that loses money on any one of them can be solidly profitable on the stack. That is what your calculator below demonstrates.

Worked example — a 5 MW / 10 MWh battery

A grid-connected 2-hour battery in Czechia. Round, realistic numbers:

Build cost at €350/kWh on 10,000 kWh of energy is €3.5M. Against €444k/yr of stacked revenue, that is a simple payback of roughly 8 years — before financing — on an asset rated for 15+ years. No single stream gets you there; the stack does.

In the room

How you frame a battery to a CFO who thinks storage is a science project:

Sales script

Think of the battery not as one asset but as the same hardware selling into three markets. It buys cheap midday power and sells it into the evening peak; it gets paid a standing fee just for being available; and it earns a premium for steadying grid frequency in seconds — something no gas plant can match. Any one of those alone is marginal. Stack all three and you are looking at a payback under a decade on a fifteen-year machine.

Objections you will hear

Batteries are expensive and the price spreads might collapse.
Rebuttal

That's exactly why we don't rely on arbitrage alone. The capacity and FCR payments are contracted or regulated income that doesn't depend on volatile spreads — drag the spread slider to zero in the model and the stack still earns. And cell prices keep falling, so the capex side is moving in our favour, not against it.

What about the round-trip losses — you're throwing energy away.
Rebuttal

About 14% is lost, yes, and we model the spread net of that — so when we quote a €60 spread, the economics already assume we only resell 86% of what we store. The losses are real but small, and they're more than covered by the price gap between cheap midday and expensive peak power.

How long do the cells last — won't they wear out from all that cycling?
Rebuttal

Cells degrade with use, so we size the duration and cycle count to the warranted life — typically 15 years or several thousand cycles — and the battery management system protects the cells from deep over-cycling. The payback we showed lands well inside that warranted window, so the asset earns back its cost before degradation matters.

Knowledge check
A battery is rated 5 MW / 10 MWh. What is its duration?

Duration = MWh ÷ MW = 10 ÷ 5 = 2 hours. Power is the tap; energy is the tank.

Why does "revenue stacking" matter for battery economics?

Arbitrage + capacity + ancillary on one asset — the stack is what pays, not any single line.
