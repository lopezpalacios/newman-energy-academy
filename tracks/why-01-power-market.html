The European power market in 90 minutes

Before you can sell clean power, you need to know how power itself is bought and sold. Electricity is a traded commodity like oil or wheat — and once you see the market behind the wall socket, every pitch you make afterwards gets sharper.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain, to a factory owner who has never thought about it, how a megawatt-hour gets priced and sold in Europe — and name the five players whose decisions land on their electricity bill.

Electricity is a traded commodity

Most people picture electricity as a service that simply arrives. In a market it is a commodity — a standardised product bought and sold in bulk, exactly like crude oil or natural gas. The unit of trade is the megawatt-hour (MWh): one megawatt of power delivered for one hour. A mid-size Czech factory might consume a few thousand MWh a year; a household, a handful.

Two features make electricity an unusual commodity, and both matter for your pitch. First, it is near-impossible to store cheaply at scale — so supply and demand must match every single second, which is why prices swing. Second, it is physically identical whoever makes it — an electron from a coal plant and one from a solar panel are the same on the wire. That is why "green" power needs a paper certificate (you will meet Guarantees of Origin later) to prove where it came from.

Key term · Megawatt-hour (MWh)

The standard unit of energy traded in power markets: one megawatt of power sustained for one hour. Prices are quoted in €/MWh. Roughly, 1 MWh = the electricity a typical Czech household uses in about three months.

Wholesale vs retail — two different prices

There are really two markets stacked on top of each other, and confusing them is the single most common newcomer mistake.

The wholesale market is where generators sell bulk power to suppliers and large buyers. The headline reference is the day-ahead spot market: every afternoon, buyers and sellers submit bids for each hour of the next day, and an auction sets one clearing price per hour. In Czechia this auction is run by OTE, a.s. (the market operator), and OTE's auction is coupled to almost every other EU country through SDAC — Single Day-Ahead Coupling, the mechanism that joins national auctions into one European price-finding system. The effect: a gas shortage in Germany pushes up the price your Czech client pays.

The retail market is what the end customer actually signs: their supplier bundles the wholesale energy cost with network charges (paying for the wires), regulated levies, and a margin. So the customer's all-in tariff is always well above the raw wholesale number.

Key term · Day-ahead spot market

A daily auction that sets one wholesale electricity price for each hour of the following day. Run by OTE in Czechia and price-coupled across the EU via SDAC. It is the benchmark every PPA and every retail tariff is measured against.

Who the players are

Five roles run the Czech (and, by analogy, every EU) power system. Learn these names — you will drop them in meetings to sound fluent fast.

  • Generators — whoever makes the power: nuclear, coal, gas, solar, wind. Your future PPA seller is a generator.
  • Suppliers (retailers) — buy wholesale, sell to homes and businesses, carry the billing relationship.
  • TSO — Transmission System Operator — runs the high-voltage backbone and keeps supply and demand balanced second by second. In Czechia this is CEPS.
  • Regulator — sets the rules and the regulated network tariffs. In Czechia this is ERÚ (Energetický regulační úřad, the Energy Regulatory Office).
  • Market operator — runs the spot exchange and the registries. In Czechia this is OTE.

Why a businessperson should care: the generator sets the commodity cost, the TSO and regulator set the network charges, and together they explain why your client's bill is high and volatile. A PPA lets you fix the biggest, swingiest piece — the commodity cost — which is exactly the part the customer cannot control today.

Worked example — anatomy of a Czech industrial bill

Take a mid-voltage Czech manufacturer buying 5,000 MWh a year. Here is roughly how their all-in tariff breaks down. Illustrative numbers, in €/MWh:

All-in industrial tariff, broken out
Component€/MWh
Wholesale energy (set near the day-ahead spot)85
Network & system charges (TSO + distribution, regulated)38
Levies, taxes & supplier margin17
All-in delivered tariff140
Annual energy cost = 5,000 MWh × €140 / MWh = €700,000

The single largest and most volatile slice is the wholesale energy at €85 — and it is the only piece a PPA can lock. The network charges (€38) are regulated and largely fixed; you cannot sell those away, so never promise to.

At EU level

The Czech market sits inside a two-layer stack. Brussels sets the frame: the European Commission (DG ENER) drives energy policy, ACER coordinates the national regulators, and ENTSO-E (the network of TSOs) runs the grid codes and the coupled day-ahead market. Member states run their own national markets underneath — each with its own regulator, TSO and day-ahead venue.

So the five roles you just met repeat in every country; only the names change, and the day-ahead auction clears on a different exchange in each:

In the room

How to frame the market for a CFO who has never separated wholesale from retail:

Sales script

Your electricity bill is really two bills in one. About a quarter of it — the wires and the regulated charges — is fixed by the operator, and we'd never claim to touch it. The biggest chunk is the raw energy, and that price is set every single day in an auction you have no seat at. That's the piece that doubled in 2022 — and it's the exact piece we can fix for you, as one number, for years. We're not selling you electricity. We're selling you a line on your budget that finally stops moving.

Objections you will hear

My utility already gives me one price. Why do I care about a wholesale market?
Rebuttal

That one price isn't protection — it's just last year's wholesale market wrapped in a margin, and it resets against you at renewal. When the spot market spikes, your next contract spikes with it; you simply feel it on a delay. A fixed contract is how you stop being a price-taker on the biggest cost you don't control, and start setting the number yourself.

Isn't this just a German or Brussels problem? We're in Czechia.
Rebuttal

It lands on you whether it's your problem or not: Czech prices are coupled to the rest of Europe through single day-ahead coupling, so OTE's auction settles against the same system as Germany and Austria. A gas event two borders away shows up on your Brno bill in the same hour. That exposure you can't escape is exactly why a local fixed-price contract is worth having — it walls your number off from everyone else's weather.

Power just comes out of the socket — why complicate it?
Rebuttal

It does for households paying tens of euros. For you it's a six- or seven-figure line item — and the most unpredictable one on your P&L. The companies that treat it as a number to be locked, not a bill to be paid, are the ones whose margins still hold the year the market turns.

Knowledge check
What sets the benchmark wholesale electricity price for the next day in Czechia?

The day-ahead spot auction (OTE in CZ, coupled EU-wide) sets one hourly clearing price — the benchmark every tariff and PPA references.

In the worked example, which slice of the €140/MWh tariff can a PPA actually fix?

A PPA fixes the commodity (energy) cost. Network charges are regulated and stay — never promise to remove them.

Which player runs the high-voltage grid and balances supply with demand in Czechia?

CEPS is the TSO — it runs the transmission backbone and keeps the system balanced second by second. OTE runs the market; ERÚ sets the rules.
