The twelve-slide pitch

A C&I energy pitch is not a slideshow — it is an argument, built one slide at a time, that ends with the prospect asking for a proposal. Twelve slides, each proving exactly one thing. Learn the order and you can pitch from memory.

Learning objective

By the end you can build and deliver a twelve-slide C&I energy pitch in which every slide proves one specific point, the saving lands before the ask, and the meeting closes with a clear yes to the next step.

A pitch is an argument, not a brochure

The job of the deck is to walk a busy decision-maker from "why am I in this meeting" to "send me the contract" without a single slide they could skip. Each slide carries one claim and earns the next. If a slide doesn't move the argument forward, it comes out.

The order matters more than the design. You establish the pain before the product, the product before the price, and the proof before the ask. Pitch the saving too early and they stop listening to the risk story; pitch the company too early and they wonder why they should care. Twelve slides is enough to be complete and few enough to hold a room.

Key term · The ask

The single, specific action you want the prospect to agree to at the end of the meeting — almost never "sign now". For a first C&I pitch the ask is usually "share twelve months of bills so we can model your exact saving". A clear, small, concrete ask closes far more meetings than a vague "let us know what you think".

The three-act arc underneath the twelve slides

Group the slides in your head as three acts and you'll never lose the thread:

  • Act 1 — Why this, why now (slides 1–4): the market and the mandate make their power cost and emissions a board-level problem they can no longer ignore.
  • Act 2 — What we'd do for you (slides 5–9): the specific product, the specific saving, with no capex and a price below their tariff.
  • Act 3 — Why you can trust us, and the ask (slides 10–12): proof they won't get burned, then the small next step.

The twelve slides — what each must prove

This is the spine of the deck. The right-hand column is the test: if a slide doesn't prove its claim, it isn't done.

1Title & promiseOne line: "Cheaper, cleaner power — no upfront cost." Sets the frame.
2The problem: volatile pricesTheir power price is set by gas at the margin and will stay high and volatile.

6How the contract worksThe PPA / EaaS model: you own and run the asset, they buy the kWh. No capex.

8The green dividendTonnes of CO₂ and Scope 2 cut; Guarantees of Origin for their reporting.
9Zero capex, zero hassleYou fund, build, insure, operate & maintain. Their risk and cash outlay ≈ nil.
10Proof: track recordComparable sites delivered; the team; who backs the financing. Why you'll still be here in year 12.
11How we get thereSimple timeline: bills → proposal → survey → contract → build → switch-on.
12The askOne concrete next step: "Twelve months of bills, and we'll model your exact saving."

Slide 4 carries the most weight: the moment you put their own numbers on screen, it stops being a sales call and becomes their problem. Never pitch without it.

At EU level

Structure the pitch by lens: open with the EU drivers that are identical in every country, then drop to the prospect's national regulator and support scheme. Slides 2–3 are the same deck in Prague, Munich or Vienna — only the national footnote on slide 3 changes.

LensWhat you pitch
EU — same everywhereRED III's ~42.5% 2030 renewables target, the 2024 Electricity Market Design reform pushing PPAs & two-way CfDs, and the EU ETS carbon cost on their power.
National — localiseTheir regulator + scheme: ERÚ / Modernisation Fund (CZ), BNetzA / EEG (DE), E-Control / EAG (AT), ElCom / Einmalvergütung (CH — outside the EU internal market today).

Across markets: EU frame first (same driver everywhere), national detail second (regulator + support scheme). Targets and schemes shift — check current.

How to open and how to close

The open (first 60 seconds). Don't open with your company. Open with their cost. "You spend roughly four hundred thousand euros a year on electricity, and that price is set by the gas market, not by you. I'm here to show you how to take a chunk of that back — with no upfront cost. Fifteen minutes?" You've named the pain, promised relief, and asked permission to continue.

The close (last 60 seconds). Restate the saving, then make the ask small and specific. "So: roughly a fifth off your power bill, no capex, and you cut your reported emissions at the same time. The only thing I need to turn this from an estimate into a firm number is twelve months of your bills. Can you have someone send those over this week?" A close is not "what do you think" — it is a concrete request the prospect can say yes to in the room.

In the room

The handoff from the saving slide to the ask — the most important ten seconds of the meeting:

Sales script

That's roughly eighty thousand euros a year back in your pocket — every year, nothing paid upfront, and your emissions reporting handled at the same time. I'm not asking you to sign anything today; I'm asking for twelve months of your bills, so the number on this slide stops being my estimate and becomes a fixed figure you can take to your board. Get me those bills and this turns from a promise into your contract. Who on your team can send them this week?

Objections you will hear

Can you just email me the deck so I can read it later?
Rebuttal

Happy to — but the deck is generic until slide four, and slide four is your bill, not an industry average. Give me fifteen minutes live and the saving I show you is built on your actual consumption — a number you can trust precisely because it's your own. That's the version worth reading, and it's the one I'll leave in your inbox.

This is a lot of slides — can you cut to the price?
Rebuttal

Absolutely — the price is slide seven and I can be there in ninety seconds. But a price on its own is just a number; the two slides before it show what you pay today and the mandate you're already exposed to, so you can see exactly what the saving is measured against. Let me set those up fast, then the number becomes something you can actually act on.

My energy manager should really be in this meeting, not me.
Rebuttal

They should — for the technical detail. But this isn't a technical decision; it's a P&L and balance-sheet call — a fixed, long-term saving with no capex on your books. That one's yours to own. Give me five minutes for the commercial version and you'll know whether it's worth your manager's time; if it is, I'll go deep with them next.

Knowledge check
Which slide most reliably turns a sales call into "their problem"?

Putting the prospect's own numbers on screen makes it personal and concrete. Never pitch without slide 4.

What is the right "ask" to close a first C&I pitch?

A concrete, low-friction ask closes far more first meetings than a hard sell or a vague hand-off.

Why prove the pain (slides 2–4) before the price (slide 7)?

Pain before product, product before price, proof before the ask. The arc is what makes the number land.
