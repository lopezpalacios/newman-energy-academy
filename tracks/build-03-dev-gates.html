Development gates & de-risking

Development is not one big leap — it is a staircase of gates. Each gate removes one named risk, and the instant it closes behind you, the project is worth more. Learn to see development as value creation, not just box-ticking.

Learning objective

By the end you can describe development as a sequence of de-risking gates — site control, grid offer, permits, offtake, financing — and explain how clearing each gate removes a specific risk and raises the asset's value toward ready-to-build.

Development as a staircase of gates

A development gate is a milestone where one major uncertainty is resolved — a yes/no checkpoint the project must pass before the next stage is worth funding. Until a gate is cleared, the question it answers ("Can we even use this land?", "Will the grid take us?") hangs over the whole project and caps its value.

The mental model is simple and powerful: value is not added by spending money — it is added by removing risk. Early on, a project is just an idea with a long list of ways it could fail. Each gate crosses one item off that list. When the list is empty, the project is ready-to-build (RTB) and worth the most it has ever been before construction starts.

This is why developers talk about "de-risking." They are not being cautious for its own sake — they are manufacturing value, one resolved uncertainty at a time.

The five gates

The exact list varies, but five gates do most of the work on a typical solar or storage project:

Gate 1 · Site control. A lease, option or purchase that gives you exclusive legal right to use the land. Without it you have nothing — anyone could build there, or refuse you. Removes: land risk.

Gate 2 · Grid offer. An accepted connection offer from the TSO or DSO (Lesson 2). Confirms the grid will physically accept your power and at what cost. Removes: interconnection risk — usually the biggest single risk.

Gate 3 · Permits. Zoning and the building permit (in Czechia, the stavební povolení), plus any EIA. Confirms you are legally allowed to build. Removes: consenting / planning risk.

Gate 4 · Offtake. A signed PPA or other route to sell the power at a known price. Confirms the plant will have revenue. Removes: revenue / market risk.

Gate 5 · Financing. Committed equity and debt to financial close. Confirms the money exists to build. Removes: funding risk — the last gate before construction.

Key term · Development gate

A checkpoint at which a single major project risk is resolved — site, grid, permits, offtake or finance. A project's value steps up each time it clears a gate, because a buyer no longer has to price in the chance that it fails. The full set cleared = ready-to-build.

Why each gate adds value

Imagine a buyer looking at your project. They will pay only what it is worth after discounting for everything that could still go wrong. A raw idea might have a one-in-twenty chance of ever reaching operation — so it is worth almost nothing, even if the finished plant would be worth millions.

Clear the grid-offer gate and that probability jumps; the biggest "this might never happen" risk is gone. Clear permits and offtake, and a professional buyer can now see a near-certain path to a revenue-generating asset. Each gate doesn't just tick a box — it raises the probability of success that the value is multiplied by.

That is the developer's craft: spend a relatively small amount of time and money clearing gates, and watch the project's value climb far faster than the cost of clearing them. The gap between the two is the subject of the next lesson — where the margin is made.

At EU level

The five gates are the same across the EU, but the offtake/financing gates (4 and 5) are being reshaped by Brussels. Under the Electricity Market Design reform, new direct price-support schemes for wind/solar/geothermal/reservoir-free hydro/nuclear concluded on or after 17 Jul 2027 must take the form of two-way Contracts for Difference (CfDs) — a hedge that pays the project when prices fall and claws back when they rise.

For the developer that changes how the revenue gate is de-risked: where a project leans on state support, a two-way CfD becomes the instrument that locks in revenue — a more bankable offtake than a bare merchant position. A private corporate PPA remains the alternative route through Gate 4. Switzerland sits outside this EU frame (its support is the Einmalvergütung PV grant) — check current as the reform transposes.

Across markets: same five gates everywhere; from mid-2027 the EU pushes new state-backed offtake toward two-way CfDs — except in non-EU Switzerland.

In the room

How you explain to an investor why you fund development in stages rather than all at once:

Sales script

We never bet the whole budget on day one. We spend a little to clear the first gate — land — then a little more to win the grid offer, then permits, then the contract that sells the power. Each gate we pass kills off one specific way this could fail, and the project's worth steps up the instant it does. By ready-to-build, almost nothing can go wrong — and that certainty is precisely what a buyer pays a premium for. We don't spend our way to value; we de-risk our way there.

Objections you will hear

Why not just buy the equipment and start building?
Rebuttal

Because building before the gates are cleared is how you end up with panels you can't connect or aren't allowed to switch on. Steel is the easy, low-risk part, so we do it last — only once land, grid, permits and offtake are all locked down. The sequence isn't caution; it's what protects the capital.

If gates are so valuable, isn't a half-developed project a bad buy?
Rebuttal

It's a cheaper buy with more risk still in it — and for a buyer good at clearing the remaining gates, that can be exactly the right trade. Value tracks risk in lockstep: less for an early-stage project, more for a de-risked one. We price each project openly for precisely where it sits on the staircase, so you pay for the certainty you're actually getting.

What if a project fails a gate after we've spent on the earlier ones?
Rebuttal

That's exactly why we run them in order — cheapest and most decisive first — so the deal-killers surface early, while almost nothing has been spent. A project that can't clear the grid gate is stopped before a cent goes on permits. The staircase is built to fail fast and cheap, never slow and expensive.

Knowledge check
In the gate model, what actually adds value to a project?

Value is manufactured by de-risking, not by spending. Each gate crosses one failure mode off the list.

Which gate typically produces the single biggest jump in value?

Consistent with Lesson 2: the grid offer removes the biggest "will it happen?" risk, so it moves value the most.

When is a project "ready-to-build" (RTB)?

RTB is the full set of gates cleared: the project is at its highest pre-construction value because almost every uncertainty is resolved.
