Qualifying: load, tariff, roof, credit

Most deals are won or lost before you build a single slide. Qualifying is the discipline of finding out, fast, whether a prospect can actually buy what you sell — so you spend your weeks on the few that close, not the many that waste them.

Learning objective

By the end you can sit across from a business owner and, in twenty minutes of plain conversation, decide whether they are a real prospect — and walk out with the five numbers that tell you whether there is a deal here at all.

Why you qualify before you pitch

A polished pitch to the wrong prospect is wasted work. Before you design anything, you need to know that the customer uses enough power, pays enough for it, has somewhere to put the kit, and is creditworthy enough to sign a long contract. That is qualifying.

Qualifying is the act of testing a prospect against the few criteria that decide whether a renewable-energy deal can exist. Do it in the first meeting, out loud, with simple questions. A prospect that fails on load or credit is not a smaller deal — it is usually no deal, and the kindest thing you can do is find that out on day one.

The good news: in C&I energy the qualifying questions are concrete and the prospect almost always knows the answers, because they all show up on the electricity bill they already pay.

The five questions that qualify a site

Everything reduces to five things. Learn them as a sequence you can run from memory.

1 · Annual load (MWh). How much electricity does the site consume in a year? This is the size of the prize. A facility using 200 MWh/yr is a small rooftop job; one using 5,000+ MWh/yr is a serious C&I opportunity. Annual load (in megawatt-hours, MWh — one MWh is 1,000 kWh) is the first number on any utility bill and it scales everything downstream.

2 · Current tariff (€/MWh). What do they pay per unit, all-in? In Czechia an industrial all-in tariff is typically of the order of €100–180/MWh once you add the energy commodity, network charges and the regulated ERÚ components. The higher their tariff, the bigger the saving you can offer against a solar cost of roughly €50–70/MWh.

3 · Daytime / load shape. Solar generates in the day. A two-shift factory that runs hard from 8am to 6pm consumes its own solar on site — the best economics, because every self-consumed kWh avoids the full retail tariff including network charges. A site that only runs at night is a far weaker fit for solar alone (though a candidate for storage).

4 · Roof or land available. Is there an unshaded roof in decent structural condition, or spare land near the meter? Roughly, 1 MWp of solar needs ~6,000–8,000 m² of roof or ~1–1.5 ha of land. No usable space behind the meter means no on-site project — you pivot to a sleeved or virtual PPA instead.

5 · Contract appetite. Will they sign a long contract? On-site PPAs and Energy-as-a-Service run 10–15 years. A business that expects to sell the site next year, or refuses anything past three years, cannot host the deal that makes the economics work.

Key term · Annual load

The total electricity a site consumes in a year, measured in MWh. It sets the scale of every renewable deal: the bigger and steadier the load, the more generation it can absorb and the larger the saving. It is the single most useful number to extract in a first meeting — and it is printed on their bill.

The fifth gate that newcomers forget: credit

A PPA or EaaS deal is, financially, a 10–15 year loan you are extending to the customer — you (or the fund that buys the asset) put up the capital and get paid back through their power payments over a decade. So the customer's ability to keep paying for that long is not a detail; it is the deal.

Offtaker creditworthiness is the financial strength of the party signing the power contract (the "offtaker"). An investment-grade corporate or a profitable, long-established mid-cap is bankable. A thinly capitalised startup on a short lease is not — not because they are bad people, but because no fund will buy an asset whose cash flow depends on a fragile counterparty. The asset is only as safe as the signature on the contract.

You don't need to be a credit analyst in the room. You need three signals: how long has the business existed, is it profitable, and who actually owns the building. A tenant on a two-year lease cannot grant a 15-year roof right — that alone can sink an otherwise perfect site.

Across the region

The five questions are identical in every country — but the registry and support-scheme gate you qualify against changes. Before you tell a prospect they're eligible, check which national scheme the site falls under and whether the unit is registered.

Red flags that kill the deal

Some answers should stop you in the room, politely. Learn to hear them.

  • "We're on a short lease" — they can't grant a 15-year roof right. No site control, no on-site deal.
  • "We might sell the business in a year or two" — the offtaker may not exist for the contract term.
  • "We only run a night shift" — solar self-consumption collapses; you're now selling storage, a different conversation.
  • "Our bill is tiny" — under a few hundred MWh/yr the fixed costs of developing a project eat the saving.
  • "The roof was last redone in the 1990s" — re-roofing under live panels is expensive; structural and waterproofing risk you don't want.
  • "We've been trading eighteen months" — too little history for a fund to underwrite a decade of payments.

None of these mean the person is not worth knowing. They mean this site, today is not a deal — log it, stay in touch, and move your energy to a prospect that scores green.

In the room

How to qualify without sounding like an interrogation — keep it conversational, anchored on their bill:

Sales script

Before I design anything, I want to check one thing: whether I can put a fixed price on your power that beats the grid. Roughly how many megawatt-hours do you burn a year, what's the blended rate on your last bill, and when does the plant run hardest — days, nights, both? Do you own the building, and would a long supply deal, say a dozen years, scare you? On those numbers there's a real saving here — the kind that turns a bill that jumps around every quarter into one fixed line you can plan a budget around. Give me a week and I'll show you exactly how much.

Objections you will hear

Why do you need all my numbers before you've even shown me anything?
Rebuttal

Because I'd rather hand you a real number than a brochure full of averages. Your own consumption and tariff are the only honest basis for a price I can stand behind — without them, anything I show you is guesswork dressed up as a proposal. Five minutes on your bill now, and everything I bring back is built on your reality, not someone else's.

I don't know our exact consumption off the top of my head.
Rebuttal

No need — it's the headline figure on any monthly electricity bill, in kWh or MWh. Forward me one recent bill and I'll pull your annual load and blended rate myself, then come back with a number you can check against your own records. One document on your side, all the work on mine.

We're a solid company — why are you asking about our finances?
Rebuttal

It's a compliment, not a credit check. A strong balance sheet is exactly what earns you the best price and zero upfront cost, because cheaper financing for a solid partner flows straight back into your rate. I'm not assessing you today — I'm making sure we build something a decade-long contract can safely sit on, which protects you every bit as much as us.

Knowledge check
Which single number best tells you the size of the prize at a new prospect?

Annual load scales everything downstream — generation, saving and deal size. It's the first number to extract, straight off the bill.

A prospect has great load and a high tariff but is on a two-year lease. What's the call?

Site control is a hard gate. A short lease means they can't grant the long roof right the project needs.

Why does offtaker creditworthiness matter so much in a PPA or EaaS deal?

A fund buying the asset underwrites the offtaker's ability to pay for a decade. Weak credit, no bankable deal.
