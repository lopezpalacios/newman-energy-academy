The institutional buyer landscape

You have a de-risked, contracted asset to sell. Who is on the other side of the table? This lesson maps the five kinds of institutional buyer for operating renewables, what each actually wants, and how a real sale process runs — teaser, NBO, due diligence, SPA.

Learning objective

By the end you can name the five institutional buyer categories for operating renewable assets, say what each one prizes, and walk a client through the four stages of a sale process — teaser → NBO → due diligence → SPA — without bluffing.

Who is actually buying — and why they bother

Operating renewable assets are not bought by people who want electricity. They are bought by institutional capital — large pools of money that need long, predictable, often inflation-linked returns to match long-dated obligations. A solar plant with a 15-year contract is, to them, a slightly-higher-yielding bond that happens to have panels attached.

That single insight explains the whole buyer universe. Every category below is a different flavour of patient money, and each one tilts toward a different mix of yield, scale, contract length and counterparty credit. Your job at exit is to know which buyer your particular asset fits — because selling to the wrong type wastes months.

Key term · Institutional buyer

A professional investor managing large third-party capital (pensions, insurance reserves, fund commitments) rather than its own pocket money. It buys de-risked, cash-generative assets to earn a steady return for its beneficiaries — which is exactly why it accepts the compressed yield you learned about last lesson.

What each buyer is really shopping for

No buyer wants "a solar plant" in the abstract. Each is solving a portfolio problem, and they screen on four dials:

  • Yield — the running cash return. Some buyers will accept the tightest yields (and pay the most); others need more.
  • Scale — cheque size. A €4M single plant is too small for some; a 500 MW platform is too big for others.
  • Contract length — how many years of contracted cash remain. Long-dated, inflation-linked offtake is gold to liability-matchers.
  • Counterparty credit — who pays the bill. An investment-grade industrial offtaker unlocks the cheapest capital.
Key term · Liability matching

Pension funds and insurers owe money far in the future (pensions, claims). They buy assets whose cash flows line up with those future payments. Long, contracted, inflation-linked renewable cash is an almost perfect match — which is why these buyers chase it and accept low yields for it.

How a sale process actually runs

You do not phone a fund and agree a price. A disciplined sale runs as a competitive process in four stages, usually steered by an adviser. Knowing the vocabulary stops you sounding green in front of a sophisticated buyer.

  • 1 · Teaser & information memorandum. A short anonymised teaser goes to a shortlist of buyers; interested parties sign an NDA and receive the fuller information memorandum (IM) — the asset's full story and data.
  • 2 · NBO (non-binding offer). Buyers submit indicative prices and terms. NBO = a non-binding offer: it sets a price range and lets you pick a shortlist to take forward. Nobody is committed yet.
  • 3 · Due diligence. The chosen bidder(s) verify everything — technical, legal, financial, tax, the PPA, the grid connection — in a data room. This is where weak contracts or permitting gaps surface and chip the price.
  • 4 · SPA & close. The winner signs a Share/Sale and Purchase Agreement (SPA) — the binding contract transferring the asset, with price, warranties and conditions. On completion, cash arrives and ownership transfers.
Key term · SPA (Sale & Purchase Agreement)

The binding legal contract that actually transfers the asset (or the company that owns it) to the buyer. It fixes the price, the warranties the seller stands behind, and the conditions to be met before completion. Everything before the SPA — teaser, NBO, due diligence — is the runway to get here.

In the room

How you reassure a project backer that there is a deep, real market for the finished asset:

Sales script

When this plant is built and contracted, we're not hoping someone shows up — we run a competitive process. It goes to infrastructure funds, pension money, the listed yield vehicles and a couple of utilities, all of whom buy exactly this kind of cash flow for a living. They bid non-binding first, we shortlist, they do their diligence, and the winner signs an SPA. Five categories of buyer competing is what protects our price.

Objections you will hear

Is there really a buyer for a single small plant like this?
Rebuttal

For a lone 5 MW asset the cheque is small for the biggest funds, so we either sell to a buyer that aggregates small assets or — more often — we bundle it with sister plants into a portfolio. At portfolio scale the deepest, cheapest pools of capital compete, and the price per megawatt goes up, not down.

What if the buyer drops the price during due diligence?
Rebuttal

That's exactly why we de-risk before we sell — clean permits, a signed long-term PPA, a verified grid connection and a tidy data room. Price chips come from surprises in diligence; we remove the surprises up front. Running a competitive process also means a single bidder can't squeeze us without losing to the others.

Why use a process and an adviser instead of just calling one fund?
Rebuttal

One phone call gives one party all the leverage. A structured process — teaser, NBOs, shortlist, diligence, SPA — puts several institutional buyers in competition and surfaces the best price and terms. The adviser's fee is small next to the tension a real auction creates.

Knowledge check
Which buyer type typically accepts the lowest yield (and so pays the most) for long, contracted, inflation-linked cash?

Liability-matchers want the safest long cash and accept the tightest yields — so they pay top price for a clean, contracted asset.

In a sale process, what is an NBO?

NBO = non-binding offer. The binding document at the end of the process is the SPA.

Why does bundling our 5 MW plant into a larger portfolio help at exit?

Scale changes the buyer set. Portfolio size brings in the deepest pools of capital — the bridge to next lesson's platform sale.
