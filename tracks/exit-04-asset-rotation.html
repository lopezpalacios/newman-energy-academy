Asset rotation & platform sales

One exit is a trade. Doing it again and again is a business. This lesson shows how developers recycle the same equity through project after project, why selling a whole platform beats selling plants one by one, and how the develop-to-sell model joins up end to end.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain asset rotation — how selling operating assets funds the next build — and why a platform of projects sells for more than the sum of its individual plants. You can sketch the develop-to-sell business model on one page.

Recycling the same equity, again and again

A developer's scarcest resource is not sites or sunshine — it is equity. You only have so much of your own (and your backers') cash to put at risk. If every plant you build ties that cash up for 25 years, you can build very few plants.

Asset rotation breaks that constraint. You put equity into a project, build it, contract it, sell the de-risked asset to a fund at a premium, and recycle the same equity into the next two or three projects. The capital does several laps instead of one. The exit is not the end of the business — it is the fuel pump.

The arithmetic is powerful. If €3M of equity builds a plant you sell for €4M, you get your €3M back plus €1M of gain — and you redeploy roughly €4M into new development. Do that on a cycle and a small equity base supports a build programme many times its size.

Key term · Asset rotation (capital recycling)

Selling completed, de-risked assets to release the equity locked in them, then redeploying that equity into new development. It lets a developer build far more megawatts than its own balance sheet could ever hold long-term — the capital "rotates" through project after project.

Why the whole platform sells for more than the parts

Sell plants one at a time and each is a small, separate transaction. Bundle them — operating assets, plus the development pipeline and the team that builds it — into a platform, and a buyer will often pay a premium over the summed value of the individual assets.

Why? Because a platform offers things a single asset cannot:

  • Scale in one transaction. A fund can deploy hundreds of millions in a single deal instead of chasing dozens of tiny ones — its diligence and legal cost per megawatt drops.
  • A pipeline of future growth. The buyer gets not just today's cash but a machine that produces more assets — they pay for the development capability, not only the steel in the ground.
  • An operating team and track record. Proven origination, construction and O&M capability is worth a premium; it is hard and slow to build from scratch.
Key term · Platform (pipeline) sale

Selling a bundle — operating assets + a development pipeline + the team — as a single going concern, rather than asset by asset. Buyers pay a platform premium for scale, future growth and capability, so the whole typically fetches more than the sum of the parts.

The €3M never grew by being held — it grew by moving. Three rotations of one equity base banked roughly €4M of development premium, versus ~€1M if you'd built once and sat on it. Illustrative numbers, but the mechanism is exactly how developers scale.

A buyer pays up — here ~15% — for scale in one deal, a growth pipeline and an operating team. That premium is on top of the yield-compression premium on each asset. Bundling is how a develop-to-sell business captures value twice.

The develop-to-sell model, end to end

Now connect the whole track. A develop-to-sell developer is in the business of manufacturing de-risked assets and selling them — not holding them for the long, thin generation margin.

  • Originate. Find sites, sign offtakers, secure grid connections — create projects from nothing.
  • De-risk & build. Clear permits, finance, construct, and reach commercial operation. Every gate cleared compresses the yield a buyer will demand.
  • Contract & season. Lock a long, investment-grade PPA and build a clean operating track record — the de-risking levers from Lesson 2.
  • Exit. Run a competitive process to the buyer landscape of Lesson 3; capture the yield-compression premium of Lesson 1.
  • Rotate. Recycle the freed equity into the next projects — and, in time, sell the whole platform for a further premium.
Key term · Develop-to-sell

A business model that earns its return by creating and selling de-risked renewable assets, not by holding them for operating margin. Profit comes from the development premium plus yield compression on each exit, multiplied by capital rotation and, ultimately, a platform sale.

In the room

How you explain the whole business to an investor deciding whether to back the platform, not just one plant:

Sales script

We don't get rich holding one plant — we get rich making the same equity work again and again. We build, contract, sell to a fund at a premium, and put that money straight back into the next two projects. Do it a few times and a small equity base builds a real fleet — then the fleet itself, plus our pipeline and team, is worth more sold as one platform than as ten separate plants. That platform premium, stacked on the premium on every asset, is the prize.

Objections you will hear

If you keep selling everything, what's left to invest in?
Rebuttal

You're backing the engine, not one car. The value is in our ability to originate, de-risk and exit projects repeatedly — and ultimately in the platform itself, which sells at a premium. Holding assets for a thin generation margin would trap the capital and slow the whole machine down.

Isn't the platform premium just hype — why pay more than the assets are worth?
Rebuttal

A buyer isn't only buying today's plants; they're buying scale in a single transaction, a pipeline of future builds and a proven team. Each of those is genuinely costly and slow to assemble from scratch — that's why the premium is paid for real, scarce capability, not sentiment. It's simply the market price of skipping years of build-up.

What if you can't keep rotating — the market for assets dries up?
Rebuttal

We're not dependent on one window or one buyer — institutional demand for contracted, inflation-linked renewable cash is structural and deep, because pensions and insurers need exactly this. If a particular window is tight we can hold an asset and earn its operating yield, or sell into a competitive process at a slightly wider yield. Rotation flexes with the market rather than betting on it.

Knowledge check
What does asset rotation let a developer do?

The equity grows by moving, not by being held — that's how a small base builds a big fleet.

Why does a platform typically sell for more than the sum of its individual assets?

The platform premium is paid for capability and future growth — value on top of each asset's compression premium.

In the develop-to-sell model, where does the profit primarily come from?

Create and sell de-risked assets, recycle the capital, then sell the platform — that's the whole engine of the track.
