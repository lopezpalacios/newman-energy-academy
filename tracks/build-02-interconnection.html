Interconnection & permitting (CZ)

A plant that can't connect to the grid is a field full of expensive glass. The connection — not the panels — is almost always the thing that decides whether a project lives or dies, and how long it takes. Here is how it works, and why it dominates the timeline.

Learning objective

By the end you can explain why grid interconnection is the critical-path risk in a renewable project — distinguish the TSO from the DSO, say what connection capacity means, and place the Czech building permit (stavební povolení) and an EIA in the development sequence.

The critical path

In project planning, the critical path is the chain of tasks that determines the earliest possible finish — delay anything on it and the whole project slips. In renewables, the critical path almost always runs through the grid connection.

Why? You can buy panels in weeks and build the plant in months, but you cannot manufacture grid capacity. A connection requires the grid operator to study your project, reserve room on their network, possibly reinforce a substation or line, and issue a formal offer. That process is queued, regulated and slow — and it is mostly outside the developer's control. This is why an experienced developer applies for the connection on day one, before almost anything else.

The hard truth to internalise: a great site with no connection is worth nothing; a mediocre site with a firm connection offer is worth real money. The connection, not the sunshine, is the scarce resource.

TSO vs DSO — who you connect to

The grid has two layers, and which one you join depends on your project's size.

The TSO (Transmission System Operator) runs the high-voltage backbone — the big pylons that move bulk power across the country. In Czechia the TSO is ČEPS. Large generators connect here.

The DSO (Distribution System Operator) runs the lower-voltage networks that deliver power to towns, factories and homes. In Czechia these are regional distributors (for example ČEZ Distribuce, EG.D and PREdistribuce). Most C&I-scale solar and storage — a few hundred kW up to tens of MW — connects to a DSO.

For a 5 MW project, you are almost certainly talking to a DSO, applying to connect at medium voltage. The principle is the same at either layer: you apply, they study, they make an offer, you accept and pay.

Key term · TSO & DSO

The TSO operates the high-voltage transmission backbone (ČEPS in Czechia); the DSO operates the medium- and low-voltage distribution networks (regional distributors in Czechia). Your project's capacity decides which one issues your connection — and the regulated tariffs for using both are set by the energy regulator, the ERÚ (Energetický regulační úřad).

Connection capacity — the scarce commodity

Connection capacity is the amount of power (in MW) the local grid will physically accept from your plant at a given point. It is finite. Once a stretch of network is full, new generators must wait for reinforcement or find another point — at their own cost.

The operator answers your application with a connection offer (in Czech practice, a study and reservation of capacity), stating how much capacity is available, where, by when, and what you must pay toward any network upgrades. A signed, paid connection offer is one of the most valuable documents a project can hold, because it converts "we hope to connect" into "we are allowed to connect."

Two phrases worth knowing: a firm connection guarantees you can always export your full capacity; a non-firm (flexible) connection may be curtailed when the grid is congested, in exchange for connecting sooner or cheaper. Which you hold materially affects revenue and value.

Czech permitting, in sequence

Alongside the connection, the project needs land-use and construction consent. In Czechia the key approvals are:

Územní rozhodnutí / souhlas (zoning / land-use decision) — confirms the site may be used for this purpose under local spatial planning.

Stavební povolení (building permit) — the formal authorisation to construct, issued by the building authority (stavební úřad). Nothing is built until this is granted. This is the Czech equivalent of the "consent to build" milestone that defines ready-to-build.

EIA (Environmental Impact Assessment) — for larger or sensitive projects, an environmental study (in Czech, posuzování vlivů na životní prostředí, the "EIA process") may be required before permitting. Many smaller C&I solar projects fall below the threshold or need only a screening, but you must check the specific case — never assume.

The EU's RED III directive pushes member states to speed renewable permitting and designate "acceleration areas," so timelines are improving — but the developer still has to run the process. Treat all of the above as illustrative of the structure; confirm current thresholds and steps for any real site.

Key term · Stavební povolení (building permit)

The Czech construction permit issued by the local building authority — the legal green light to build. Until it is in hand, a project cannot reach ready-to-build, no matter how good the site or the offtake. Together with an accepted grid connection offer, it is one of the two consents that define a de-risked, bankable project.

The single longest, least-controllable item is the grid study and offer. That is why connection is the critical path: every other task can be worked in parallel, but the network operator sets a clock you cannot wind faster.

Across the region

The shape is identical everywhere — apply, study, offer, accept, pay, then permit — only the regulator and grid operator change. Czechia is our worked example; the DACH neighbours run the same playbook on different rails.

The EU lever to know: RED III tells member states to designate renewables acceleration areas — pre-screened zones where permitting is faster and renewables count as "overriding public interest." Designation was due around Feb 2026 and many states are running late — check current for any real site.

Across markets: same connect-then-permit sequence; swap ERÚ/ČEPS for BNetzA/MaStR, E-Control/APG or ElCom/Swissgrid — and check whether your site sits in a RED III acceleration area.

In the room

How you explain delay risk to a customer or investor without losing their confidence:

Sales script

The panels are the easy part. What sets our timeline is the grid connection — the distribution operator has to study the network and reserve capacity for us, and that's a regulated queue nobody can jump. So we file it first, on day one, and run the building permit in parallel. The day we hand you a signed connection offer and that permit, this stops being a plan and becomes a certainty — two documents that turn "we hope to connect" into "we're allowed to connect."

Objections you will hear

Can't you just connect it like a normal building?
Rebuttal

A generator isn't a normal connection — you're pushing power back into a shared network, so the operator is required by regulation to study capacity and protection before they'll allow it. Nobody can waive that step, and frankly you wouldn't want a developer who tried. We turn it into an advantage by filing first, on day one, so the mandatory wait runs in the background while everything else gets done.

What if there's no grid capacity at my site?
Rebuttal

That's exactly why the connection application is our first move — it tells us in month one whether the capacity is there, before a euro goes on design or permits. If it's tight, we size the plant to fit, take a flexible connection, or move to a nearby point. You learn the answer when it's still cheap to act on, not after the spending's done.

Do we need a full environmental assessment? That sounds like years.
Rebuttal

Often not — many C&I-scale solar projects sit below the EIA threshold or need only a quick screening. We check your specific site against the current rules before we promise you anything, so the answer you get is the real one. And EU permitting reform is actively shortening these timelines for renewables, not lengthening them.
