Selling a C&I deal in Italy

Italy has some of the most expensive wholesale power in Europe — its grid leans heavily on gas, so the gas price sets the electricity price almost every hour. That makes the savings case for a renewable PPA unusually strong, and Italy is now the EU's second-largest corporate-PPA market. The catch you must handle in the room is not price — it is permitting: getting a project authorised through Italy's regional maze. Here is how to pitch the upside and pre-empt the bottleneck.

Learning objective

By the end you can walk an Italian C&I prospect through a renewable PPA — naming the right regulator (ARERA), grid operator (Terna) and trading venue (GME/MGP), explaining why Italy's gas-set prices are among the highest in the EU and so make the savings case strong — and you can pre-empt the permitting risk (the regional aree idonee bottleneck) that is Italy's real development gate, then point to the PNRR/Transizione 5.0 funds that de-risk the build.

Italy at a glance

Italy is one of the most expensive places in Europe to buy electricity, and the reason is structural: its generation mix leans heavily on natural gas, so in the marginal-pricing market a gas plant sets the wholesale price almost every hour. The headline reference is the PUN (Prezzo Unico Nazionale, the single national price); in 2025 it ran roughly €100–130/MWh (the second quarter averaged about €101.65/MWh, with winter peaks higher), structurally above most of the bloc. High prices are not a problem to sell around — they are the strongest savings case in Europe. (Live figures — check current.)

The institutions are clean to name. The regulator is ARERA (Autorità di Regolazione per Energia Reti e Ambiente). The transmission system operator — the company that owns and runs the high-voltage grid — is Terna. Wholesale power trades on the day-ahead market (MGP — Mercato del Giorno Prima) run by the market operator GME (Gestore dei Mercati Energetici) within the power exchange IPEX. And a fourth body matters here that has no exact Spanish twin: GSE (Gestore dei Servizi Energetici) administers the renewable incentives and runs the auctions.

Key term · Zonal pricing & the PUN

Italy splits its market into bidding zones (North, Centre-North, Centre-South, South, Sicily, Sardinia), so the price differs by region according to local generation and grid limits. Historically consumers paid one blended national price, the PUN. From 1 January 2025 the TIDE dispatching reform moved wholesale purchases to zonal prices; the PUN now survives only as the "PUN Index GME," a consumption-weighted average reference (with a transitional equalisation), rather than the settlement price itself. (Check current — the transition is still bedding in.)

Compare this to your Czech spine: ERÚ → ARERA, ČEPS → Terna, OTE → GME. Same product, different rails — and here a fourth body, GSE, runs the incentives.

FER X, GSE & renewable energy communities

Italy's headline support scheme is the FER decree family — "FER" stands for Fonti Energetiche Rinnovabili (renewable energy sources). The current engine is the FER X decree: a Transitional FER X came into force on 28 February 2025 (a roughly €9.7bn tranche of an EU-approved ~€23bn scheme to add about 37 GW), offering 20-year two-way Contracts for Difference awarded through GSE auctions for plants above 1 MW, or direct access for smaller ones. The first competitive results, published on 1 December 2025, awarded roughly 7.7 GW of solar (clearing near €62.7/MWh) plus around 0.9–1.1 GW of wind. As the transitional decree lapses, the full FER X takes over, with GSE planning on the order of 10 GW of solar and 16 GW of wind across 2026–27 auctions. (Check current.)

Key term · Two-way CfD (Contract for Difference)

The generator sells into the market as normal; the scheme then settles the difference against an awarded strike price. When the market runs below the strike, the generator is topped up; when it runs above, the surplus is clawed back. It stabilises long-run revenue while keeping the asset market-exposed — the same logic the EU's 2024 market-design reform now pushes across the bloc, and which becomes the required form of new price support from 2027. The older FER 1 (2019) and FER 2 decrees, and the legacy Conto Energia PV feed-in regime (closed 2013), are the predecessors.

The grassroots story is the renewable energy community (CER — Comunità Energetica Rinnovabile): households, businesses and municipalities that share locally generated renewable power. GSE pays a 20-year feed-in incentive tariff on the energy shared within the community, and — for communities in smaller municipalities — a PNRR capital grant of up to 40% of eligible cost. A 16 July 2025 decree widened eligibility to municipalities of up to 50,000 inhabitants (up from 5,000). The PNRR grant envelope was reprogrammed down over 2025 and the grant window ran to 30 November 2025, while the feed-in tariff continues. (Check current — the incentive rules are actively moving.)

The PPA market — and the permitting catch

This is the good news. Italy is the EU's second-largest corporate-PPA market by volume, behind only Spain. 2025 was a breakout year: solar corporate-PPA volumes rose roughly 184% year on year, capped by Italy's largest-ever solar corporate PPA — around 420 MW in June 2025. Because Italy is at an earlier stage of market maturity than Spain, it suffers less midday cannibalisation, so big deals are still easy to strike at prices that work. High grid prices plus low capture-price drag is an unusually clean demand picture. (Spain ~3.9 GW vs Italy ~1.8 GW in 2025 — orders of magnitude, check current.)

But there is a catch you must never hide, and in Italy it is not price — it is getting the project built. Authorisation is largely a regional matter, and the rules have been in legal flux.

Key term · "Aree idonee" (suitable areas)

The aree idonee ("suitable areas") framework was meant to fast-track renewables by pre-designating where they may be built. A June 2024 decree handed regions the power to set their own rules — but it delegated too much: in May 2025 the Lazio administrative court (TAR) partially annulled it for unconstitutional over-delegation, and the provisions later migrated into Decree 175 of 21 November 2025. The practical result is regional authorisation delays and legal uncertainty — the single biggest development risk in the Italian market. (Check current — the legal picture is still settling.)

For a salesperson this is not a reason to walk away — it is a reason to de-risk the pitch up front. You qualify the permitting and grid status of a specific site before you promise a build, and you offer a virtual or financial PPA that hedges the customer's price with no new physical authorisation at all where the local consenting route is slow.

The grid, permitting & storage

Connecting to the grid runs through Terna, and the connection point is the practical gate on any physical project — as everywhere, qualify it first. In Italy the connection conversation is inseparable from the permitting one above: the regional aree idonee route, the environmental authorisation and the grid connection together set the timeline, and the regional layer is where projects stall. Lead with secured-capacity sites and a realistic consenting plan, not a wish.

Across the region

Italy's answer to its renewables build-out is increasingly storage, procured through Terna's MACSE (Meccanismo di Approvvigionamento di Capacità di Stoccaggio Elettrico) — a storage-capacity auction. The first MACSE (results 1 October 2025) procured 10 GWh under 15-year contracts starting 2028, was about four times oversubscribed, and cleared at roughly €12,959/MWh/year — far below the ~€37,000 reserve — for around €1bn of investment, concentrated in the South and the islands. Italy also runs a capacity market through Terna. (Check current.)

The strategic read: Italy is pricing storage cheaply precisely to absorb new renewables and cut its gas dependence. For a developer that means a storage or solar-plus-storage structure has a real, contracted revenue line behind it, which strengthens both the customer pitch and the eventual exit.

Carbon: EU ETS & CBAM

Italy is an EU member, so the full carbon frame applies. Industrial CO₂ carries a price under the EU ETS (Emissions Trading System), and from 2026 the CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism) puts a carbon cost on imports of carbon-intensive goods — so EU industry needs clean power to stay competitive against that border charge.

Unlike Germany, Italy has no separate national carbon price on the nEHS model — it relies on the EU ETS. The pitch logic still holds: a renewable PPA cuts the customer's Scope 2 emissions (the emissions from their purchased electricity, a board-level reporting liability under CSRD) and delivers Guarantees of Origin to evidence it. With CBAM raising the cost of a carbon-heavy supply chain, demonstrably clean power becomes a competitiveness asset for Italy's large export-heavy manufacturing base, not just a compliance line.

The funding stack

Here is the most common mistake people make about Italy: assuming it taps the Modernisation Fund. It does not. The Modernisation Fund is reserved for the EU's 13 lower-income member states (Czechia, Poland and peers). Italy funds its transition from a different — and enormous — pot:

  • PNRR / Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) — Italy is by far the largest beneficiary of NextGenerationEU in absolute terms. Its national recovery plan (PNRR — Piano Nazionale di Ripresa e Resilienza) is worth around €194bn after its 2023 revision (including a dedicated REPowerEU energy chapter), coordinated nationally by the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), with the deadline to complete milestones on 31 August 2026.
  • GSE — the body that administers most of the energy measures: the FER X CfD auctions, the CER feed-in incentive and the PNRR-funded community grants. It is the agency an Italian renewable project actually interacts with.
  • Transizione 5.0 — a tax-credit scheme rewarding energy efficiency plus self-generation and self-consumption from renewables (PV and storage). Note the moving parts: its ceiling (~€2.5bn) closed early in 2025, and from 1 January 2026 it converts from a tax credit into "hyper-amortisation" (a maximum of ~180%, with a "made-in-Europe" clause). Treat the exact terms as live.
  • State-aid ceiling — because Italy is in the EU, every grant must fit inside the CEEAG (Climate, Energy & Environmental Aid Guidelines, 2022) and GBER (General Block Exemption Regulation) caps. Aid is sized to a viability gap; you cannot stack pots to 100%.
At EU level

Because Italy is in the EU, the full EU frame applies: RED III's binding renewables target, EU ETS carbon pricing, CBAM, and the 2024 Electricity Market Design reform that actively promotes PPAs and two-way CfDs. Lead with that shared frame, then localise to FER X auctions + GSE + the PNRR/Transizione 5.0 stack — not the Modernisation Fund. (Programme details and call windows — check current.)

In the room

How you open with an Italian industrial prospect — leading with the price gap, then pre-empting the permitting reality before they raise it:

Sales script

You're buying some of the most expensive power in Europe — because the Italian grid runs on gas, the gas price sets your bill almost every hour. A ten-to-twenty-year PPA locks a big slice of your load in euros per megawatt-hour, comfortably below your grid tariff, with Guarantees of Origin for your CSRD reporting and a hedge against CBAM; Italy is now the second-biggest corporate-PPA market in Europe, so this is well-trodden ground. Now the honest part: the hard bit here isn't price, it's permitting — clearing a plant through the regional aree idonee rules, still in legal flux — so we qualify the permits and grid connection first and promise only what's actually consentable, and where the local route is slow a virtual PPA hedges your price with no authorisation at all. Highest prices in Europe, met with the certainty of a fixed one — with the permitting risk on the table from minute one.

Objections you will hear

Permitting in Italy is a nightmare — the rules keep changing. How do I know this gets built?
Rebuttal

You've named the real risk, and it's exactly why we put it first: authorisation is regional and the aree idonee framework has been through the courts, so we never promise a build on an unconsented site. We qualify the permitting status and the Terna connection point before we sign anything, commit capex only to projects that are genuinely consentable, and where the local route is slow a virtual or financial PPA hedges your price with no physical authorisation at all. We size the deal to what the system can actually deliver, not to a wish.

Italian power prices are high now, but they're volatile — what if they fall and I'm locked into a PPA above the market?
Rebuttal

That's the right question, and it's precisely what a PPA manages. Italy's prices are high because the grid is gas-set, and gas volatility cuts both ways — exactly the risk you want out of your P&L — so we price the PPA off renewable LCOE, well below today's grid tariff, and you start saving from day one rather than betting on spot. You're not buying a wager that prices stay high; you're buying certainty below today's bill, with Guarantees of Origin attached and the strike set conservatively so the saving is durable, not a one-year headline.

The FER incentives and the 5.0 credit keep getting cut or closed early. Is anything still actually open?
Rebuttal

Fair — the schemes do move, so we always work off the live call, not last year's brochure, and crucially your savings case doesn't depend on a grant: the high grid price already makes the PPA work, and any incentive is upside, not the foundation. The core engine is open and large, though — FER X is running 20-year CfD auctions through GSE with 10 GW of solar and 16 GW of wind planned across 2026–27, renewable energy communities still get a 20-year GSE feed-in tariff, and Transizione 5.0 is converting to hyper-amortisation from 2026, so the form changes but the self-generation incentive continues. (Check current.)

Knowledge check
Who runs Italy's day-ahead wholesale electricity market, and what is distinctive about Italian pricing?

GME runs the MGP within IPEX; Italy is split into bidding zones and moved purchases to zonal pricing in 2025. ARERA regulates and Terna runs the grid — don't mix the roles in front of a client.

Italy's wholesale prices are among the highest in the EU. What is the main commercial opportunity that creates, and the main risk to flag?

Gas sets the Italian price most hours, so it's high — a strong savings case. The binding constraint is permitting: authorisation is regional and the aree-idonee framework has been in legal flux. Price is the upside; permitting is the risk.

Which EU fund does Italy rely on for its energy transition?

The Modernisation Fund is for the 13 lower-income states (Czechia, Poland…). Italy is the single largest RRF beneficiary, deploying it through the PNRR with GSE running the energy incentives (FER X, CER) and Transizione 5.0 alongside. All grants sit under the CEEAG/GBER state-aid caps.
