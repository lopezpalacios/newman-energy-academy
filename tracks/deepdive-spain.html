Selling a C&I deal in Spain

Spain has the cheapest solar electrons in Western Europe and signs more corporate PPAs than any other country in the EU. It is the sunniest large market on the continent — and that very abundance creates the one problem you must handle in the room: so much solar floods the grid at midday that prices collapse, sometimes below zero. Here is how to pitch the best solar resource in Europe while managing the price the asset actually captures.

Learning objective

By the end you can walk a Spanish C&I prospect through a renewable PPA — naming the right regulator (CNMC), grid operator (REE) and trading venue (OMIE/MIBEL), explaining why Europe's best solar resource gives the lowest LCOE — and you can manage the capture-price expectation that comes with heavy midday solar, then point to the RRF/PERTE funds that de-risk the build.

Spain at a glance

Spain is the sunniest large economy in Europe, and it has built solar faster than almost anyone — solar PV is now the country's largest single source of installed capacity. The number that drives every deal here is the solar yield: roughly 1,500–1,900 kWh per kWp per year (tracker plants can top 2,000), against ~950–1,100 in Czechia or Germany. More sun per panel means more energy per euro of capex, which means utility-scale solar LCOE around €30–50/MWh — among the lowest in the EU. (Live figures — check current.)

The institutions are straightforward. The regulator is the CNMC (Comisión Nacional de los Mercados y la Competencia, the national markets & competition commission), which also runs the Guarantee-of-Origin registry. The transmission system operator — the company that owns and runs the high-voltage grid — is Red Eléctrica de España (REE), part of the Redeia group. Wholesale power trades on OMIE, the Iberian market operator, within MIBEL (Mercado Ibérico de Electricidad) — a single day-ahead and intraday market shared with Portugal under a Spain–Portugal treaty. Forward contracts trade on OMIP (in Lisbon).

Renewable auctions & the autoconsumo boom

Spain's headline support scheme is the renewable auction (subasta) system. Under Royal Decree 960/2020 it created the REER (Régimen Económico de Energías Renovables, the Economic Regime for Renewable Energy). Projects bid in a closed-envelope, pay-as-bid auction; winners receive a sliding market premium — a contract-for-difference-style top-up referenced to the market — for 10 to 15 years, depending on technology.

Key term · Sliding market premium (CfD-style)

Not a fixed feed-in tariff. The generator sells into the OMIE market as normal; the scheme then adjusts revenue toward the awarded price. When the market runs below the strike, the generator is topped up; when it runs above, the surplus is clawed back. It stabilises long-run revenue while keeping the asset exposed to the market — the same logic as the EU's 2024 market-design reform, which now pushes two-way CfDs across the bloc.

The bigger grassroots story is self-consumption (autoconsumo). Spain notoriously taxed rooftop solar with the "impuesto al sol" (the "sun tax") — until it was scrapped in 2018. The following year, Royal Decree 244/2019 set a clean framework: two tiers (with or without surplus injection) plus compensación simplificada, a net-billing-style monthly credit for the surplus a small system exports. The result was explosive: from near-zero in 2018 to over 500,000 installations and more than 7 GW of self-consumption capacity by the end of 2024. (Check current.)

The PPA market — and the capture-price catch

This is where Spain leads Europe outright. It is the EU's largest corporate-PPA market by volume — roughly 3.9 GW of corporate PPAs contracted in 2025, ahead of Italy (~1.8 GW) and Poland (~1.5 GW), and it has signed more than 2 GW a year since 2023. The cheap, abundant solar resource makes long-term clean supply genuinely attractive to industry, so the demand is real and deep. (Live figures — check current.)

But there is a catch you must never hide from a sophisticated buyer, and it is the exact opposite of the Polish story. Spain has so much solar that the grid is flooded at midday — precisely when every solar plant is generating at once. That midday glut drives the wholesale price down, sometimes to zero or below zero. So the price a solar plant actually captures is well under the average market price.

Key term · Cannibalisation & the capture price

Capture price is the average price a generator actually earns for its output, weighted by when it produces — not the simple market average. Cannibalisation is what drags it down: because all solar generates in the same midday hours, solar floods the market and depresses the price in exactly those hours. In Spain, 2025 solar capture rates fell to roughly 52–61% of the average price, the volume-weighted capture price dropped about 12% year on year to under €40/MWh, and negative-price hours roughly doubled versus 2024. High yield is wonderful; midday gluts compress what the asset is paid. (Check current.)

For a salesperson this is not a reason to walk away — it is a reason to structure the deal honestly. The cheapest electrons in Europe still beat a Spanish industrial tariff comfortably, so a PPA delivers a real saving plus Guarantees of Origin for Scope 2 reporting. But you sell a price the customer can trust, manage expectations on shape, and let storage and baseload-style or shaped PPAs absorb the midday risk rather than pretending it away.

The grid & the April 2025 blackout

Connecting to the grid runs through REE, and grid capacity is the practical gate on any physical project — as everywhere, qualify the connection point first. But in Spain there is a second, unavoidable conversation: on 28 April 2025 the entire Iberian Peninsula — Spain and Portugal — suffered a peninsula-wide blackout lasting around ten hours. A prospect will raise it, so know the facts.

Across the region

ENTSO-E's final report attributed the blackout to a combination of factors — gaps in voltage and reactive-power control, oscillations, rapid output reductions and a cascade of generator disconnections — rather than simply "too much renewable energy." The honest framing: it was a grid-control and stability event, and the policy response has been to strengthen exactly that. Check current — the official analysis is still settling.

The aftermath pivoted Spanish energy policy hard toward grid strength, storage and flexibility. An emergency decree (RDL 7/2025) on storage, voltage control and demand response was passed in June 2025 but then repealed by parliament in July 2025 — so the legislative path is live. More durably, Operational Procedure 7.4 was updated so that renewables themselves provide voltage control (full implementation in March 2026), and the Electricity Planning 2025–30 proposal earmarks on the order of €13.6bn of transmission investment. Grid-scale batteries are still small today but are a national priority. (Check current.)

Carbon: EU ETS & CBAM

Spain is an EU member, so the full carbon frame applies. Industrial CO₂ carries a price under the EU ETS (Emissions Trading System), and from 2026 the CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism) puts a carbon cost on imports of carbon-intensive goods — so EU industry needs clean power to stay competitive against that border charge.

Unlike Germany, Spain has no separate national carbon price on the nEHS model — it relies on the EU ETS. The pitch logic still holds: a renewable PPA cuts the customer's Scope 2 emissions (the emissions from their purchased electricity, a board-level reporting liability under CSRD) and delivers Guarantees of Origin to evidence it. With CBAM raising the cost of a carbon-heavy supply chain, demonstrably clean power becomes a competitiveness asset, not just a compliance line.

The funding stack

Here is the most common mistake people make about Spain: assuming it taps the Modernisation Fund. It does not. The Modernisation Fund is reserved for the EU's 13 lower-income member states (Czechia, Poland and peers). Spain funds its transition from a different — and very large — pot:

  • Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) — Spain is one of the largest beneficiaries in the EU, with roughly €160bn+ over 2021–26 in grants and loans, delivered through its national recovery plan (PRTR — Plan de Recuperación, Transformación y Resiliencia), which concludes around August 2026.
  • PERTE de Energías Renovables — the PERTE ERHA (Proyecto Estratégico para la Recuperación y Transformación Económica — Energías Renovables, Hidrógeno Renovable y Almacenamiento), the strategic project channelling RRF money into renewables, renewable hydrogen and storage.
  • IDAE — the Instituto para la Diversificación y Ahorro de la Energía administers most of the renewables, storage and value-chain calls (e.g. lines for storage, repowering and manufacturing). It is the agency a Spanish project actually applies to.
  • State-aid ceiling — because Spain is in the EU, every grant must fit inside the CEEAG (Climate, Energy & Environmental Aid Guidelines, 2022) and GBER (General Block Exemption Regulation) caps. Aid is sized to a viability gap; you cannot stack pots to 100%.
At EU level

Because Spain is in the EU, the full EU frame applies: RED III's binding renewables target, EU ETS carbon pricing, CBAM, and the 2024 Electricity Market Design reform that actively promotes PPAs and two-way CfDs. Lead with that shared frame, then localise to REER auctions + CNMC + the RRF/PERTE/IDAE stack — not the Modernisation Fund. (Programme details and call windows — check current.)

In the room

How you open with a Spanish industrial prospect — leading with the resource, then handling the capture-price reality before they raise it:

Sales script

You're sitting on the best solar resource in Western Europe — your panels make roughly twice the energy of a German plant, which is why solar here is the cheapest power on the continent to build. A ten-to-fifteen-year PPA locks a big slice of your load in euros per megawatt-hour, well below your grid tariff, with Guarantees of Origin for your CSRD reporting and a hedge against CBAM as your supply chain gets carbon-priced. Now the honest part: there's so much solar here that midday prices sometimes hit zero, so I price your PPA on the shaped capture profile, not the dream number, and bring storage or a baseload structure to manage it. The saving is real and durable, not a midday mirage — the strongest resource in Europe, sold straight.

Objections you will hear

Prices go to zero — even negative — at midday. Why would I sign a solar PPA?
Rebuttal

You've spotted the real issue, and it's exactly why a PPA helps rather than hurts: a negative midday spot punishes a merchant generator chasing the price, but your PPA fixes a blended price per megawatt-hour for years, insulating you from that volatility entirely. We price the contract on the shaped capture profile, not the average market number, so the figure you sign is one we can stand behind — and storage or a baseload structure shifts value out of the saturated midday hours. The cannibalisation is the very thing your fixed price protects you from.

The grid is saturated and connections take forever — how do I know this gets built?
Rebuttal

Connection is the gate everywhere, and Spain is no exception — so we qualify the REE connection point first and build only on secured capacity, while a virtual or financial PPA can hedge your price with no new physical connection at all. The policy wind is behind us too: the Electricity Planning 2025–30 puts roughly €13bn into transmission, and storage is now a national priority precisely to absorb the midday solar. We size the structure to what the grid can actually deliver, not to a wish.

After the April 2025 blackout, is solar even reliable? It took the country down.
Rebuttal

It's a fair question — and the key point for you: your PPA is a price contract, it doesn't expose you to grid operation at all, and on-site generation plus storage makes your site more resilient, not less. ENTSO-E's final report pinned the blackout on grid voltage and reactive-power control failures and a cascade of disconnections — a system-stability event, not too much solar as such — and the response has been to make renewables part of the fix, providing voltage control under the updated Operational Procedure 7.4 alongside heavy investment in grid strength and storage. (Check current — the analysis is still settling.)

Knowledge check
Which venue runs Spain's day-ahead wholesale electricity market, and what is special about it?

OMIE runs the spot market within MIBEL, shared with Portugal; OMIP handles futures. CNMC regulates and REE runs the grid — don't mix the roles in front of a client.

Spain has Europe's best solar resource. What is the key commercial downside a buyer should understand?

All that midday solar pushes prices down exactly when solar generates, so the capture price falls well below the average — the reason you price a PPA on the shaped profile, not the headline number.

Which EU fund does Spain rely on for its energy transition?

The Modernisation Fund is for the 13 lower-income states (Czechia, Poland…). Spain is a major RRF recipient, deploying it via the PRTR and PERTE ERHA, with IDAE administering the calls. All grants sit under the CEEAG/GBER state-aid caps.
