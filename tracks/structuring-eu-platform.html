Structuring a European platform to access EU funding

You already own 100 MW of operating solar in Mexico, backed by international funds. Now you want into Europe — to tap the Modernisation Fund, national grant schemes and EU institutional capital. The catch: your Mexican company cannot receive a single euro of EU grant directly. This lesson shows the compliant platform you build so it can — and stresses, throughout, that the structure only works if the substance is real.

Learning objective

By the end you can outline a compliant European platform structure that can receive EU grants and institutional capital, and explain why a foreign (e.g. Mexican) entity cannot be the grant beneficiary itself — naming the layers, the holding-jurisdiction trade-offs, and the real substance rules that make the structure stand up.

Why you need an EU entity at all

Start with the rule that decides everything else: EU and national grants flow to a beneficiary that is established in the European Union — usually in the specific member state where the project sits. A grant is a transfer of public money under EU and national law; the recipient has to be a legal person the granting authority can contract with, audit, claw back from and hold to the rules. A company incorporated in Mexico is none of those things to a Czech or Polish authority.

The Modernisation Fund makes this concrete. It is an EU fund (financed by selling EU ETS emission allowances) for a group of lower-income member states, including Czechia and Poland (check the current beneficiary list). But money does not flow EU-to-developer: each beneficiary member state submits investment proposals, and the support is paid out through a national operator under national programmes — SFŽP ČR in Czechia (e.g. the RES+ programme for new solar), NFOŚiGW in Poland. You apply to them, against a project located in their country, run by a legal entity registered there. (Check the current call text — programmes and eligibility change.)

The same logic governs centrally-managed EU calls (Innovation Fund, Horizon Europe, LIFE) on the EU Funding & Tenders Portal: applicants must be legal entities established in an eligible country. There is no door marked "foreign parent."

Key term · The beneficiary must be EU-established

Your Mexican HoldCo can own the European platform — equity ownership is not the issue. What it cannot be is the grant beneficiary, the PPA signatory or the permit holder. Those roles belong to a locally established company. So the question is never "how do I get the grant in Mexico" — it is "what EU entity do I create to be eligible, and how do I own it cleanly?"

The platform architecture

The market answer is a layered structure. It is not a trick; it is how essentially every cross-border renewables developer and infrastructure fund is built, for reasons of liability, financing and clean exits. Three layers do the work.

At the bottom sits the SPV (special-purpose vehicle), one per project — also called the ProjectCo. It is incorporated in the country where the asset is. It owns the plant, holds the grid connection and permits, signs the PPA, and is the entity that applies for and receives the grant. Ring-fencing each project in its own SPV means one project's problems (a construction dispute, a lender's security) do not contaminate the others.

Above the SPVs sits a national / intermediate HoldCo — typically in the same country (or region) as the projects — that owns the local SPVs and consolidates them. Above that sits the group (platform) HoldCo, the top of the European stack, where international capital comes in. Your Mexican parent owns the platform HoldCo, not the SPVs directly.

You set up a Czech SPV per solar park (the RES+ / Modernisation Fund beneficiary) and a Polish SPV per park (the NFOŚiGW / OZE-auction beneficiary), each under a national HoldCo, all under one European platform HoldCo, which your Mexican group owns. The grant lands in the SPV; the capital comes in at the platform; the asset, contract and permit all live in the right country.

Choosing the HoldCo location

Where do you put the platform HoldCo? You can hold local SPVs from anywhere, but developers and funds gravitate to a short list of EU holding jurisdictions — most commonly the Netherlands, Luxembourg and Ireland. The reasons are legitimate and well-worn:

  • Holding regimes. A participation exemption (the Netherlands and Luxembourg) means qualifying dividends and capital gains from subsidiaries are not taxed again at the holding level — so profits and a future SPV sale are not taxed twice on the way up. Ireland has moved to a participation exemption for foreign dividends too (check current — phased in recently).
  • Treaty networks. Wide double-tax-treaty coverage plus the EU Parent-Subsidiary and Interest & Royalties Directives reduce withholding-tax friction when cash moves between the project country and the HoldCo. Luxembourg has 80+ treaties; the Netherlands' network is among the world's deepest.
  • Fund familiarity. EU pension and infrastructure funds, and their lawyers and lenders, know these jurisdictions cold — the fund vehicles, governance and reporting are standard. That makes raising platform capital and a future exit easier, which is often the real reason, not the tax line.
Critical caveat · Substance, not a letterbox

A holding jurisdiction only works if the entity has real substance there: genuine management, local directors who actually decide, an office, and board decisions taken in-country — not a brass plate and a mailing address. This is enforced. The Anti-Tax-Avoidance Directive (ATAD / ATAD2) imposes EU-wide anti-abuse, CFC and anti-hybrid rules; tax treaties carry a Principal Purpose Test (via the OECD BEPS Multilateral Instrument) that denies benefits to arrangements whose main purpose is the tax benefit; and beneficial-ownership (UBO) registers under the EU anti-money-laundering regime record who really owns the chain. National tax authorities (the Dutch, for instance, have tightened substance scrutiny markedly) will challenge a structure with no economic reality. Letterbox / shell structures are exactly what the rules target — do not build one.

Check current — Unshell / ATAD3: the EU's proposed "Unshell" Directive (ATAD3), which would have set explicit EU-wide minimum-substance tests for holding companies, was withdrawn by the European Commission (flagged in its 2026 work programme) after years of deadlock; substance-style principles are instead being folded into a reform of the DAC administrative-cooperation rules. The direction of travel is unchanged: substance is mandatory and scrutiny is rising. Treat the precise legal instrument as live and verify the current status with counsel.

How the grant actually flows

Trace one euro. A Czech solar SPV — owned up the chain by your platform — applies to SFŽP ČR under a Modernisation Fund / RES+ call. If it wins, the grant is paid to the SPV, the EU-established beneficiary that owns the asset and signed the PPA. The platform and your Mexican parent never touch the grant; they sit above it as owners.

Two rules shape how much the SPV can take, both set at EU level as the state-aid frame national schemes must fit inside:

  • CEEAG (2022) — the Climate, Energy and Environmental Aid Guidelines — and the GBER (General Block Exemption Regulation) cap how much public support a single project may legally absorb. Aid is sized to close a viability gap, not to make a project "too profitable." In state-aid language the beneficiary is an "undertaking" — any entity carrying on an economic activity, regardless of who owns it.
  • Co-financing. A grant typically funds only part of eligible capex (often ~30–65%, scheme-dependent — check the call). The SPV must bring the rest: equity from the platform plus project-finance debt. The grant improves the project's returns and de-risks the build; it does not replace the capital stack.

Note the foreign-ownership point clearly: CEEAG/GBER eligibility turns on the project and the undertaking, not the nationality of the ultimate parent. A Czech SPV that is wholly owned, up the chain, by a Mexican group is still a Czech undertaking carrying on a Czech activity — eligible — provided it meets the call's substance and "incentive effect" tests (e.g. you must not have started works before applying).

Bringing your funds & the Mexico track record

Here your existing portfolio becomes an asset in a second sense. 100 MW of operating solar in Mexico, financed by international funds, is a credential. It tells EU lenders, grant officers and co-investors that you can permit, build, finance and operate at scale — the single biggest question a counterparty has about a newcomer. A first-timer with a slide deck and a developer with a live, fund-backed operating fleet are not assessed the same way.

Where does capital actually go? Two typical entry points:

  • Platform-level equity. Infrastructure and institutional funds increasingly back a platform — they take equity in the group HoldCo to fund a whole pipeline across several SPVs, and platform/HoldCo financing is now standard for renewables portfolios. This is where your existing international funds and new EU funds typically sit.
  • SPV-level co-investment & project finance. Project-finance lenders lend into the SPV against its contracted cash flow (the PPA), secured on the asset; some funds also co-invest directly at SPV level on a specific project. Lender due diligence will trace ownership up to your Mexican parent — so a clean, substance-backed chain is what gets the deal financed.

The track record also feeds the grant case: programmes weigh an applicant's readiness and capacity to deliver, and a demonstrated 100 MW operating history is direct evidence of both.

Structuring for the exit

Build the structure on day one for the day you sell. EU infrastructure-fund buyers almost always acquire an operating renewable asset by buying the shares of the local SPV (a share deal), not the plant and contracts one by one (an asset deal). A share sale transfers the permits, the grid connection, the PPA and — critically — keeps the asset's history intact, which is cleaner and usually more tax-efficient for both sides.

That is exactly why the one-SPV-per-project architecture pays off: each project is a tidy, ring-fenced parcel of shares the platform can sell on its own, into a competitive process, without disturbing the rest of the fleet. The participation exemption at the HoldCo can mean the gain on that share sale is not taxed again at the holding level (check current; fact-specific) — so the structure that made you grant-eligible is the same structure that makes the exit clean.

This ties straight back to the exit track: you build the SPV at a developer's hurdle rate, de-risk it through construction and a signed PPA, then sell the shares to a fund that accepts a lower yield. The platform is the machine that lets you do that, project after project. (See NPV, DCF & the exit valuation.)

This is not advice

This lesson is educational. It describes standard, legitimate market practice — it is not tax, legal or investment advice, and nothing here is a recommendation to adopt any particular structure. The right structure depends entirely on your specifics: the countries you build in, your investors, your existing group, and the live state of the law. Engage qualified tax and legal counsel in each relevant jurisdiction before you incorporate anything. And the non-negotiable: substance is mandatory. A holding structure without genuine management, decision-making and presence where it claims to be is not a tax plan — it is a compliance and reputational liability that EU authorities and lenders are built to find. Many figures, programmes and rules here change — verify the current position before relying on it.

In the room

How you describe your structure to an EU infrastructure fund or local partner — leading with the track record, not the tax line:

Sales script

We already own and operate a hundred megawatts of solar in Mexico, financed alongside international funds — so permitting, building and running assets at scale is a track record you can check, not a promise. For Europe we use the same structure every serious cross-border developer uses: a project company in-country for each park — the entity that holds the permits, signs the PPA and is the eligible Modernisation Fund beneficiary — under a national holding company, under one European platform you'd invest into. The holding sits where you'd expect, with real directors and real substance on the ground, because counsel in each country has to stand behind it and so do we. Grants land in the project company, your capital comes in at the platform, and every park is a clean, ring-fenced parcel of shares you can exit on its own. The structure that makes us grant-eligible is the very same one that makes your exit clean.

Objections you will hear

Why not just receive the grant in Mexico and fund the project from there?
Rebuttal

There's no loophole to find here, and not finding one is what protects you. An EU grant is public money paid only to a beneficiary the authority can contract with, audit and claw back from under EU and national law — the Modernisation Fund pays out through national operators like SFŽP ČR or NFOŚiGW, to a project in that country, run by a locally registered entity. A Mexican company simply cannot be that beneficiary. So you stand up an EU project company to receive it cleanly, and your Mexican group keeps full ownership of the platform above it.

Isn't a Dutch or Luxembourg HoldCo just tax dodging?
Rebuttal

Not when it's built with substance — and substance is exactly what we're committing to. These are mainstream holding jurisdictions because participation exemptions stop double taxation and EU funds and lenders know them cold, which is what makes your capital and your exit easier. The rules that strike down empty shells — ATAD anti-abuse, the treaty Principal Purpose Test, beneficial-ownership registers — are on your side here: they're why we build a real holding company with real directors and real decisions, and why we put counsel's name behind it, not a brass plate.

We have no EU presence yet — won't that sink the grant application and the raise?
Rebuttal

No EU track record isn't the same as no capability — and capability is what de-risks the application. We have 100 MW operating in Mexico, financed with international funds: verifiable proof we can permit, build, finance and run, which is precisely what grant officers and lenders assess. The EU presence is what the platform creates — a locally established project company that is the eligible beneficiary, with genuine substance at the holding level. We're not pretending to be European overnight; we're bringing a proven operating record to a real European platform.

Knowledge check
Your Mexican group wants Modernisation Fund money for a Czech solar park. Who is the grant beneficiary?

The beneficiary must be established in the member state where the project sits. The grant flows to the local SPV; the Mexican parent and platform sit above it as owners and cannot receive the grant directly.

What is the single most important requirement that keeps an EU HoldCo structure legitimate?

ATAD anti-abuse rules, the treaty Principal Purpose Test and UBO registers all target empty shells. A holding company must have economic reality where it claims to be, or it gets challenged. (And ownership chains are disclosed in beneficial-ownership registers, not hidden.)

Why does the one-SPV-per-project architecture help at exit?

Buyers acquire operating assets through a share deal on the SPV — transferring permits, grid connection and PPA intact. One SPV per project makes each one a tidy, independently saleable package. (A participation exemption may shelter the gain at HoldCo level — check current, fact-specific.)
