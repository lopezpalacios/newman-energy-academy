Learning objective

By the end you can describe the three ways a business deploys solar, quote the four numbers that decide if a project is worth doing, and use the LCOE to show a factory owner that their own roof beats the grid.

Three ways a business deploys solar

Commercial & industrial — C&I — solar means panels sized for a single business, not a household and not a national grid. There are three shapes it takes, and you should be able to tell them apart in a sentence.

Rooftop solar sits on the roof of the customer's own building — a warehouse, a cold store, a factory. The power is used on-site, so it directly replaces grid electricity at the full retail tariff. Cheapest to connect, smallest in scale, biggest saving per unit.

Ground-mount solar is a field of panels on open land, usually larger (several MWp) and exporting to the grid or feeding a nearby site. More power, lower cost per kWp, but it needs land, a grid connection and planning consent.

Behind-the-meter (BTM) is not a third location so much as a wiring choice: the panels connect inside the customer's electricity meter, so the energy never touches the public grid and never pays network charges. Most C&I rooftop is behind-the-meter — that is exactly why it saves so much.

Key term · Behind-the-meter (BTM)

Generation connected on the customer's side of the utility meter. Because the electrons are consumed before reaching the public grid, they avoid network charges and regulated levies — the part of a bill that often costs as much as the energy itself. This is the single biggest reason rooftop solar undercuts the grid.

The four numbers that decide a project

You do not need an engineering degree. You need four figures, and you can carry them in your head.

Capex (capital expenditure) is the all-in build cost, quoted in €/kWp — euros per kilowatt-peak of panel capacity. kWp (kilowatt-peak) is the rated output of the array under standard test sun. For C&I solar in Czechia and the EU, budget roughly €500–750/kWp; rooftop sits a little higher than ground-mount because of mounting and access.

Specific yield is how much energy each kWp actually produces in a year, in kWh/kWp/yr. It depends on latitude and weather. Czechia delivers about 950–1,100 kWh/kWp/yr — meaningfully less than Spain's 1,500+, which is why Czech projects live or die on capex and tariff, not sunshine.

Opex (operating expenditure) — cleaning, inverter servicing, insurance, land lease — runs maybe €10–15/kWp/yr. Small, but it never stops.

And the grid tariff to beat: the all-in industrial price the customer pays today, order of magnitude €100–180/MWh in Czechia depending on voltage level and network charges. If your solar produces below that, you have a sale.

Key term · LCOE (Levelised Cost of Energy)

The LCOE is the all-in lifetime cost of one unit of generated electricity — total capex plus all opex, spread across all the energy the plant ever makes, adjusted for the time value of money. It turns a pile of build costs into one clean number in €/MWh that you can put side-by-side with the grid tariff. Czech C&I solar typically lands around €50–70/MWh — well below the grid.

Degradation & lifetime

Solar panels do not die at a fixed age; they slowly fade. Degradation is the gradual loss of output, typically about 0.5% per year. After 25 years a panel still produces roughly 87–88% of its first-year output — which is why manufacturers warrant performance for that long.

Asset life for planning is usually taken as 25–30 years. The panels often outlast that; the inverter (the box that converts panel DC into usable AC) is the part you budget to replace once, around year 12–15. None of this breaks the economics — it just means you model a slightly declining production line, not a flat one.

In the room

How you say this to a plant owner who has never thought about their roof as an asset:

Sales script

You own a hundred thousand square feet of roof that does nothing but keep the rain off. Put panels on it and it makes electricity at about sixty euros a megawatt-hour, for twenty-five years. You are paying the grid around a hundred and forty for the same unit. We are not asking you to gamble on the energy market — we are offering to halve the cost of the power you already buy, using space you already own.

Objections you will hear

Czech weather is terrible — does solar even work here?
Rebuttal

It works on light, not heat, and even cloudy Czechia delivers around a thousand kilowatt-hours per kilowatt-peak every year. The project doesn't depend on Spanish sunshine — it depends on your grid tariff, and yours is high enough that solar at sixty euros a megawatt-hour still beats it comfortably. Germany, with the same climate, is one of the largest solar markets on earth.

The panels degrade — in fifteen years they'll be useless.
Rebuttal

They fade by about half a percent a year, so after 25 years you still get roughly 87% of day-one output — which is why the performance is warranted for that long. The LCOE we quote already bakes that decline in. The one part we plan to replace once is the inverter, around year twelve, and that's a known, budgeted cost, not a surprise.

My roof isn't big enough to cover my whole bill.
Rebuttal

It doesn't have to. Solar covers your daytime base load — the part you run when the sun is up — which for most factories is the biggest, steadiest slice. You keep buying the rest from the grid. We size the array to what your roof and daytime demand can actually use, so every panel you install is one you self-consume at full saving.

Knowledge check
Why does behind-the-meter rooftop solar save more per unit than a grid-export project?

Behind-the-meter energy never touches the public grid, so it dodges the network charges that make up a big slice of the bill.

What does "specific yield" measure, and roughly what is it in Czechia?

Specific yield is kWh per kWp per year. Czechia sits around 1,000 — lower than Iberia, which is why capex and tariff drive the deal.

What makes the LCOE a useful number to show a customer?

LCOE turns capex plus opex over the asset's life into a single cost per unit — set it next to the grid tariff and the saving is obvious.
